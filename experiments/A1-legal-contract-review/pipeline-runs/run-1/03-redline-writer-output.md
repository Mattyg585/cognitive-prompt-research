### Section 1.6 — Machine Learning Rights over Customer Content

**Current language**: "Usage Data and Customer Content may be used to develop, train, or enhance artificial intelligence or machine learning models that are part of Provider's products and services, including third-party components of the Product, and Customer authorizes Provider to process its Usage Data and Customer Content for such purposes. However, (a) Usage Data and Customer Content must be aggregated before it can be used for these purposes, and (b) Provider will use commercially reasonable efforts consistent with industry standard technology to de-identify Usage Data and Customer Content before such use."

**Proposed redline**: "Provider may use Usage Data to develop, train, or enhance artificial intelligence or machine learning models that are part of the Product, provided that (a) Usage Data is aggregated with data from a minimum of fifty (50) other customers before such use, and (b) Provider de-identifies Usage Data using methods that satisfy the de-identification standards under Applicable Data Protection Laws (including, where applicable, GDPR Recital 26 and CCPA Section 1798.140(m)) before such use. Provider will not use Customer Content for the purposes described in this section without Customer's prior written opt-in consent, which Customer may revoke at any time upon thirty (30) days' written notice. Nothing in this section authorizes Provider to provide Customer Content or Usage Data to third parties for machine learning or artificial intelligence purposes. This section does not survive expiration or termination of the Agreement, and Provider's rights under this section cease upon the earlier of expiration or termination of the Agreement or Customer's written revocation of any opt-in consent previously granted. Nothing in this section will reduce or limit Provider's obligations regarding Personal Data that may be contained in Usage Data or Customer Content under Applicable Data Protection Laws. Due to the nature of artificial intelligence and machine learning, information generated by these features may be incorrect or inaccurate. Product features that include artificial intelligence or machine learning models are not human and are not a substitute for human oversight."

**Rationale**: The standard market position for SaaS agreements distinguishes between Usage Data (which providers routinely use for product improvement) and Customer Content (which should be used solely to deliver the contracted service). This redline preserves Provider's ability to use aggregated, de-identified Usage Data for product improvement while protecting Customer Content from ML training without explicit consent. It replaces subjective safeguards ("commercially reasonable efforts consistent with industry standard technology") with objective legal standards, defines a measurable aggregation threshold, removes the third-party ML authorization, and ensures these rights do not survive termination.

**Priority**: Must-have

**Note**: This is the highest-priority item in the agreement given the stated data protection and IP ownership focus. Senior counsel should confirm whether the organization would accept any ML training on Customer Content with opt-in consent, or requires a complete prohibition. If complete prohibition is preferred, remove the opt-in consent mechanism and replace with: "Provider will not use Customer Content to develop, train, or enhance artificial intelligence or machine learning models for any purpose."

### Section 2.2 + Section 4 — Suspension Without Notice, Cure, or Payment Relief

**Current language**: "If Customer (a) has an outstanding, undisputed balance on its account for more than 30 days; (b) breaches Section 2.1 (Restrictions on Customer); or (c) uses the Product in violation of the Agreement or in a way that materially and negatively impacts the Product or others, then Provider may temporarily suspend Customer's access to the Product with or without notice. However, Provider will try to inform Customer before suspending Customer's account when practical. Provider will reinstate Customer's access to the Product only if Customer resolves the underlying issue."

**Proposed redline**: "If Customer (a) has an outstanding, undisputed balance on its account for more than thirty (30) days after written notice of non-payment from Provider; (b) materially breaches Section 2.1 (Restrictions on Customer); or (c) uses the Product in a way that creates an imminent security risk to the Product, Provider's infrastructure, or other customers' data, then Provider may suspend Customer's access to the Product as follows:

(i) For non-payment under (a): Provider will give Customer no fewer than ten (10) business days' prior written notice specifying the unpaid amount, and an opportunity to cure, before suspension takes effect.

(ii) For breach under (b): Provider will give Customer no fewer than five (5) business days' prior written notice specifying the nature of the breach, and an opportunity to cure, before suspension takes effect.

(iii) For imminent security risk under (c): Provider may suspend access immediately and will provide Customer with written notice specifying the nature of the security risk within twenty-four (24) hours of suspension. Customer will have a reasonable opportunity to cure, and Provider will promptly reinstate access upon cure or upon resolution of the security risk.

During any period of suspension exceeding five (5) business days, Customer's obligation to pay Fees for the suspended Cloud Service will be suspended proportionally, unless the suspension results from Customer's failure to pay undisputed amounts under (a). Customer will retain access to retrieve and export Customer Content during any suspension period. Any suspension under this section will not exceed thirty (30) days; if the underlying issue is not resolved within that period, either party may terminate the affected Order Form pursuant to Section 5.3."

**Rationale**: The current provision permits suspension without notice or cure opportunity, with no time limit and continued payment obligations. These revisions add procedural protections that are standard in enterprise SaaS agreements: written notice before suspension (except for genuine security emergencies), defined cure periods proportional to the severity of the trigger, fee abatement during extended suspensions, continued data access, and a cap on suspension duration. The narrowing of trigger (c) from "materially and negatively impacts the Product or others" to "imminent security risk" removes a subjective standard that could be used as commercial leverage.

**Priority**: Must-have

**Fallback**: If Provider requires the broader trigger language in (c), accept "materially and negatively impacts the Product or other customers" (adding "customers" to narrow from "others") provided that (1) written notice is given within 24 hours, (2) Customer receives a cure opportunity, (3) fees are suspended during any suspension exceeding ten (10) business days, and (4) Customer retains data access. The data-access-during-suspension and fee-abatement provisions are the core requirements.

**Note**: The escalation flag reflects the compound nature of this issue. Individual elements may be resolvable at working level, but the overall position on payment-during-suspension and the breadth of trigger (c) require business-level confirmation.

### Sections 3 and 2.1(a)(v) — No Security Obligations, Audit Rights, or Breach Notification

**Current language**: The Standard Terms contain no security obligations on Provider. Section 2.1(a)(v) states Customer will not "conduct security or vulnerability tests on, interfere with the operation of, cause performance degradation of, or circumvent access restrictions of the Product."

**Proposed redline — new Section 3.3 (Security)**:

"**3.3 Security.** (a) Provider will maintain administrative, technical, and physical safeguards designed to protect Customer Content and Personal Data against unauthorized access, use, alteration, or destruction. Provider's security program will include, at a minimum: (i) encryption of Customer Content in transit (TLS 1.2 or higher) and at rest (AES-256 or equivalent); (ii) access controls limiting Provider personnel access to Customer Content to those with a documented business need; (iii) annual third-party security assessments resulting in SOC 2 Type II certification (or equivalent); and (iv) a documented incident response plan.

(b) Provider will notify Customer in writing without undue delay, and in any event within seventy-two (72) hours, after Provider becomes aware of any Security Incident involving unauthorized access to, or unauthorized acquisition of, Customer Content or Personal Data. The notification will include: (i) the nature of the incident; (ii) the categories and approximate number of records affected (if known); (iii) Provider's assessment of the likely consequences; and (iv) the measures taken or proposed to address the incident and mitigate its effects. Provider will cooperate with Customer in investigating and remediating any Security Incident and will provide reasonable updates until the incident is resolved.

(c) Upon Customer's reasonable request (no more than once per twelve-month period), Provider will make available its most recent SOC 2 Type II report (or equivalent third-party security assessment) and will respond in writing to reasonable security questionnaires within thirty (30) days of receipt. Provider will give Customer no fewer than thirty (30) days' advance written notice before making material changes to its security program that would reduce protections for Customer Content.

(d) Provider will maintain a current list of subprocessors and subcontractors that access or process Customer Content, and will make this list available to Customer upon request. Provider will give Customer no fewer than thirty (30) days' advance written notice before engaging a new subprocessor or subcontractor that will access Customer Content. Customer may object to a new subprocessor in writing within the notice period, and the parties will negotiate in good faith to resolve the objection. If the parties cannot resolve the objection within thirty (30) days, Customer may terminate the affected Order Form and receive a prorated refund of prepaid Fees for the remainder of the Subscription Period. Provider will ensure that all subprocessors are bound by data protection and security obligations no less protective than those in this Agreement."

**Proposed redline — amend Section 2.1(a)(v)**: Replace existing text with: "(v) interfere with the operation of, cause performance degradation of, or circumvent access restrictions of the Product; provided, however, that Customer may conduct or commission security assessments or penetration testing of the Product upon no fewer than thirty (30) days' advance written notice to Provider and in accordance with a mutually agreed scope, methodology, and schedule. Provider will cooperate in good faith to facilitate such testing at least once per twelve-month period."

**Rationale**: Enterprise SaaS procurement requires baseline security assurances, breach notification commitments, and verification mechanisms. The current agreement asks Customer to entrust its data to Provider while providing no contractual security commitments and prohibiting independent security assessment. The proposed provisions establish industry-standard protections: defined security controls, timely breach notification (aligned with the 72-hour GDPR standard and emerging U.S. state law requirements), audit-report access, subprocessor transparency, and coordinated security testing. These are consistent with the security expectations for a SaaS platform handling internal operational data.

**Priority**: Must-have

**Note**: The specific security standards (SOC 2 Type II, encryption specifications) should be confirmed with the organization's information security team. If Provider holds ISO 27001 or other certifications in lieu of SOC 2, the language can be adjusted to reference the applicable standard. The subprocessor provisions should be reviewed alongside the data protection redline (Section 3.1 below) for consistency.

### No SLA — Uptime/Availability Commitment or Service Credits

**Current language**: No provision in the Standard Terms addresses uptime, availability, or service credits. Section 6.3 warrants only that Provider "will not materially reduce the general functionality of the Cloud Service."

**Proposed redline — new Section 2.3 (Service Levels)**:

"**2.3 Service Levels.** (a) Provider will maintain the Cloud Service at a Monthly Uptime Percentage of no less than 99.5% per calendar month. 'Monthly Uptime Percentage' means total minutes in the calendar month minus Downtime minutes, divided by total minutes in the month. 'Downtime' means any period during which the Cloud Service is materially unavailable to Customer, excluding: (i) scheduled maintenance for which Provider has given at least forty-eight (48) hours' advance notice, not to exceed eight (8) hours per calendar month; (ii) unavailability caused by Force Majeure Events; and (iii) unavailability caused by Customer's systems, networks, or equipment.

(b) If the Monthly Uptime Percentage falls below 99.5% in any calendar month, Customer will receive a service credit applied against future invoices as follows:

- Below 99.5% but at or above 99.0%: credit equal to 5% of monthly Fees for the affected Cloud Service
- Below 99.0% but at or above 95.0%: credit equal to 15% of monthly Fees for the affected Cloud Service
- Below 95.0%: credit equal to 30% of monthly Fees for the affected Cloud Service

Customer must request service credits in writing within thirty (30) days of the end of the calendar month in which the Downtime occurred. Service credits are Customer's sole financial remedy for failure to meet the Monthly Uptime Percentage.

(c) If the Monthly Uptime Percentage falls below 95.0% in any three (3) months within a rolling twelve-month period, Customer may terminate the affected Order Form upon thirty (30) days' written notice and receive a prorated refund of prepaid Fees for the remainder of the Subscription Period.

(d) Provider will provide Customer with at least forty-eight (48) hours' advance written notice of scheduled maintenance that may affect availability, and will use commercially reasonable efforts to perform scheduled maintenance during non-business hours (defined as 10:00 PM to 6:00 AM in Customer's primary time zone)."

**Rationale**: A SaaS platform used for internal operations requires defined availability commitments with corresponding remedies. The absence of any performance standard means Customer currently has no contractual recourse for service outages. A 99.5% uptime target with a graduated service credit structure is standard for enterprise SaaS at this deal size. The termination right for persistent underperformance provides an ultimate remedy without creating an aggressive exit mechanism for isolated incidents.

**Priority**: Must-have

**Fallback**: If Provider resists a 99.5% commitment, 99.0% is acceptable for a non-mission-critical internal operations platform with adjusted credit thresholds (5% at 99.0%, 15% at 97.0%, 30% below 95.0%). The termination right for persistent underperformance below 95.0% should be retained regardless of the uptime target. If Provider's position is that the SLA belongs in the Order Form rather than the Standard Terms, that is acceptable provided the Order Form is finalized concurrently and includes substantively comparable terms. The Standard Terms should reference the SLA obligation with a fallback default.

**Note**: Whether this provision resides in the Standard Terms or in a separate SLA exhibit is a structural question. Confirm with Provider where they house SLA terms and ensure the chosen location has contractual force equivalent to the Standard Terms.

### Section 1.6 + Section 5.5 + Section 5.6 — Post-Termination Data Rights and Retention

**Current language**: Section 5.5(b): "Upon Customer's request, Provider will delete Customer Content within 60 days." Section 5.6 lists Section 1.6 (Machine Learning) among provisions surviving termination.

**Proposed redline — replace Section 5.5(b) and add new 5.5(e)**:

Replace Section 5.5(b) with: "(b) Upon expiration or termination, Provider will make Customer Content available to Customer for export in a standard, machine-readable format (such as CSV, JSON, or via API, or such other format as mutually agreed) for a period of sixty (60) days following the effective date of expiration or termination ('Transition Period'). Upon the earlier of Customer's written request or the expiration of the Transition Period, Provider will delete all Customer Content from Provider's systems, including production systems, disaster recovery systems, and backups, within thirty (30) days and will certify such deletion in writing upon Customer's request."

Add new Section 5.5(e): "(e) During the Transition Period, Provider will provide reasonable technical assistance for data export at no additional charge for standard export functionality. Custom migration assistance, if requested, will be provided at Provider's then-current professional services rates."

**Proposed redline — amend Section 5.6**: Remove "Section 1.6 (Machine Learning)" from the list of surviving provisions. Add the following sentence at the end of Section 5.6(a): "For the avoidance of doubt, Provider's rights under Section 1.4 (Feedback and Usage Data) survive only with respect to Feedback voluntarily provided by Customer prior to expiration or termination and Usage Data that has been aggregated and de-identified prior to expiration or termination."

**Rationale**: Post-termination data portability is essential for a SaaS platform handling internal operational data. Customer needs the ability to export its data in a usable format for migration to a successor platform. Automatic deletion with certification (rather than deletion only on request) aligns with data governance best practices. Removing Section 1.6 from the survival clause ensures Provider's ML training rights terminate with the agreement, consistent with the Section 1.6 redline. The clarification on Section 1.4 survival prevents post-termination collection or use of new Usage Data.

**Priority**: Must-have

**Note**: The removal of Section 1.6 from the survival clause is directly linked to the Section 1.6 redline above. If the Section 1.6 negotiation results in a narrower ML provision (e.g., Usage Data only, no Customer Content), the survival question should be revisited — survival of a narrower, Usage-Data-only right may be acceptable. Senior counsel should confirm the preferred position.

### Section 1.5 + Section 11.1 — Customer Content License Scope ("Related Offerings")

**Current language**: "Provider may copy, display, modify, and use Customer Content only as needed to provide and maintain the Product and related offerings." (Section 1.5). "Customer retains all rights in Customer Content, subject to Sections 1.5 and 1.6." (Section 11.1)

**Proposed redline (Section 1.5)**: "Provider may copy, display, modify, and use Customer Content solely as needed to provide and maintain the Product in accordance with this Agreement."

**Proposed redline (Section 11.1)**: "Customer retains all rights in Customer Content. Provider's rights to use Customer Content are limited to the license granted in Section 1.5."

**Rationale**: The license for Customer Content should be limited to providing the contracted service. "Related offerings" extends the license to undefined adjacent products or services beyond the scope of what Customer has purchased. The cross-reference to Section 1.6 in the ownership provision is unnecessary if Section 1.6 is revised to require opt-in consent.

**Priority**: Must-have

**Fallback**: If Provider requires "related offerings" for service components that are functionally necessary to deliver the Product (e.g., an integrated analytics dashboard, a bundled monitoring tool), an acceptable alternative is: "Provider may copy, display, modify, and use Customer Content solely as needed to provide and maintain the Product and any ancillary services specifically identified in the applicable Order Form." This limits the scope to enumerated services rather than an open-ended category.

### Section 8.2 — Consequential Damages Waiver Including Direct Lost Profits

**Current language**: "Neither party will be liable for lost profits or revenues (whether direct or indirect), or for consequential, special, indirect, exemplary, punitive, or incidental damages, even if informed of the possibility."

**Proposed redline**: "Neither party will be liable for consequential, special, indirect, exemplary, punitive, or incidental damages, even if informed of the possibility. The foregoing waiver does not apply to: (a) either party's breach of Section 10 (Confidentiality); (b) Provider's breach of Section 3.3 (Security) or its obligations regarding Personal Data under Applicable Data Protection Laws or any applicable data processing agreement; (c) either party's indemnification obligations under Section 9; or (d) Provider's unauthorized use or disclosure of Customer Content in breach of Section 1.5 or Section 1.6."

**Rationale**: The mutual consequential damages waiver is commercially standard, but the explicit inclusion of direct lost profits closes a common and legitimate recovery path. The waiver is effectively asymmetric: Customer's consequential losses from service failure or data breach are typically larger and more varied than Provider's losses from non-payment. The proposed carve-outs target the scenarios where consequential damages are most likely and most significant — data breach, unauthorized data use, and confidentiality breach — consistent with the agreement's heightened data-use provisions.

**Priority**: Should-have

**Fallback**: If Provider resists removing "lost profits or revenues (whether direct or indirect)" from the waiver, an acceptable alternative is to designate data protection and unauthorized data use claims as Increased Claims on the Cover Page, subject to a higher liability cap. This preserves the mutual waiver structure while providing enhanced coverage for the most material risk areas. Proposed Cover Page language: "Increased Claims include: (i) claims arising from either party's breach of Section 3 (Privacy & Security), (ii) claims arising from either party's breach of Section 10 (Confidentiality), and (iii) claims arising from Provider's breach of Sections 1.5 or 1.6."

### Section 12.12 + Section 5.4 — Force Majeure Payment Asymmetry

**Current language**: Section 12.12: "Neither party liable for delays from Force Majeure Events, except Customer's payment obligations." Section 5.4: termination with prorated refund available "if a Force Majeure Event prevents the Product from materially operating for 30 or more consecutive days."

**Proposed redline — amend Section 12.12**: "Neither party will be liable for delays or failures in performance caused by Force Majeure Events. During any period in which a Force Majeure Event prevents the Product from materially operating, Customer's obligation to pay Fees attributable to the affected Product will be suspended proportionally for the duration of the disruption."

**Proposed redline — amend Section 5.4**: "Either party may terminate an affected Order Form upon notice if a Force Majeure Event prevents the Product from materially operating for (a) thirty (30) or more consecutive days, or (b) forty-five (45) or more days in any ninety (90)-day period. Provider will pay to Customer a prorated refund of any prepaid Fees for the remainder of the Subscription Period."

**Rationale**: Customer should not pay for a service rendered unavailable by events outside either party's control. The proportional fee suspension aligns the parties' positions symmetrically. The cumulative-day trigger addresses intermittent disruptions that may never reach the 30-consecutive-day threshold but substantially impair service availability over a longer period.

**Priority**: Should-have

**Fallback**: If Provider resists full fee suspension during force majeure, an acceptable alternative is a credit mechanism: "If a Force Majeure Event prevents the Product from materially operating, Customer will receive a service credit equal to the prorated Fees for each full day of unavailability, to be applied against future invoices." The cumulative termination trigger should be retained regardless.

### Section 1.3 — User Account Responsibility Scope

**Current language**: "Customer is responsible for all actions on Users' accounts and for all Users' compliance with this Agreement."

**Proposed redline**: "Customer is responsible for (a) all actions on Users' accounts performed by authorized Users or attributable to Customer's failure to maintain commercially reasonable security measures for account credentials, and (b) all Users' compliance with this Agreement. Customer will not be responsible for unauthorized actions on Users' accounts to the extent that such unauthorized access results from Provider's breach of its security obligations under Section 3.3 or occurs despite Customer having maintained commercially reasonable credential security practices and having promptly notified Provider of any known or suspected compromise pursuant to this Section 1.3."

**Rationale**: Unqualified strict liability for all account activity — including compromised accounts, credential theft by sophisticated attackers, or provider-side security failures — places disproportionate risk on Customer. The proposed language ties responsibility to Customer's actual security practices and carves out unauthorized access attributable to Provider's security failures.

**Priority**: Should-have

**Fallback**: If Provider requires broader responsibility language, an acceptable minimum is: "Customer is responsible for all actions on Users' accounts, except to the extent that unauthorized access results directly from Provider's breach of its obligations under this Agreement, including Section 3.3 (Security)." This preserves Customer's general responsibility while creating a narrow exception for Provider-caused breaches.

### Section 6.3 + Section 7 — Warranty Limited to Non-Degradation

**Current language**: "Provider represents and warrants to Customer that it will not materially reduce the general functionality of the Cloud Service during the Subscription Period." (Section 6.3). Section 7 disclaims all other warranties including merchantability, fitness for a particular purpose, and uninterrupted operation.

**Proposed redline — amend Section 6.3**: "Provider represents and warrants to Customer that (a) the Cloud Service will perform materially in accordance with the Documentation during the Subscription Period, and (b) it will not materially reduce the general functionality of the Cloud Service during the Subscription Period."

**Proposed redline — amend Section 6.4**: "If Provider breaches the warranty in Section 6.3, Customer must give Provider notice within forty-five (45) days of discovering the issue. **Priority**: Should-have

### Section 12.6 — Assignment on Change of Control

**Current language**: "No assignment without consent, except in merger/acquisition/change of control." (Section 12.6 permits assignment without consent for merger, acquisition, or change of control for both parties.)

**Proposed redline**: "Neither party may assign this Agreement without the other party's prior written consent, not to be unreasonably withheld. Notwithstanding the foregoing, either party may assign this Agreement without consent in connection with a merger, acquisition, or change of control, provided that: (a) the assigning party gives the other party written notice within thirty (30) days of the closing of such transaction, (b) the assignee assumes all obligations under this Agreement in writing, and (c) Customer may terminate this Agreement upon sixty (60) days' written notice following receipt of notice of a Provider change of control, and Provider will pay Customer a prorated refund of prepaid Fees for the remainder of the Subscription Period. **Priority**: Should-have

**Fallback**: If Provider objects to a termination right on change of control, an acceptable alternative is a confirmation mechanism: "In the event of Provider's change of control, Customer may request written confirmation from the successor entity that it will assume and comply with all terms of this Agreement, including without limitation Sections 1.5, 1.6, 3, and 10. If the successor entity does not provide such confirmation within thirty (30) days, Customer may terminate this Agreement upon thirty (30) days' written notice and receive a prorated refund." This is a softer mechanism triggered only if the acquirer will not stand behind the existing terms.

### Section 3.1 — GDPR-Only Data Protection Coverage

**Current language**: "Before submitting Personal Data governed by GDPR, Customer must enter into a data processing agreement with Provider. If the parties have a DPA, each party will comply with its obligations in the DPA, the terms of the DPA will control each party's rights and obligations as to Personal Data, and the terms of the DPA will control in the event of any conflict with this Agreement."

**Proposed redline**: "Before submitting Personal Data governed by Applicable Data Protection Laws, Customer and Provider will enter into a data processing agreement or equivalent data protection addendum ('DPA') that addresses the requirements of all Applicable Data Protection Laws governing such Personal Data, including without limitation GDPR, the California Consumer Privacy Act and California Privacy Rights Act, and any other applicable U.S. state, federal, or international data protection laws. If the parties have a DPA, each party will comply with its obligations therein, the terms of the DPA will control each party's rights and obligations as to Personal Data, and the terms of the DPA will control in the event of any conflict with this Agreement."

**Rationale**: Limiting the DPA requirement to GDPR-governed data leaves personal data subject to other privacy regimes without equivalent contractual protection. This is particularly material given the broad data use provisions in Sections 1.4 and 1.6 — the DPA's conflict-override language could mitigate some of those provisions, but only for GDPR-governed data under the current drafting. Extending the DPA requirement to all applicable data protection regimes ensures consistent protection regardless of the governing law.

**Priority**: Should-have

**Fallback**: If Provider's existing DPA is specifically a GDPR instrument and broadening it is impractical in the 2-week timeline, an acceptable interim position is to add a new Section 3.1(b): "For Personal Data not governed by GDPR but subject to other Applicable Data Protection Laws, Provider will process such Personal Data in accordance with safeguards no less protective than those set forth in the DPA, and the limitations on use of Personal Data in the DPA will apply to all Personal Data regardless of the governing data protection regime." This extends the substantive protections without requiring a new DPA instrument.

### No Subprocessor or Subcontractor Transparency

**Current language**: No provision in the Standard Terms addresses subprocessors or subcontractors.

**Proposed redline**: This is addressed in proposed Section 3.3(d) under the Security Obligations redline above. If Section 3.3 is accepted in full, no separate provision is needed.

If Section 3.3 is not accepted, add the following standalone provision:

"**3.3 Subprocessors.** Provider will maintain a current list of subprocessors and subcontractors that access or process Customer Content, and will make this list available to Customer upon request. Provider will give Customer no fewer than thirty (30) days' advance written notice before engaging a new subprocessor or subcontractor that will access Customer Content. Customer may object to a new subprocessor in writing within the notice period, and the parties will negotiate in good faith to resolve the objection. Provider will ensure that all subprocessors are bound by data protection and confidentiality obligations no less protective than those in this Agreement."

**Rationale**: Transparency regarding who processes Customer data is a baseline expectation for enterprise SaaS procurement. This is particularly important given the ML training provision's extension to "third-party components" and the absence of any current visibility into Provider's supply chain.

**Priority**: Should-have

**Fallback**: If Provider resists objection rights, an acceptable minimum is notification and transparency only: "Provider will maintain a current list of subprocessors that access Customer Content, make this list available to Customer upon request, and notify Customer in writing at least thirty (30) days before adding a new subprocessor that will access Customer Content."

## Cross-Reference Notes

Several redlines interact and should be reviewed as a package:

**Data rights package** (Sections 1.5, 1.6, 5.5, 5.6, 11.1): These redlines collectively define the scope and duration of Provider's rights over Customer Content. The Section 1.6 redline removes Customer Content from ML training without opt-in, Section 1.5 removes "related offerings" from the content license, Section 5.6 removes ML survival rights, Section 5.5 adds data portability and automatic deletion, and Section 11.1 removes the qualification on Customer's ownership. These should be negotiated as a unit — conceding on one affects the value of the others.

**Security and data governance package** (new Section 3.3, Section 2.1(a)(v), Section 3.1, subprocessor transparency): These provisions collectively establish the security and data governance framework that is absent from the Standard Terms. The security testing carve-out appears in both the new Section 3.3 and as a standalone amendment to Section 2.1(a)(v) — only one version is needed. The DPA scope extension (Section 3.1) and subprocessor provisions (Section 3.3(d)) should be reviewed together with the organization's data protection team.

**Performance and remedy package** (new Section 2.3, Sections 6.3/6.4, Section 2.2, Sections 12.12/5.4): These collectively define the service commitment and Customer's remedies for non-performance. The warranty fallback position references the SLA, the suspension redline interacts with the fee-abatement provisions, and the force majeure redline interacts with the SLA exclusions. These should be internally consistent.

**Cover Page dependencies**: Several redlines reference or depend on Cover Page variables (liability caps, Increased Claims, Unlimited Claims, Non-Renewal Notice Date). The Cover Page should be reviewed and negotiated concurrently with the Standard Terms to avoid gaps. Proposed Cover Page positions for liability caps are not within the scope of this redline document but should be addressed — the Playbook Comparator identified the "defaults to none" principle as systematically favoring Provider.
