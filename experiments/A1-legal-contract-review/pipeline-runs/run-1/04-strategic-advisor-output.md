# Strategic Advisor Output

**Important**: This analysis assists with legal workflows and does not constitute legal advice. All strategic recommendations should be reviewed by qualified legal professionals and relevant business stakeholders before implementation.

**Business context**: Customer side. $150K/year SaaS platform for internal operations. New vendor (not strategic but important). Standard procurement process. 2-week deadline. Data protection and IP ownership are priorities.

---

## Deal Assessment

This contract was drafted by a vendor that has invested in appearing collaborative while retaining maximum optionality. The Common Paper framework signals openness and market-standard positioning, but the Standard Terms are loaded with one-sided provisions hidden inside that neutral architecture. The ML training rights, the absence of any security commitments, the suspension-without-notice clause, and the total lack of performance standards are not oversights -- they are deliberate drafting choices that shift risk systematically to the Customer.

The pattern tells a story: this is a vendor that wants broad latitude over customer data (particularly for AI/ML development) and wants to avoid being held to specific operational commitments. The Common Paper wrapper makes these positions look more moderate than they are. A customer negotiating casually against the "standard" framing will concede more than they realise.

The balance of power is moderately in your favour, but not overwhelmingly. At $150K/year you are a meaningful mid-market customer -- large enough that losing you matters to the sales team, small enough that you will not get the vendor's most senior legal resources or the flexibility reserved for their largest accounts. The vendor has many customers like you. You are one deal among many for them. But you are also exactly the customer profile that their sales team needs to close consistently, so they have commercial incentive to reach agreement.

The 2-week deadline creates mild time pressure, but it cuts both ways. The vendor's sales team also has a quota, likely a quarter-end or pipeline target. A deal that stalls or falls out of their forecast hurts them. Do not treat the deadline as leverage against yourself.

The tone of this negotiation should be professional, direct, and commercially grounded. You are not adversarial. You are not trying to rewrite their contract. You are a sophisticated buyer who has read the contract carefully and needs specific provisions addressed before procurement can approve. Frame everything as "our procurement process requires this" rather than "your contract is unfair." The former is a problem to solve together; the latter is an argument to win.

## Negotiation Priorities

The redline document identifies seventeen distinct items. That is too many to fight on simultaneously in a 2-week window. Strategic prioritisation requires sorting these into tiers based on what actually matters for this deal, not just deviation severity.

**Tier 1 -- Non-negotiable. Walk away if these are not resolved.**

The data sovereignty cluster is the top priority and should be treated as a single interconnected position. This includes the ML training rights over Customer Content (Section 1.6), the survival of those rights post-termination (Section 5.6), the post-termination data portability and deletion provisions (Section 5.5), and the "related offerings" extension of the content license (Section 1.5). These provisions collectively determine whether the vendor gets perpetual, broad, transferable rights over your operational data. Given that data protection and IP ownership are your stated priorities, no version of this deal works if the vendor retains the right to train ML models on your content, feed it to third-party systems, and keep those rights after you leave. This is your hardest line.

Security obligations and breach notification (new Section 3.3) form the second non-negotiable cluster. A SaaS platform handling internal operational data with zero contractual security commitments, no breach notification, no audit rights, and a prohibition on security testing is a procurement non-starter for any responsible enterprise buyer. This is not an aggressive ask -- it is a baseline expectation. Frame it that way.

**Tier 2 -- Strongly pursue. Concede only in exchange for Tier 1 wins.**

Suspension protections (Section 2.2) matter because the current provision allows the vendor to suspend service without notice, without cure, and while continuing to bill you. This is commercial leverage masquerading as a contract term. Push for notice, cure periods, and fee abatement. If you cannot get everything, the fee-abatement-during-suspension and data-access-during-suspension provisions are the core requirements.

SLA and uptime commitments (new Section 2.3) are important for a platform supporting internal operations, but the specific numbers are less critical than having a commitment at all. A 99.5% target is the opening position; 99.0% is acceptable. What matters is that some defined standard exists with a remedy mechanism.

The consequential damages carve-outs (Section 8.2) acquire strategic significance specifically because of the data provisions in this contract. If the vendor breaches the data protection terms you are fighting for in Tier 1, you need a remedy that is not capped at a number that makes the breach economically painless for them. The carve-outs for data breach and unauthorised data use are the important ones.

Change-of-control termination right (Section 12.6) is elevated in priority because of the data rights at stake. If the vendor is acquired, every data right in this contract transfers to the acquirer. Your ML training protections and security commitments are only as good as the entity that inherits them. The compound risk identified in the deviation analysis -- ML rights plus change of control plus surviving rights -- is a real scenario, not a hypothetical. Push for at least the confirmation mechanism: the acquirer must affirm compliance with the data provisions within 30 days, or you can exit with a prorated refund.

**Tier 3 -- Pursue if there is bandwidth. Accept fallback positions readily.**

The remaining items -- force majeure payment symmetry, user account responsibility qualification, auto-renewal notice default, warranty enhancement, logo rights consent, PO term rejection, GDPR-only DPA scope, and subprocessor transparency -- are all legitimate improvements but none of them are deal-breakers at this deal size. Several of them (subprocessor transparency, DPA scope extension) are partially addressed if you win the security obligations provision. Others (logo rights, PO terms) are low-impact annoyances rather than material risks. Raise them. Take the fallback positions if needed. Concede them entirely if doing so helps close a Tier 1 or Tier 2 item.

## Counterparty Analysis

**What the vendor cares about.** The drafting pattern reveals three priorities. First, maximum flexibility on data use for ML/AI development -- this is not a throwaway provision. The specificity and breadth of Section 1.6 suggest this is strategically important to their product roadmap. They drafted it deliberately, and the extension to "third-party components" suggests they may already be integrating third-party ML services. Expect this to be where they push back hardest, because it has real product development value to them. Second, minimal operational commitments -- no SLA, no security standards, no breach notification. This keeps their cost structure flexible and their liability exposure low. The pattern of deferring everything operational to the Order Form and Cover Page suggests a vendor that wants to avoid committing to standards in the Standard Terms that apply across all customers. Third, revenue predictability -- the auto-renewal mechanics, the suspension-without-payment-relief structure, and the PO term rejection all serve to lock in recurring revenue and prevent deductions.

**Where they will push back hardest.** The ML training provision is their most valuable asset in this contract. Their likely counter will be to offer opt-out rather than opt-in, or to narrow the scope rather than eliminate Customer Content from the ML provision. They will argue that aggregation and de-identification safeguards are sufficient, and that ML training is how the product improves for all customers. Be prepared for this argument but do not be moved by it -- the question is not whether ML training is useful, it is who controls the decision to participate, and whether "commercially reasonable efforts" at de-identification is an adequate standard for your operational data.

Security obligations will generate a different kind of resistance. The vendor may not currently hold SOC 2 Type II certification and will resist committing to a specific standard they have not yet met. Listen carefully to the nature of the pushback: "we will not do this" is fundamentally different from "we cannot commit to this specific standard yet." The latter is workable -- you can accept equivalent certifications, or a timeline to achieve SOC 2. If they offer to share existing security documentation informally as an alternative to contractual commitments, that is a signal of willingness but not sufficient. Informal sharing is not enforceable and does not survive personnel changes on either side.

Embedding SLA terms in the Standard Terms will face structural resistance. The vendor will argue these terms are product-specific and belong in the Order Form. This is a legitimate point -- accept the structural argument but insist on concurrent negotiation of Order Form SLA parameters as a condition of signing.

**Where they likely have flexibility.** Suspension protections (adding notice and cure periods costs them nothing operationally and they know the current provision is aggressive). Logo rights (low commercial value, easy concession). Auto-renewal notice period defaults (administrative). Warranty enhancement to include documentation conformance (if their product works as documented, this costs them nothing). Post-termination data export (most mature SaaS vendors already have export capabilities). The change-of-control confirmation mechanism (as distinct from a termination right -- the confirmation mechanism is less threatening to their M&A positioning). Force majeure fee credits (rarely triggered, low expected cost).

**Where you have leverage.** You are a paying customer with alternatives -- you can walk away and use a different platform. The vendor's sales team has invested pipeline and effort in this deal. Your procurement requirements are institutional, not personal: framing your asks as "our security team requires this" or "procurement will not approve without breach notification" depersonalises the negotiation and makes it harder for the vendor to negotiate through relationship pressure. The deadline is mutual -- if they want this deal in their quarter, they need to move too. You also have the analytical high ground: you have read the contract carefully enough to identify the compound risks (ML rights plus change of control plus survival) that the vendor may not expect a mid-market buyer to raise. Demonstrating that level of understanding shifts the dynamic.

**Where the vendor has leverage.** You selected their product through a standard procurement process. Switching to an alternative involves restarting your evaluation, which has real internal cost. Their Standard Terms are Common Paper-based, giving them a "we use industry-standard terms" defence that carries some weight with internal stakeholders who want to close quickly. If your operational teams are already anticipating this platform, your negotiating team will feel internal pressure to close, and the vendor's team will sense it.

## Recommended Approach

**Lead with the data sovereignty cluster as a unified position.** Open the negotiation with the ML training provision (Section 1.6), the content license scope (Section 1.5), the ownership qualification (Section 11.1), the post-termination survival clause (Section 5.6), and the data portability and deletion provisions (Section 5.5). Present these not as five separate redlines but as a single data governance position: "Our data protection and IP policies require us to maintain control over how our content is used, both during and after the relationship. Here is our position on data governance as a package."

This framing has three advantages. It leads with your strongest asks on your stated priority areas. It signals seriousness without being adversarial -- data protection is an area where reasonable counterparties agree protections are necessary. And it creates the largest negotiation space early, establishing that the data provisions are interconnected and cannot be cherry-picked (conceding on one without the others leaves gaps the redline document explicitly flags).

**Present security obligations as a procurement gate, not a negotiation ask.** After the data cluster, introduce the security provisions -- breach notification, security standards, audit rights, security testing carve-out, subprocessor transparency. Frame these as institutional requirements: "Our information security team has reviewed the agreement and these are conditions of procurement approval." This framing is harder to negotiate against because it positions you as the messenger of an institutional process. It is also likely true -- most enterprise procurement processes require baseline security assurances. The vendor's sales team will understand this framing because they encounter it from other customers.

**Package the operational protections as a second-round conversation.** Suspension protections, SLA, warranty enhancement, and the consequential damages carve-outs form a natural group around "how the service works and what happens when it does not." Present these after the data and security items are on the table. They are important but less emotionally charged, and resolving them after the harder conversations builds momentum toward closing.

**Hold Tier 3 items as visible concession inventory.** Logo rights, PO term acceptance, force majeure symmetry, auto-renewal notice defaults, and user account responsibility qualification are your concession stock. When the vendor pushes back on Tier 1 or Tier 2 items, having items you can visibly concede creates reciprocity without costing you anything material. "We can accept your standard marketing provision and withdraw our position on PO terms if you accept the Customer Content protections" is a trade that gives the vendor something to report internally while preserving what matters to you.

**The key trade on ML training.** This is the provision most likely to reach an impasse because it touches the vendor's product strategy, not just their legal risk preferences. The recommended sequence:

First position: remove Customer Content from ML scope entirely. Usage Data (aggregated, de-identified to an objective legal standard rather than "commercially reasonable efforts") remains available for ML training within the contracted Product only, with no third-party ML systems and no post-termination survival. This is the redline as drafted.

If rejected, the structured compromise: accept opt-in consent for ML training on Customer Content (the fallback identified in the redline document). This gives the vendor a path to what they want while giving you control over the decision. Present the opt-in mechanism as the middle ground -- "we are not saying you can never use our data for ML, we are saying we need to choose whether and when to participate." Pair the opt-in with the measurable aggregation threshold (minimum 50 customers), objective de-identification standards, no third-party ML systems, and no post-termination survival.

If the vendor insists on the current opt-out or no-consent model: this becomes a business-level decision. At $150K/year for a non-strategic vendor, the question is whether the operational value of this platform justifies giving a vendor perpetual, broad, uncontrolled ML training rights over your internal operations data. The answer for most organisations is no. Be prepared to walk away from this point specifically.

**Before entering the negotiation, resolve one internal question.** Does your organisation accept any ML training on Customer Content with opt-in consent, or does it require complete prohibition? The entire data sovereignty negotiation branches on this answer. If complete prohibition is the only acceptable outcome, your position is simpler but harder -- you are asking the vendor to give up something they clearly value, with no compromise path. If opt-in consent is acceptable, you have a natural landing zone. Get this decision made before the first call.

**The SLA structural question.** If the vendor argues the SLA belongs in the Order Form rather than the Standard Terms, accept the structural point. Do not spend negotiation capital on where the SLA lives. But make it a condition: "We are fine with SLA terms in the Order Form, provided we agree on the specific parameters before signing the Standard Terms, and the first Order Form is executed concurrently." The Standard Terms should contain a reference establishing that an SLA will be provided, with a minimum fallback default if the Order Form is silent (e.g., 99.0% uptime). This prevents the vendor from agreeing to "put it in the Order Form" and then presenting an Order Form with no SLA.

**The consequential damages approach.** Leading with the removal of "direct lost profits" from the waiver is your opening position, but the more likely landing zone is the fallback: designating data protection, confidentiality breach, and unauthorised data use as Increased Claims on the Cover Page, subject to a higher liability cap. Frame the ask around specific carve-outs rather than the waiver structure itself. The vendor's legal team will be more comfortable adding enumerated exceptions than restructuring a mutual waiver they use across their customer base.

**The change-of-control approach.** Lead with the termination right on Provider change of control. Expect to land on the confirmation mechanism: the successor entity must affirm compliance with the data and security provisions (Sections 1.5, 1.6, 3, and 10) within 30 days, and if they do not, Customer may terminate with a prorated refund. This achieves the practical result -- protecting you if the acquirer will not stand behind the data commitments -- in a way that is less threatening to the vendor's M&A flexibility. The vendor's corporate development team will appreciate the distinction.

**The Cover Page must be negotiated concurrently.** The Playbook Comparator identified that the "defaults to none" principle for undefined Cover Page variables systematically favours the vendor. The liability caps (General Cap Amount, Increased Cap Amount), Increased Claims and Unlimited Claims definitions, and Non-Renewal Notice Date must all be resolved before signing. Do not let the vendor treat the Cover Page as a separate, subsequent negotiation. Specific positions: General Cap at minimum 12 months of annual fees, Increased Claims to include data protection and confidentiality breaches at 2x the General Cap, Non-Renewal Notice Date at 60 days.

## Timeline Considerations

Two weeks is tight but workable, provided you manage the process actively and do not let the vendor control the pace.

**Days 1-2: Send the complete redline package.** Transmit all redlines simultaneously in a single marked-up document with a cover note that frames the two major themes (data governance and operational assurance) and identifies the data sovereignty cluster as the priority. Do not drip-feed issues -- it frustrates the counterparty, invites piecemeal responses, and wastes time on sequential exchanges. Request a substantive response within 3 business days. Make clear that you intend to reach agreement within the 2-week window and that you are available for a call to discuss.

**Days 3-5: Internal preparation while waiting for the vendor response.** Use this time to resolve the internal ML training question (complete prohibition vs. opt-in acceptable). Brief your negotiating team on the priority tiers, the trade-offs, and the fallback positions. Align with your information security team on acceptable security standards (SOC 2 vs. ISO 27001 vs. equivalent). If the vendor has not responded substantively by end of day 5, escalate to their sales leadership: "We sent our redline on [date] and would like to schedule a call to discuss. Our timeline requires engagement this week."

**Days 6-8: First substantive negotiation round.** Whether by call or written exchange, this is where the real negotiation happens. Your goal for this round is to resolve Tier 3 items entirely (or concede them as trades), narrow Tier 1 and Tier 2 items to specific language disputes rather than conceptual disagreements, and identify where the vendor's resistance is principled versus reflexive. If the ML provision is a conceptual disagreement (vendor insists on retaining broad ML rights without consent), escalate to a call between senior stakeholders by day 8 at the latest. Do not let this item consume the remaining days in written back-and-forth.

**Days 9-11: Close the gaps.** Focus on the remaining open Tier 1 and Tier 2 items. This is where the package trades happen -- deploy your concession inventory. If the data sovereignty cluster and security obligations are substantively agreed, the remaining items (SLA parameters, damages carve-outs, suspension mechanics) should resolve relatively quickly because the hardest conversations are behind you.

**Days 12-14: Finalise and execute.** Final language review, Cover Page completion, Order Form alignment. Ensure the Cover Page variables are all resolved -- these are easy to overlook in the rush to close but they determine the economic substance of several provisions. Confirm the SLA parameters in the Order Form. Obtain internal sign-offs.

**If the timeline slips.** If by day 10 you are still fundamentally apart on the data sovereignty cluster, you face a three-way choice: accept a compromise position (with the internal ML training decision guiding how far you can go), extend the deadline by one to two weeks, or walk away. Do not accept an unsatisfactory data protection position under time pressure. At $150K/year with a non-strategic vendor, the organisational cost of a short delay is real but manageable -- the cost of a 3-year contract with the wrong data provisions is not.

One specific timeline risk: the vendor may attempt to run out the clock, knowing your deadline creates pressure to close. If their responses are slow, partial, or non-substantive, that is a negotiation tactic. Counter it by reframing the deadline as mutual: "We want to proceed with [vendor name], but our procurement timeline requires executed agreements by [date]. If we cannot reach agreement by then, we will need to evaluate alternative platforms." This is not a bluff if you have genuine alternatives -- and at $150K/year for a non-strategic platform, you likely do.

A second timeline risk: internal delays. If the ML training question requires escalation to senior business leadership, that conversation needs to happen in the first few days, not when the vendor's response arrives. Anticipate the decision and tee it up now.
