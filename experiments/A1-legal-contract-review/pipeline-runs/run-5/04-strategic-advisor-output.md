## Deal Assessment

This is a mid-market procurement — $150K/year, new vendor, operationally important but not strategic — and the contract reads like one drafted by a vendor that expects most customers to sign without serious pushback. The Common Paper framework is designed to be "reasonable by default," but this vendor has used the flexibility of the Cover Page variable structure to hollow out the substantive protections while keeping the framework's balanced appearance. The Standard Terms establish a comprehensive risk allocation architecture — liability caps, claim designations, support commitments, SLA — and then defer all the actual numbers and definitions to a Cover Page that isn't part of this review. That's a drafting strategy, not an oversight. It tells you the vendor is sophisticated enough to build a complete framework and confident enough to leave the substance for bilateral negotiation.

The pattern of what's present and what's absent is revealing. The vendor drafted meticulous restrictions on the customer (Section 2.1 is thorough and specific), broad data rights for itself (Sections 1.4 and 1.6 are expansive), and strong self-protective provisions (suspension without notice, non-refundable fees, broad disclaimers). Meanwhile, the vendor's own obligations — security, uptime, support, breach notification — are either absent or deferred entirely to documents that don't exist yet. This is a contract optimized to protect the vendor while appearing structurally balanced. The negotiation is about filling in the gaps the vendor deliberately left empty.

The good news: this is standard procurement for a non-strategic vendor. The customer has leverage. The vendor wants the deal — $150K/year is meaningful revenue for most SaaS companies operating with Common Paper templates. The customer is not dependent on this specific vendor, the relationship is new with no switching costs, and the two-week timeline, while real, is not a crisis deadline that forces premature concessions. The vendor will negotiate. The question is how to structure the conversation efficiently.

## Negotiation Priorities

The deviation list is long — six escalation items, eight negotiation items — but the strategic picture is simpler than it looks. The issues cluster into three themes, and the negotiation should be organized around themes, not around individual clauses.

**Theme 1: Data sovereignty and control (highest priority).** This is where the customer's stated priorities — data protection and IP ownership — converge with the most aggressive provisions in the contract. The ML training rights over Customer Content, the absence of security commitments, the missing breach notification, the post-termination survival of data rights, and the open-ended backup retention policy are all facets of a single problem: once data enters this platform, the customer has no contractual control over what happens to it, no ability to verify how it's protected, and no mechanism to fully withdraw it. This is the core of the negotiation. Everything else is secondary.

**Theme 2: Operational risk management (important but tradeable).** The suspension rights, SLA absence, warranty remedy timeline, and support commitments are operational concerns. They matter — an operational SaaS platform that can be suspended without notice or that has no uptime commitment creates real business risk. But these are conventional enterprise SaaS terms. Every vendor negotiates them. They are the expected battlefield, and the vendor's opening position (no SLA, no notice before suspension) is likely their aspirational starting point, not their actual floor. These items have clear market standards the customer can point to, and the vendor will move on most of them because refusing looks unreasonable.

**Theme 3: Commercial housekeeping (concession candidates).** The auto-renewal notice period, logo rights, and payment dispute timeline are legitimate issues but low-stakes relative to the data and operational themes. These are the items to trade.

Within Theme 1, the ML training provision is the single most important item in the negotiation. It is where the customer's data protection priority is most directly threatened, it is the provision most likely to provoke vendor resistance (because ML training on customer data has real commercial value to the vendor), and it is the item that will set the tone for the rest of the discussion. How the vendor responds to the ML training redline will tell the customer whether this is a vendor that respects data boundaries or one that views customer data as a product development resource. Lead with it.

## Counterparty Analysis

The vendor's drafting choices tell a clear story about their priorities and likely negotiating behavior.

**What they care about most**: Data rights. The ML training provision is carefully drafted — it addresses aggregation, de-identification, scope across the product line including third-party components, and survival post-termination. This is not boilerplate; someone thought about this clause and built it to be comprehensive. The vendor's product strategy likely depends on ML training across their customer base, and they will resist restrictions on Customer Content usage more than almost anything else. Expect this to be the hardest negotiation point.

**What they're protecting second**: Liability exposure. The decision to leave all liability cap amounts as Cover Page variables, combined with the explicit exclusion of direct lost profits, suggests a vendor that has been burned (or fears being burned) by liability claims. They will resist uncapped or high-cap liability categories, but they will negotiate on structure — which claims go in which tier — because the framework already anticipates tiered liability.

**Where they'll push back but will move**: Suspension rights and SLA. The vendor drafted broad suspension rights because it's standard vendor-template practice, but they know enterprise customers will push back. The absence of an SLA is likely a negotiating tactic — start with nothing, let the customer propose terms, negotiate to something modest. Expect the vendor to accept suspension with notice and a basic SLA (probably 99.9% with service credits) without enormous resistance, because these are table-stakes for enterprise SaaS.

**Where they have flexibility they may not volunteer**: Security commitments and breach notification. If the vendor already has SOC 2 Type II (which any vendor at this price point likely does), committing to it contractually costs them nothing. The resistance will be performative — "we don't usually put that in the Standard Terms" — but the substance is already in place. Push through the objection. Breach notification is similar: the vendor almost certainly has an internal incident response process; making it contractual just formalizes what already exists.

**Where they won't fight**: Logo rights, auto-renewal notice periods, assignment change-of-control termination rights. These are standard enterprise asks. The vendor will concede quickly or offer minor compromises. Don't spend negotiation capital here.

**The vendor's probable negotiating style**: The use of Common Paper's framework, which is designed to be "negotiation-friendly," combined with the deliberate deferral of substance to the Cover Page, suggests a vendor that is experienced with enterprise procurement and comfortable with negotiation. They are not going to be offended by a comprehensive redline. They expect it. The question is whether they have internal flexibility on the data rights issues or whether those are policy positions set by their product team. If the ML training provision is a company-wide policy, the negotiation there will be harder and may require escalation to more senior stakeholders on both sides.

## Recommended Approach

**Structure the conversation around the three themes, not around a clause-by-clause walkthrough.** Send the full redline package, but frame the cover note around themes: "We have three areas to discuss — data rights, operational protections, and a few commercial clean-up items." This signals that the customer has a clear hierarchy and is not treating every redline as equally important. It also makes it easier for the vendor's legal team to triage internally, which speeds up the process.

**Lead with ML training rights.** This is the hardest issue and the one that will take the longest to resolve. Starting with it avoids the trap of spending the first week on operational terms and then running into a wall on data rights with no time left. The proposed redline — Usage Data yes, Customer Content only with opt-in, outcome-based de-identification, no third-party component use without equivalent protections, termination of training rights at agreement end — is commercially reasonable and defensible. The vendor will counter. Likely counters: they'll try to keep Customer Content in scope with stronger de-identification language (outcome-based rather than effort-based, which is a genuine concession), or they'll propose an opt-out rather than opt-in mechanism. The opt-out approach is acceptable as a fallback if it's a genuine opt-out (on the Order Form, effective immediately, no penalty) rather than a theoretical one.

**Package the security and breach notification items together.** Present the security commitments, breach notification, and security testing provisions as a single package: "We need a contractual security baseline — here's what that looks like." This is more effective than negotiating three separate provisions because it frames the ask as a coherent expectation rather than three individual demands. It also makes it harder for the vendor to accept SOC 2 disclosure but resist breach notification — the package creates internal consistency.

**Trade the commercial housekeeping items explicitly.** When the vendor pushes back on substance, have concessions ready: "We can accept your standard auto-renewal notice period and your standard logo rights language in exchange for [X]." This is not about the value of the concessions — these items are low-stakes — it's about giving the vendor wins. Negotiations move faster when both sides feel they're gaining something. The customer's concession candidates are: auto-renewal notice period (accept the Cover Page variable if it's 90 days or less), logo rights (accept the fallback allowing customer-list usage without consent), and the payment dispute timeline (accept the current language if the SLA with service credits is agreed).

**Handle the liability framework through the Cover Page.** The Standard Terms liability structure is fine as a framework — the issue is that the Cover Page variables are empty. Rather than redlining the Standard Terms heavily, focus the liability discussion on confirming the Cover Page will contain: General Cap at 12 months of fees, Increased Cap at 2x for data/confidentiality/indemnification claims, and preservation of direct lost profits. This is a Cover Page negotiation, not a Standard Terms rewrite, and framing it that way will meet less resistance.

**On the escalation items**: The deviation analysis flags six items for escalation. For the negotiation itself, the ML training rights, liability framework, and suspension rights should be handled directly by the procurement team — they are commercially complex but within the scope of standard SaaS procurement. The security commitments, breach notification, and SLA absence are actually the easier items to resolve because they have clear market standards. The items that genuinely require senior review are: (a) the ML training provision if the vendor refuses to remove Customer Content from scope entirely, because that is a policy decision about data risk tolerance, and (b) the liability cap amounts if the vendor proposes caps significantly below 12 months of fees, because that is a commercial risk acceptance decision.

**Be firm on the must-haves, flexible on the mechanism.** The customer's non-negotiable positions should be: Customer Content excluded from ML training (or genuinely opt-in), outcome-based de-identification, breach notification within 72 hours, contractual security baseline, and a defined liability floor. How these are achieved — in the Standard Terms, the Cover Page, a DPA, or an exhibit — is less important than that they are achieved. If the vendor prefers to address security in a separate Security Exhibit rather than in Section 3, that's fine. If they want breach notification in the DPA rather than the Standard Terms, that's fine as long as the DPA is executed concurrently with the Agreement and covers all data, not just Personal Data. Focus on substance, not location.

## Timeline Considerations

Two weeks is tight but workable for a deal of this complexity, provided the negotiation is structured efficiently.

**Week 1**: Send the full redline package with the thematic cover note on day one. Do not wait for internal alignment on every position — send the redline with the proposed language and flag the two genuine escalation items (ML training scope and liability cap amounts) for internal decision by end of week one. The vendor needs time to review internally, and every day of delay on the customer side is a day lost from the vendor's response window.

**Days 3-5**: Expect the vendor's initial response. They will likely accept the operational items (SLA, suspension with notice, support baseline) with modest adjustments, push back on data rights (ML training scope, post-termination survival), and defer on liability (pending Cover Page discussion). Use their response to calibrate which items need a call versus which can be resolved in markup.

**Week 2**: Schedule a single negotiation call for the items that require discussion — likely ML training scope, liability cap amounts, and any remaining security provisions. Resolve the commercial housekeeping items via email in parallel. Aim for a final markup by day 10, with days 11-14 for internal legal review and execution.

**If the timeline slips**: The items to close first are the data rights and security provisions — these are the customer's stated priorities and the hardest to resolve. The operational items (SLA, support, warranty timeline) can be closed quickly once the data issues are resolved, because they follow market standards. The commercial housekeeping items can be resolved on the last day if necessary. Do not let the vendor use the deadline to force concessions on data rights — it is better to extend the timeline by a few days than to accept broad ML training rights over Customer Content under time pressure.
