# How to Fix Distributed Team Collaboration Without More Meetings

Your team spans four timezones. Somebody made a critical architecture decision at 3 AM your time. You wake up to 47 notifications, a Slack thread that forked three ways, and a meeting invite titled "sync on yesterday's sync." Sound familiar? Distributed engineering teams have been told that better collaboration means more tools, more channels, more meetings. But the problem was never a lack of communication — it was the wrong kind. Today, we're launching Relay, an async collaboration platform built from the ground up for teams where nobody shares a clock.

## The Real Problem With Distributed Work

Every collaboration tool on the market was designed for people in the same room at the same time. Then remote happened, and those tools bolted on async features as an afterthought. Slack added threads. Notion added comments. Confluence added... more Confluence. But none of them solved the fundamental issue: when your team is spread across three or more timezones, synchronous communication becomes a tax on everyone's time.

The numbers back this up. The average distributed engineering team spends 35% of their working hours in meetings — most of them existing just to transfer context that somebody missed. That is not collaboration. That is damage control.

We built Relay because we were tired of it ourselves. And after eight months of private beta with 200 engineering teams, we know it works.

## What Relay Actually Does

Relay is an async-first collaboration platform for distributed engineering teams. It combines the structured workspace of tools like Notion, the project clarity of Linear, and the video communication of Loom — but rebuilt around one principle: your team should never need to be online at the same time to do great work together.

Here is what that looks like in practice.

### Timezone-Aware Threads That Tell You What You Missed

This is the feature 80% of our beta users mentioned as their favourite, and it is the one that changes daily workflows the most. When you open Relay in the morning, conversations do not hit you as a wall of unread messages. Instead, the platform structures what happened while you were offline into a narrative. You see the key points, the decisions, the open questions — in context, in order, without scrolling through hours of back-and-forth.

One VP of Engineering at a 120-person company put it simply: "We stopped having catch-up meetings. The catch-up just... happens."

### Decision Logs That Capture the Why

Every team makes decisions. Few teams remember why. Relay's decision logs capture the context that led to a choice, the alternatives that were considered, and who was involved. When an engineer in Singapore wakes up to see that the API schema changed, they do not just see the outcome. They understand the reasoning. No more "why did we do this?" messages that restart the whole conversation.

### Video Snippets, Not Video Calls

Two-minute video updates that auto-transcribe, auto-summarise, and attach to the relevant thread. Record a quick walkthrough of a PR, explain a design trade-off, or give a demo — and everyone can watch it on their own schedule. The transcript means it is searchable. The summary means people can decide whether to watch. The two-minute cap means nobody is recording hour-long monologues that sit unwatched in a shared drive.

### Async Standups That Reveal Patterns

Relay's structured daily updates compile into a team pulse over time. Instead of a status check that evaporates after the morning call, you get visible patterns: what is blocking people week over week, where momentum is building, and where energy is dropping. It turns the standup from a ritual into an actual signal.

### Deep Work Protection Built In

This one matters more than people expect. Relay has configurable focus windows where notifications are batched and summarised. The platform does not just help you communicate asynchronously — it actively protects maker time. No pings during your four-hour coding block. When your focus window ends, you get a single, structured summary of what needs your attention.
