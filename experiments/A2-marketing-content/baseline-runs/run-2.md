# Relay: The Async Collaboration Platform Built for How Distributed Teams Actually Work

## Introduction

Here is a question most collaboration tools don't want you to ask: why does your team have more tools than ever and still spend half the day in catch-up meetings?

Distributed teams have been told the answer is better chat, better video calls, better notifications. But the real problem is simpler and harder. These tools were designed for people in the same room at the same time, then patched for remote work as an afterthought. They generate noise, fragment context, and punish anyone who isn't online right now.

Today, we are launching Relay publicly — an async collaboration platform built from the ground up for teams spread across timezones. After eight months in private beta with 200 teams, we are opening the doors. Relay doesn't bolt async onto a synchronous workflow. It starts with async as the foundation, and everything else follows from that.

## Why Async-First Is Not Just a Feature Toggle

Most tools claim to support asynchronous work. What they actually do is let you leave a message that someone else reads later — the same thing email did in 1995. That is not async-first. That is sync-default with a delay.

An async-first platform means the entire system is designed around the assumption that your teammates are not online at the same time as you. Every feature answers the question: how does this work when nobody is in the same room, the same timezone, or even the same workday?

Relay was built by a distributed team at Threadline. We felt the pain firsthand — the Monday morning scroll through hundreds of Slack messages, the meetings that exist only to repeat what was already written down somewhere, the decisions that get made in a call and never documented. We built Relay because we needed it, and because nothing else treated the async problem as a design constraint rather than an edge case.

## What Relay Does Differently for Distributed Teams

### Timezone-Aware Threads

This is the feature 80% of our beta users called out as their favorite, and it is easy to see why. When you open Relay after being offline, you don't face a wall of notifications sorted by timestamp. Instead, the platform structures what happened while you were away into a narrative. Key decisions, ongoing discussions, and items that need your input surface in context, not chronologically.

The result is that catching up takes minutes instead of an hour. You understand not just what happened, but why it matters to your work right now.

### Decision Logs That Capture the Why

In most tools, decisions look like this: a message in a channel that says "we're going with option B." If you were asleep when that message dropped, you have the outcome but no understanding. Why option B? What was option A? Who weighed in?

Relay's decision logs capture the full picture — the context that prompted the decision, the alternatives considered, and who was involved. Teams working across timezones don't just see results. They see reasoning, which means they can contribute meaningfully even after the conversation has moved forward.

### Video Snippets, Not Video Calls

Long-form video recordings are where information goes to die. Nobody watches a 45-minute meeting replay. Relay takes a different approach: two-minute max video snippets that auto-transcribe, auto-summarize, and attach directly to the relevant thread.

You record a quick update, a walkthrough, or a decision rationale. Your teammates get the transcript and summary immediately. They watch the video only if they need the full nuance. This is timezone collaboration without the timezone tax — no scheduling, no calendar Tetris, no "can everyone do 7am?"

### Async Standups That Show Patterns

Daily standups are supposed to create visibility. In practice, for distributed teams, they create scheduling headaches. Relay's async standups let each person post a structured update on their own time. Those updates compile into a team pulse that reveals patterns over weeks — not just what someone did today, but where momentum is building and where work is stalling.

Engineering managers and tech leads get signal without adding another meeting to anyone's calendar.

### Deep Work Protection

Here is something most collaboration tools will never tell you: they are designed to interrupt you. More engagement means more daily active users means better metrics.

Relay does the opposite. Configurable focus windows let each person define when they do deep work. During those windows, notifications are batched and summarized. The platform actively protects maker time, because a collaboration tool that destroys the work it's meant to support is not actually collaborating — it is just making noise.
