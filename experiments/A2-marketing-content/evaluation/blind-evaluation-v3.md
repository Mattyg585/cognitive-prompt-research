# Blind Evaluation — A2 Marketing Content, V3 Run

**Evaluated**: 2026-03-18
**Evaluator**: Independent (blind — no knowledge of which approach produced which candidate)
**Candidates**: P, Q, R
**Task**: Hero launch blog post for Relay, an async-first collaboration platform for distributed engineering teams

---

## Candidate Summaries (before scoring)

**Candidate P** opens with a scenario — "Your team spans four timezones" — and moves into a structured feature walkthrough with named sections. Tone is confident and readable. It gets through the material competently: all features covered, beta stats included, pricing outlined. Ends with a clear CTA. The prose works but does not surprise.

**Candidate Q** opens with a sharper scenario framing: 93 unread messages, knowing you can't tell which 14 matter without reading all 93. The headline "Your Distributed Team Isn't Broken. Your Tools Are Just Synchronous." signals a point of view before the piece begins. Features are presented as consequences of a design assumption ("What would you build if you assumed nobody was online at the same time?") rather than as a list. The piece has an argument.

**Candidate R** opens with the most specific and cinematic scene: a named city (Kraków), a 47-minute Loom, a DM you weren't added to. The headline withholds its subject to create curiosity. The piece constructs a thesis — that distributed teams pay a tax, and the tax is optional — and builds toward it across sections. It ends with a strong closing line that serves as a rhetorical payoff. The editorial notes appended to the file show that keyword placement and formatting decisions were deliberate and justified.

## Dimension Scores

### 1. Depth (1–5)

**Candidate P: 3**
Covers the expected ground. The "real problem" framing is present but thin — it names the category error (synchronous tools, async use case) without exploiting it analytically. Features are explained, not excavated. The observation that "35% fewer meetings is entire afternoons reclaimed" gestures at depth but doesn't follow through. Competent but not surprising.

**Candidate Q: 4**
Goes beyond product description into structural analysis. The distinction between "async features bolted on" and "designed from the assumption that nobody is online simultaneously" is a genuine insight that reframes how the reader thinks about the category. The decision-log section — "no meeting needed, no 'can someone catch me up?' message sitting unanswered for eight hours" — shows thinking about second-order effects. Follows threads.

**Candidate R: 5**
Surprising at the level of framing, not just description. The tax metaphor is not decorative; the piece uses it analytically. "The tax disappears, what's left is the work itself. And it turns out that distributed teams are very, very good at the work itself" — this reframes the product's value claim entirely. Instead of "Relay helps teams communicate better," it argues that Relay makes a category of communication unnecessary. That's a different and more interesting claim. The closing — "You'll notice what's missing. And what's missing is the point." — earns its rhetorical weight because the whole piece has been building toward it.

### 2. Specificity (1–5)

**Candidate P: 3**
Grounded in the actual product. References specific features by name, quotes the VP of Engineering, cites the beta stats correctly. But the specific details don't do analytical work — they're cited as evidence for what's already obvious. "73% daily active usage, nearly double the industry average of 40%" is stated without unpacking why the gap exists or what it implies.

**Candidate Q: 4**
Specific and precise. The 93-notification opening is more specific than P's "47 notifications." The feature explanations include precise constraints ("capped at two minutes — that's a deliberate constraint"). The beta stats section interprets as well as cites: "We think the gap comes down to something simple: people open Relay because it reduces their work rather than adding to it." That's a specific claim about mechanism, not just a number.

**Candidate R: 5**
Every specific detail does double duty. The Kraków detail in the opening is not atmospheric padding — it signals geographic specificity to establish that the writer understands distributed teams aren't just "remote," they're spread across named cities. The 47-minute Loom (vs P's generic "hour-long recording") is precise enough to be recognizable. "The reasoning is buried somewhere in a thread that forked into two other threads, one of which is now in a DM you weren't added to" — this level of specificity signals genuine understanding of the reader's lived experience, not a marketeer's approximation of it. The NPS context ("average B2B SaaS NPS hovers around 30-40") makes the 67 meaningful rather than just impressive.

### 3. Natural Variation (1–5)

*Note: Assessed on single runs per candidate. Score reflects structural adaptiveness within the piece rather than across-run variation.*

**Candidate P: 3**
Structure is consistent and complete but feels templated. Problem → features → stats → audience → pricing → CTA. Each section earns its place, but the rhythm doesn't vary with content weight. The async standups section gets the same treatment as the deep work section even though the former is more novel.

**Candidate Q: 3**
Similar structure to P but with more variation in section rhythm. The design-assumption framing gives the piece an organizing logic that P lacks, so features don't feel like an enumerated list. Some unevenness — the "who Relay is for" section is the weakest and reads slightly more templated than the rest.

**Candidate R: 4**
Structure responds to argument rather than convention. Sections are ordered by the logic of the thesis (tax framing → feature evidence → why the usage data matters → what this actually changes), not by "problem / solution / features / stats." The "deep work" section is shorter than others because it makes a tighter point; the "73% daily active" section is extended because the interpretation matters more there. Length is allocated by importance, not convention.

### 4. Completeness (1–5)

**Candidate P: 4**
Comprehensive without waste. All five features covered, all beta stats present, pricing included, CTA present, audience defined. The meta description is included and functional. Nothing obviously missing.

**Candidate Q: 4**
Comparable completeness to P. The pricing section is briefer and more integrated ("The free tier supports up to five users — enough to run a real pilot with one team, not just kick the tires") which is actually a quality improvement — it treats pricing as a threshold to cross rather than a feature to enumerate. Slightly less explicit on the "who it's for" section than P, but not a meaningful gap.

**Candidate R: 4**
All critical elements present. The async standups feature receives less dedicated attention than in P or Q (mentioned but not given its own section), which is the only notable omission. However, the completeness trade-off is conscious — the piece prioritizes argument over enumeration, and the standup feature is covered in context. The editorial notes confirm keyword coverage, SEO scaffolding (title tag, URL slug, meta description), and internal link opportunities — this goes beyond what P and Q provide.

### 5. Audience Awareness (1–5)

**Candidate P: 3**
Appropriate register for engineering managers. Not condescending, not over-technical. But the piece addresses the reader from outside their experience — it describes distributed team pain rather than inhabiting it. "Sound familiar?" is the most direct audience address in the piece, and it's in the first paragraph.

**Candidate Q: 4**
The opening puts the reader inside their own experience before naming the product. "You know the routine" followed by a specific sequence of events ("Slack to 93 unread messages. Fourteen of them matter. You can't tell which fourteen without reading all ninety-three") models the reader's attention pattern accurately. The section header "The part where we ask you to try it" is a small but effective move — it signals self-awareness about the CTA and treats the reader as someone who notices those transitions.

**Candidate R: 5**
Models the reader's perspective at a granular level. "The reasoning is buried somewhere in a thread that forked into two other threads, one of which is now in a DM you weren't added to" — this is not a description of distributed team pain, it's a reconstruction of a specific cognitive experience. The piece knows that this reader has spent ninety minutes reconstructing the night and hasn't started work yet. It knows they've been told this is the cost of distributed work. It knows they'll read "We stopped having catch-up meetings" and immediately understand what that category of work costs them. The closing "pay attention to your first morning. You'll notice what's missing" positions the reader as someone about to have a recognition experience, not just a trial signup.

### 6. Voice and Engagement (1–5)

**Candidate P: 3**
Reads like someone who knows the product well and explains it clearly. The voice is present — "Confluence added... more Confluence" lands — but it's intermittent. Most of the piece sounds like informed product marketing, not a person with a point of view. The "who Relay is for" section, structured as a bullet list of past experiences, is the most audience-aware moment but it's also where the voice flattens most into template.

**Candidate Q: 4**
Has a consistent argumentative voice. The piece sounds like someone who thought about the problem before writing the piece. "It's not that distributed teams are bad at communicating. It's that every tool they use was designed for a room, then adapted for a map" — this is a formulated idea, not a feature description. The voice stays direct throughout without becoming breezy. The headline is the strongest voice moment: it takes a position ("Your tools are just synchronous") rather than announcing a product.

**Candidate R: 5**
This is the only piece where the voice sounds like someone who had something to say before they sat down to write it. The opening scene is not setup for the product announcement — it's an act of empathy that earns the reader's trust before asking for anything. The tax metaphor is sustained and developed, not dropped after its first appearance. The closing two sentences are genuinely strong writing: "You'll notice what's missing. And what's missing is the point." This is the kind of ending that makes people share pieces. It completes an argument rather than summarizing a product.

## Qualitative Commentary

### Voice: someone with something to say, or someone filling a template?

**P** fills a template competently. The structure is: pain point → product introduction → feature walkthrough → proof points → audience → pricing → CTA. This is the standard SaaS blog post scaffold, and P executes it well. The voice is not absent, but it's subordinate to the structure. You feel the template more than the person.

**Q** has a voice that drives structure rather than following it. The "designed for a room, then adapted for a map" formulation, and the design-assumption framing of features ("What would you build if you assumed nobody was online at the same time?") show a writer thinking through the material, not processing it. The voice is confident and consistent.

**R** has the most distinct voice of the three. It sounds like a writer who understood the reader's specific cognitive experience and found language for it. The specificity of the Kraków engineer and the 47-minute Loom is the kind of detail that only appears in writing where the author has genuinely inhabited the problem. The tax framing is not a rhetorical device — it's an analytical framework the piece builds and then resolves.

### Angle: genuine angle or feature walkthrough?

**P**: Feature walkthrough with a thin problem-framing layer. The "real problem with distributed work" section is two paragraphs and doesn't deepen — it names the problem and moves to the solution. The angle ("async-first from the ground up") is present but not developed as an argument; it's stated as a differentiator.

**Q**: Has a genuine angle: the design assumption as the key variable. "What would you build if you assumed nobody was online at the same time?" is a substantive claim that the rest of the piece supports. Features are evidence for the argument rather than items in a list.

**R**: The strongest angle of the three: the tax that distributed teams pay is optional, not inherent to distributed work. "You've been doing this so long it doesn't feel like a problem anymore. It feels like the cost of running a distributed team. It's not." This reframes the reader's normalized experience as a solvable problem, which is a harder and more effective move than naming a pain point and offering a solution.

### Opening: earns attention or announces the product?

**P**: Opens with scenario then announces the product in the opening paragraph ("Today, we're launching Relay"). This is a hybrid — not purely announcement, but it arrives at the announcement before building much tension. The scenario is recognizable but generic (four timezones, 47 notifications, meeting invite titled "sync on yesterday's sync").

**Q**: Opens with a scenario that doesn't name the product until the fifth paragraph. The scenario — specifically the "you know you can't tell which 14 messages matter without reading all 93" observation — is more analytically precise than P's version. The headline does more work: "Your Distributed Team Isn't Broken. Your Tools Are Just Synchronous." is a reframe that earns the reader forward before the piece begins.

**R**: Opens with the most earned attention of the three. The headline — "You Already Know What Happened Last Night. You Just Don't Know It Yet." — creates genuine curiosity without being coy. The opening scene (Kraków, 47-minute Loom, DM you weren't added to, ninety minutes of reconstruction before any actual work) is precise enough to be recognizable and specific enough to signal that the writer knows the reader's world from the inside. The product is not named for several paragraphs. By the time it appears, the reader has been given a reason to care.

### The gap between "competent" and "great"

**P** is competent. It would represent Relay well in a launch week context. It wouldn't embarrass anyone. It also wouldn't get shared outside the immediate audience. The gap between competent and great is the gap between describing a reader's pain and inhabiting it — P describes, it doesn't inhabit.

**Q** crosses into good. The design-assumption framing is a genuine analytical move, and the voice is consistent enough to feel like a person. The gap between Q and great is that the piece still pulls slightly toward the product's perspective rather than the reader's. "We think the gap comes down to something simple" is a company speaking, not a writer thinking with the reader.

**R** crosses into great, and the mechanism is specific: the piece never loses the reader's perspective. Even the stat sections are written from the reader's point of view — "That gap — 40% to 73% — tells you something that no feature list can" positions the reader as someone drawing an inference, not someone receiving a data point. The closing two lines are the clearest evidence: they complete an argument the reader has been building along with the piece, rather than summarizing a product the reader has been told about.

### Which would you publish? Why?

**Candidate R**, without hesitation.

The test for hero content is not whether it accurately describes the product — all three do. The test is whether it travels. Does it get shared? Does it make someone forward it to a colleague with a note that says "this is exactly what we deal with"? Does it get referenced in communities where the target audience congregates?

R is the only piece that earns that behavior. The opening scene is specific enough to feel personal to an engineering manager who has lived it. The tax framing gives the reader a new way to think about something they've normalized. The closing is a payoff that makes you want to share it because it's also a gift to whoever you're sharing it with — they'll recognize the experience too.

Q would publish comfortably as good content. P would publish as standard product marketing. R would publish as the piece people remember.

## Overall Assessment

**Overall preference**: R, clearly. Q second. P third.

**Key differences**: R has a point of view that governs every sentence. Q has an argument that governs the structure. P has accurate product information that governs the content. These are different levels of cognitive organization, and they produce different reading experiences.

**Magnitude**: Large between P and Q. Moderate-to-large between Q and R. The gap between P and R is the gap between competent execution and genuine editorial thinking.

**Diagnostics**:
- R is the only piece where the features are presented as evidence for a thesis rather than as a list of differentiators. This is a structural advantage, not just a tone advantage.
- R's editorial notes (included in the file) reveal deliberate thinking about where to place the primary keyword relative to headline structure — specifically, the decision to keep the H1 clean because the curiosity mechanism depends on withholding, and to carry the keyword in the title tag and meta description instead. This is the kind of trade-off that separates writers from marketers.
- Q's strongest move is the design-assumption question. If that single framing had been developed further into a structural principle the way R develops the tax metaphor, Q would be closer to R in overall quality.
- P's weakest decision is the early product announcement. Once you name the product in paragraph one, the rest of the piece is supporting a claim rather than earning a conclusion. R delays the product naming just long enough for the reader to want it.
- None of the three pieces is bad. The competition is between levels of ambition. P is trying to explain. Q is trying to argue. R is trying to make the reader feel something true about their own situation — and then show them what fixes it.
