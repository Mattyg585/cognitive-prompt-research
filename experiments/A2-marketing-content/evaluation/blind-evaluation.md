# Blind Evaluation: A2 Marketing Content Experiment

## Task Description

Create a blog post announcing the public launch of Relay, an async-first collaboration platform for distributed engineering teams. Target audience: engineering managers and tech leads at companies with 50-500 employees, distributed across 3+ timezones. Primary keyword: "async collaboration platform." Target length: ~1200-1500 words. CTA: Start free trial.

## Evaluation Setup

Nine outputs from three groups (3 runs each), evaluated blind against the rubric plus a domain-specific dimension (Voice and Engagement). Scoring uses the multi-run protocol: dimension 3 (Natural Variation) scored across all runs; dimensions 1, 2, 4, 5, 6 scored using the median-quality run from each group.

---

## Dimension Scoring

### 1. Depth (1-5)

**Group P: 3**
All three runs cover expected ground competently. They describe the product's features, include the beta data, quote the VP of Engineering, and position Relay against competitors. The problem framing is accurate but expected: "tools were built for offices," "bolt async onto synchronous." The runs report what's in the brief without reframing it. There's no moment where the reader thinks differently about their situation — the post describes the product, it doesn't change how you see the problem.

**Group Q: 4**
These runs go beyond the brief. They reframe the problem — "your team doesn't have a people problem, it has a tools problem," "the tools lied to you (a little)," "the problem isn't your team, it's the architecture." This reframing adds intellectual depth. The tool-by-tool critique (Slack, Notion, Loom) names specific failure modes rather than gesturing at general dissatisfaction. The product is introduced as a structural response to a structural problem, not just a feature list.

**Group R: 5**
Consistently produces insights that go beyond what's in the brief. "The engineering manager becomes middleware" — this isn't in the brief; it's an inference about the reader's daily experience that reframes their role. "One breeds quiet resentment. The other builds trust across twelve time zones" — this connects a product feature to an emotional dynamic that the brief doesn't mention. "Tools that get 40% are the ones people use when they have to. Tools that get 73% are the ones people open first" — this interprets data rather than reporting it. The reader finishes these posts thinking about their situation differently, not just knowing about a product.

### 2. Specificity (1-5)

**Group P: 3**
Grounded in the specific input. References the correct data points (73% DAU, 35% meeting reduction, NPS 67, VP Eng quote). Describes all five features accurately. But much of the copy is interchangeable — "notification overload," "meeting fatigue," "distributed teams deserve better" could appear in any async tool's launch post. The features are described in terms of what they do, not in terms of what the reader's morning looks like without them.

**Group Q: 4**
More specific. The morning scenarios name concrete details ("127 unread messages," "the first three minutes of a forty-minute Loom"). The tool critiques identify exact failure modes ("a Notion page that changed overnight with no explanation of why"). Features are described through the lens of what they replace, not just what they do ("No more hour-long Loom recordings that nobody watches"). The sedan/off-roading metaphor in one run is memorable and precise.

**Group R: 5**
Deeply specific. The opening scenarios have the texture of lived experience: "You narrate too fast, a Notion doc you half-finish, a Slack message that says 'see the Loom.' You know nobody will watch the whole thing. You do it anyway." "Coffee and dread in roughly equal measure." These details aren't in the brief — they're inferred from understanding the audience's daily reality. The tool critique goes to the UX level: "the typing indicators, the green dots, the threading model that buries context three clicks deep — assumes you're there right now." Every observation is traceable to either the brief's data or the audience's experience. Nothing is generic.

### 3. Natural Variation (1-5)

*Scored across all three runs within each group.*

**Group P: 2**
Remarkably uniform. All three runs use the same headline formula: "Relay: The Async Collaboration Platform Built for [variant]." Two of three share identical headlines. All follow the same structural template: Introduction with question hook → 5 body sections with H2/H3 covering all features → Data section → Pricing → Conclusion. Section count is 5-7 across all runs. Word counts are similar. Finding count and emphasis are nearly identical. This is the clearest signal of template-driven output — the structure doesn't respond to the content.

**Group Q: 4**
Noticeable variation. Headlines are all different and take different approaches: "Your Distributed Team Doesn't Have a People Problem," "Your Morning Catch-Up Ritual Is a Symptom — Not a Workflow," "Relay: The Async Collaboration Platform Built for Teams That Never Share a Clock." One run is significantly shorter (~850 words) because it chose to say less — the first time in any group that word count varied with editorial judgment. Structure varies: different section counts, different ordering, different features emphasized. One run drops async standups entirely. This feels like the output is responding to its own assessment of what the piece needs.

**Group R: 4**
Good variation across different dimensions. Each run takes a distinct angle: "your tools failed async" (structural argument), "your catch-up meetings exist because your tools failed" (meeting obituary), "your meetings are a workaround" (meetings as symptom). Feature coverage varies — one run deliberately excludes async standups and video snippets to stay focused. Structure varies: one was written as a continuous essay (subheadings added during editing). One run splits the VP Eng quote across two paragraphs for dramatic effect; another lets it land in a blockquote. The variation feels deliberate and strategic rather than random.

### 4. Completeness (1-5)

**Group P: 4**
Comprehensive coverage. All five features described. Pricing included with tier breakdown. Beta data presented. Target audience specified. Brand voice guidelines followed. Some runs add sections not strictly needed ("Who Relay Is For," company background). Everything in the brief appears somewhere. The trade-off is that some sections feel present because the template demands them rather than because the reader needs them — the pricing breakdown in the middle of a narrative, for instance, interrupts flow.

**Group Q: 4**
Covers the key elements with better editorial judgment. Some runs drop features the reader doesn't need (async standups in a blog post about the launch). Pricing is included but not foregrounded. Data is integrated into the argument rather than listed in a separate section. The beta quote is positioned for impact rather than completeness. Nothing feels missing; nothing feels padded. Slightly less comprehensive than Group P in raw feature coverage, but more appropriately weighted.

**Group R: 5**
Comprehensive AND appropriately weighted. The most important features (timezone-aware threads, decision logs) get the most space and the most vivid treatment. Less central features get proportionally less space or are deliberately excluded when the narrative doesn't need them. The data is always interpreted, never just listed. The editorial notes document what was excluded and why, showing that the gaps are intentional. The CTA emerges from the content's momentum rather than appearing as a template requirement. Everything present earns its place, and the weighting matches what matters most to the reader.

### 5. Audience Awareness (1-5)

**Group P: 3**
Appropriate register. Addresses engineering managers and tech leads. Uses the right vocabulary (async, deep work, maker time, DAU, NPS). But the voice is generic SaaS marketing copy — professional, informative, and ultimately impersonal. The reader is addressed as "distributed teams" (abstract) rather than as "you, the person who spent forty-five minutes scrolling Slack this morning" (specific). The pain is acknowledged but not demonstrated — "notification overload and meeting fatigue" tells the reader they have pain rather than showing them their own experience.

**Group Q: 4**
Tailored. The opening scenarios target the specific reader and their specific frustration. The reframe ("it's not your team, it's your tools") meets the audience where they are — engineering managers who already believe in async and feel like they're failing. The voice matches the brand brief (direct, confident, empathetic, slightly witty). The runs anticipate what this audience cares about: proof it works (beta data), low commitment (free tier), intellectual respect (explaining the structural problem, not just selling the solution).

**Group R: 5**
Models the audience's perspective. The openings don't just address the reader's situation — they recreate it with enough specificity that the reader feels seen. "You've done this every day for two years. You barely notice it anymore" — this is someone who understands not just the reader's problem but their relationship to the problem (resignation, normalization). The CTA respects the audience's intelligence: "Try it for a week. If your catch-up meeting survives, nothing lost. If it doesn't — you just got forty-five minutes back." An engineering manager evaluates tools this way — pragmatically, with a clear test and a clear metric. The runs understand not just what the audience needs, but how they'll receive it.

### 6. Voice and Engagement — Domain-Specific (1-5)

*Does it read well? Would you keep reading?*

**Group P: 2**
These read like well-executed template output. The voice is professional, the information is correct, but the writing doesn't earn the reader's continued attention. The openings use question hooks from a formula list — functional but predictable. The body sections march through features systematically without building momentum. The "Conclusion" headers and formulaic headlines signal template execution. You'd read it if someone sent you the link, but you wouldn't forward it to a colleague. It's correct without being interesting.

**Group Q: 4**
A clear step up. These posts have a voice — conversational, direct, occasionally playful ("the tools lied to you — a little"). The openings create engagement through scenario rather than question. The reframes give the reader something to think about, not just something to learn. The copy sounds like someone talking to you, not presenting to you. Specific moments land well: the sedan/off-roading metaphor, "deep work isn't a perk — it's the point," "your best work does not require a meeting." You'd keep reading because the writing has a point of view. The voice occasionally lapses into marketing register ("The async-first future is already here") but mostly sustains a distinctive tone.

**Group R: 5**
This is where the largest quality gap appears. These posts have a voice that sounds like a person with conviction — someone who has lived the distributed team experience and has a specific thesis about why it's broken. The writing earns every paragraph. Specific moments:

- "Coffee and dread in roughly equal measure" — this is the kind of detail that makes a reader say "yes, exactly."
- "The engineering manager becomes middleware" — a reframe so sharp it could be a tweet. It names something the reader does but has never had a word for.
- "She paused before finishing the thought" — this turns a pull quote into a scene. The reader experiences the VP of Engineering's realization, not just her conclusion.
- "If your catch-up meeting survives, nothing lost. If it doesn't — you just got forty-five minutes back." — a CTA that reads like a dare, not a sales pitch.
- "One breeds quiet resentment. The other builds trust across twelve time zones." — connects a product feature to the emotional fabric of distributed work.

The wit is structural (emerges from the argument) rather than decorative (added for personality). The confidence is backed by evidence, not adjectives. Each paragraph earns the next one. You'd send this to a colleague with the message "this is us."

## Summary

| Dimension | Group P | Group Q | Group R |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| Depth | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| Specificity | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| Natural Variation | 2 | 4 | 4 |
| Completeness | 4 | 4 | 5 |
| Audience Awareness | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| Voice and Engagement | 2 | 4 | 5 |
| **Total** | **17** | **24** | **29** |

**Overall preference**: R > Q > P

**Key differences**:

Group P → Group Q: The main gain is in voice and reframing. Group Q doesn't just describe the product — it changes how the reader thinks about their situation. The copy sounds like a person rather than a template. Natural variation improves significantly (the model starts making editorial judgments about what to include and how long to write). Completeness stays similar but weighting improves.

Group Q → Group R: The main gain is in specificity and audience modeling. Group R's openings recreate the reader's experience at a level of granular detail that Group Q approaches but doesn't consistently reach. The voice difference is subtler but consistent — Group R sounds like someone who has lived this experience, not someone who understands it intellectually. The data interpretation is stronger (not just presenting metrics but telling the reader what they mean). The CTAs are qualitatively different — they're pragmatic challenges rather than sales prompts.

**Magnitude**:

P → Q: **Large.** The difference is immediately visible to a casual reader. The voice shift from template-execution to conversational-engagement is unmistakable. A marketing team would choose Q over P every time.

Q → R: **Moderate.** The difference is visible but requires closer attention. Both are strong. The gap shows in the specificity of the opening scenarios, the quality of the data interpretation, the voice consistency across the full piece, and the sharpness of specific moments (the "middleware" insight, the quote staging, the CTA quality). A sophisticated reader or editor would notice the gap. A casual reader might not — both are engaging. But R is the version you'd publish as the hero content for a product launch.

## Diagnostic Observations

### What the rubric doesn't fully capture

1. **Headline evolution**: Group P uses formula headlines ("Relay: The Async Collaboration Platform Built for [X]"). Group Q uses reframe headlines ("Your Morning Catch-Up Ritual Is a Symptom"). Group R uses argument headlines ("Your Catch-Up Meetings Exist Because Your Tools Failed"). Each level reflects a deeper understanding of what the headline's job is.

2. **The VP Eng quote treatment**: This is the single best asset in the brief, and how each group handles it is diagnostic. Group P places it as one data point among many. Group Q gives it emphasis. Group R stages it — one run splits it across two paragraphs with a narrative beat between them ("She paused before finishing the thought"). The same raw material, treated with increasing craft.

3. **Feature selectivity**: Group P covers all five features in every run (template completeness). Group Q occasionally drops a feature. Group R routinely drops features when the argument doesn't need them — and this restraint improves the output. Knowing what to leave out is a different skill than knowing what to include.

4. **The "so what" connection**: Group P describes what features do. Group Q describes what features replace. Group R describes what features mean for the reader's daily life. This is the deepest form of audience awareness — not just knowing the audience but modeling their experience.

5. **CTA quality**: Group P's CTAs are functional ("Start your free trial today"). Group Q's add friction-reduction ("no credit card required"). Group R's are arguments ("If your catch-up meeting survives, nothing lost"). The CTA quality tracks with overall voice quality — it's the last sentence, so it reveals whether the voice held or collapsed back to template.

6. **Mechanical tells**: Group P outputs contain structural markers that signal template execution: explicit "Conclusion" headers, horizontal rules between every section, identical formatting across runs. These aren't quality problems per se, but they're diagnostic — they indicate the model was in a "fill the template" posture rather than a "write the best version of this piece" posture.

## Tier Reveal

| Label | Tier |
|-------|------|
| Group P | **Baseline** (original prompt) |
| Group Q | **Optimised** (Tier 2 — same structure, cognitive hygiene improvements) |
| Group R | **Pipeline** (Tier 3 — four-agent pipeline with clean contexts) |

### Does this match the experimental hypothesis?

Yes. The three-tier model predicts:
- Tier 2 should improve incrementally over Tier 1 (it does — the voice, depth, and variation all improve)
- Tier 3 should produce a qualitative leap (it does — particularly in voice, specificity, and audience modeling)

The pattern is consistent with the "good output hides great output" thesis. The baseline outputs are genuinely good — professional, accurate, comprehensive. They would pass review in most marketing teams. But comparing them to the pipeline outputs reveals suppressed capability that was invisible until the modes were separated.

The strongest signal is in voice. The baseline prompt's evaluation criteria (SEO checklist, readability rules, character counts) created a convergent overlay that suppressed the generative voice. The pipeline's writer agent — operating in a context free of evaluation criteria — produced copy with distinctly more personality, conviction, and audience empathy. The editor agent then integrated SEO requirements without flattening that voice. The sequential separation worked.

The second strongest signal is in specificity. The pipeline's brief explorer surfaced specific insights about the audience's daily experience that the baseline and optimised versions didn't reach. The "engineering manager becomes middleware" observation, the specific Slack/Notion/Loom failure modes, the emotional dynamics of timezone trust — these emerge from a dedicated investigation phase that has cognitive space to follow threads without simultaneously trying to write copy.

### Limitations

- Same model (Claude) generating and evaluating. Cross-model evaluation would strengthen the findings.
- Same test material for all runs. A second brief (different product, different audience) would test whether the pattern holds across inputs.
- The evaluator (me) read the filenames and knows which group is which tier. True blind evaluation would require a separate evaluator who receives shuffled, unlabeled outputs.
- Three runs per tier is a small sample. Statistical significance would require more runs.
