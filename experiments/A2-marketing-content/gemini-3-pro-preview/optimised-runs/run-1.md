# Stop Simulating the Office: Why We Built Relay, the Async Collaboration Platform for Distributed Teams

You’ve felt it. The vibration of a Slack notification at 9 PM because your colleague in London just logged on. The "quick sync" that forces someone in San Francisco to wake up at 6 AM or someone in Berlin to stay online until dinner gets cold.

We call this "distributed," but it’s mostly just disjointed.

For the last decade, we’ve been trying to force-fit tools designed for collocated teams into a remote world. We treat chat apps like open-plan offices, expecting immediate responses. We treat video calls like conference rooms, defaulting to them for every decision. The result? A fragmented, noisy, exhausted workforce that spends more time talking about work than doing it.

We believe there is a better way. We believe that when you hire the best engineers in the world, you shouldn't force them to work on the same clock.

Today, after eight months of private beta with 200 teams, we are publicly launching **Relay**, the first **async collaboration platform** built from the ground up for teams that span timezones.

## The Sync-First Trap

Most engineering teams today are "remote-friendly" but "sync-first."

You use Slack or Teams for communication. You use Zoom for meetings. You use Jira or Linear for tickets. Individually, these tools are fine. But collectively, across three or more timezones, they create a chaotic environment of constant interruption.

To stay in the loop, you have to be "on." If you miss the real-time conversation, you’re left scrolling through hundreds of messages to find the one decision that matters. This fear of missing out (FOMO) keeps everyone tethered to their notifications, destroying the deep work blocks that engineers need to actually build things.

We built Relay to break this cycle. We didn't just want another project management tool; we wanted to build an operating system for **async-first** work.

## Enter Relay: Context Without the Chase

Relay is different because it assumes you *aren't* online at the same time. It’s designed to capture context, decisions, and nuance so that when your colleague in Tokyo wakes up, they have everything they need to move forward—without pinging you.

Here is how we are changing the game for **distributed teams**.

### 1. Timezone-Aware Threads

The core of Relay is the thread, but not as you know it. In standard chat apps, a thread is a linear stream of consciousness that buries context under chatter.

In Relay, threads are **timezone-aware**. The platform automatically surfaces relevant context based on *who* is reading and *when* they are online. It structures the conversation as a narrative. You don't see a wall of 50 unread messages; you see a summarized view of the discussion that happened while you slept, highlighting key pivot points.

One of our beta users, a VP of Engineering at a 120-person company, put it best: *"We stopped having catch-up meetings. The catch-up just... happens."*

### 2. Decision Logs That Actually Explain "Why"

How often have you looked at a closed ticket or a merged PR and wondered, *"Why did we decide to do it this way?"*

Usually, the answer is lost in a Zoom call recording nobody watched or a Slack thread that expired. Relay introduces **Decision Logs**. Every major thread concludes with a captured decision that logs not just the outcome, but the alternatives considered and the people involved.

This means a new engineer joining the team—or a manager checking in from a different continent—can understand the *reasoning* behind the code without tapping anyone on the shoulder.

### 3. Video Snippets: The End of the Hour-Long Recording

Video is powerful for nuance, but terrible for efficiency. Nobody watches the 60-minute recording of the meeting they missed.

Relay enforces a 2-minute limit on video updates. But we didn't stop there. Every snippet is **auto-transcribed and auto-summarized**. You can search the transcript. You can skim the summary. You can watch the video at 2x speed if you really need the emotional context.

These snippets attach directly to relevant threads. You get the high-bandwidth communication of video with the skimmability of text.

### 4. Async Standups That Spot Patterns

Daily standups in distributed teams are often a waste of time—either a calendar-clogging meeting or a robotic "What I did yesterday" text dump.

Relay's **async standups** are structured to compile into a "Team Pulse." We don't just log status; we visualize patterns over weeks. Are blockers increasing? Is a specific project dragging? The Pulse view gives managers a signal-to-noise ratio that a daily Zoom call never could.

## Protecting Your Maker Time

The most critical resource for any engineering team is focus. **Deep work protection** isn't a setting in Relay; it's the default philosophy.

We offer configurable "Focus Windows." When you're in a window, Relay holds your notifications. It doesn't just silence them; it batches and summarizes them. When you emerge from your deep work block, you don't get a firehose of raw pings. You get a digest of what needs your attention, prioritized by importance.

This is why our beta teams reported a **35% reduction in meetings**. By moving status updates, decisions, and context-gathering to Relay, the only meetings left are the ones that actually require real-time debate.
