# Relay: The async collaboration platform built for distributed teams

Launching a new tool is easy. Launching a tool that actually changes how distributed teams work is the hard part. After eight months of private beta with 200 teams, we’re opening Relay to everyone — an async collaboration platform built from day one for teams spread across three or more timezones. If your team is tired of waking up to a wall of notifications, sitting in “catch-up” meetings that could have been a message, or losing context in endless threads, this launch is for you. Relay turns the messy parts of remote coordination into a structured, narrative workspace so work keeps moving while people sleep. Today, we’re sharing why we built Relay, what makes it different, and the results we’ve already seen from engineering managers and tech leads who made the switch.

## Distributed teams deserve better than notification chaos

Most collaboration tools were designed for teams that share a timezone, a hallway, and a whiteboard. When those tools get stretched across the globe, the gaps show up fast: status pings pile up overnight, context fragments into “FYI” threads, and engineers spend their mornings reconstructing what happened while they were offline. The result is a false sense of alignment and a very real sense of fatigue.

Here’s the pattern we kept hearing from teams with 50–500 employees: they were moving fast but felt slow. Their tools created urgency without clarity. The average collaboration tool sees daily active usage around 40%, yet still generates a stream of notifications that demand attention. In an async-first environment, that mix doesn’t work. You need a workspace that respects deep work, makes decisions legible across timezones, and helps teams stay in sync without forcing them into the same hour of the day.

That’s why Relay starts with a simple premise: distributed teams shouldn’t have to choose between speed and sanity. A great async-first platform reduces noise, preserves context, and makes progress visible without meetings.

## Meet Relay, built async-first from the ground up

Relay is the first workspace we’ve seen that treats timezone collaboration as a core design constraint, not an afterthought. It brings the clarity of a product management tool, the depth of documentation, and the richness of short video — but in a single async collaboration platform optimized for how engineers actually work.

At a high level, Relay is built around five pillars:

- **Timezone-aware threads** that surface context based on when you’re online, so you can read “what happened while I slept” as a narrative, not a scrollback.
- **Decision logs** that capture the context, alternatives, and people involved, so the why doesn’t disappear when the meeting ends.
- **Video snippets with transcripts** — two-minute updates that auto-transcribe and auto-summarize, so quick demos don’t turn into hour-long recordings.
- **Async standups** that roll individual updates into a team pulse, making trends visible over weeks, not just today.
- **Deep work protection** with focus windows that batch and summarize notifications, protecting maker time without losing urgency.

Together, those features change the rhythm of work. Instead of chasing every ping, teams get a coherent story of progress. Instead of repeating decisions, they can build on them. And instead of scheduling another call, they can move forward with clarity.

## Timezone-aware threads: the feature teams loved most

We designed timezone-aware threads because the most painful part of remote work isn’t distance — it’s context decay. When a discussion happens while you’re asleep, you typically wake up to an unstructured list of messages. You can skim, but you can’t see the reasoning arc. That leads to rework, duplicate questions, and the infamous “wait, why did we decide this?” moment.

Relay solves this by automatically reshaping conversations into a narrative timeline. When you sign in, you see what changed, why it changed, and what you should do next. It feels less like scrolling through Slack history and more like reading an executive summary of your team’s progress.

In beta, timezone-aware threads were mentioned in 80% of feedback as the most-loved feature. One VP of Engineering at a 120-person company told us, “We stopped having catch-up meetings. The catch-up just… happens.” That quote stuck with us because it captured the real outcome: less coordination overhead and more time spent shipping.

For teams spread across North America, Europe, and APAC, that narrative view becomes a shared memory. It lowers the cost of being out of sync, which is exactly what distributed teams need.

## Decisions, updates, and focus — without the meeting bloat

Timezone-aware threads are the backbone, but Relay’s other async-first capabilities keep the work moving without the meeting load. Decision logs ensure that when a choice gets made — whether it’s a framework migration or a design tradeoff — the full context is preserved. The alternatives considered are recorded, the owners are noted, and the reasoning is visible. When a new engineer joins or a downstream team needs clarity, the decision is there in one place.

Video snippets fill the gap between written updates and live demos. Each snippet is capped at two minutes, auto-transcribed, and attached directly to the relevant thread. That means you can show a new UI flow or explain a tricky bug without scheduling a call. The transcript makes it skimmable, and the summary makes it shareable. It’s async-first communication that still feels human.

Async standups turn daily updates into signal. Instead of a dozen disconnected notes, Relay compiles progress into a rolling team pulse. Over time, patterns emerge: a bug class that keeps returning, a sprint goal that’s slipping, or a team that’s consistently blocked on the same dependency. That visibility helps engineering managers act earlier, not later.

And then there’s focus. With deep work protection, teams configure focus windows so notifications are batched and summarized. One engineering lead put it best: “The noise doesn’t disappear, but it stops interrupting.” That’s the difference between being available and being productive.

If you’re an engineering manager leading distributed teams, those outcomes translate into something tangible: fewer calendar fights, more time for deep work, and a shared narrative of progress that doesn’t depend on who was awake at 9 a.m.
