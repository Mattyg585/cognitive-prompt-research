# Relay: the async collaboration platform for timezones

If you manage a distributed team, you already know the pain: you wake up to a wall of pings, spend the morning catching up, and then lose your deep work window to the same meeting everyone else just had. Relay is an **async collaboration platform** built for that reality, not a synchronous office pretending to be remote.

After 8 months in private beta with 200 teams, Relay is now public. We built it for engineering managers and tech leads across 3+ timezones who are tired of noisy tools and exhausted teams. This post walks through how Relay makes async work feel natural, why it is different from the usual stack, and what you can expect in the first week of using it.

## What an async collaboration platform should actually do

Most tools claim to support async, but they still default to chat-first behavior. The result is a constant drip of interruptions and a backlog of context you can never fully absorb. An async collaboration platform should do the opposite: it should *deliver the right context when you are ready*, and stay out of your way when you are not.

In beta, Relay teams averaged **73% daily active usage**, nearly double typical collaboration tool engagement. That happened because teams were not forced to be online at the same time. Instead, Relay organizes work into purposeful narratives—what changed, why it changed, and what needs a response. The app is designed for decisions, not just messages.

A real example: a distributed platform team used Relay to replace their weekly “status sync.” Engineers posted short updates before their local evening. When teammates woke up, they saw a single narrative thread with decisions, blockers, and next steps, not a hundred scattered messages. That one shift reclaimed hours of meeting time per week and turned status into a daily habit instead of a weekly scramble.

## Timezone collaboration that feels human

Great **timezone collaboration** is not about compressing time; it is about honoring it. Relay’s timezone-aware threads surface what happened while you were asleep and summarize it as a story, not a notification dump. You can scan the narrative, drill into a decision, and respond without needing to ask, “What did I miss?”

This feature was mentioned in **80% of beta feedback** as the most-loved part of Relay. One VP of Engineering at a 120-person company put it simply: “We stopped having catch-up meetings. The catch-up just… happens.”

That is the north star for Relay. Your team should not need a second meeting just to interpret the first meeting. When the platform can explain the context in plain language, everyone moves faster—and the work feels less frantic.

## Decisions that travel with context

Remote teams do not fail because they lack decisions; they fail because the *reasoning* behind decisions gets lost. Relay’s decision logs capture the context, alternatives considered, and who was involved, so someone in another timezone can pick up the thread without guessing.

The result is alignment you can trust. Relay earned an **NPS of 67** during beta because teams felt like they finally had a shared memory. Instead of debating old debates, they could reference a decision log and move forward.

Relay also pairs decision logs with 2‑minute video snippets that auto-transcribe and auto-summarize. That means a tech lead can say, “Here is why we are switching to the new queue,” record a short update, and attach it to the decision. Teammates get the gist, the transcript, and the link to the discussion—all in the same place. It is the fastest way we have found to reduce “can we hop on a quick call?” requests without losing nuance.

If you want to see how distributed teams document decisions at scale, the [GitLab remote handbook](https://about.gitlab.com/handbook/remote/) is a useful reference. Relay is built to make that level of clarity practical for teams that do not want to live inside a wiki.

## Async-first rituals that protect deep work

Async does not mean unstructured. Relay supports **async-first** rituals that give teams a steady cadence without forcing everyone into the same hour.

Two features drive this:

- **Async standups** that compile into a weekly pulse, so patterns show up over time.
- **Focus windows** that batch notifications and summarize them later, protecting maker time.

Across the beta, teams reported a **35% reduction in meetings** after moving standups and handoffs into Relay. That is not just time saved—it is more uninterrupted time for engineering work, fewer context switches, and less burnout.

A simple habit helps: ask each engineer to post one update at the end of their day. Relay turns those into a coherent daily narrative by timezone, so the next person can start with context instead of questions. It sounds small, but it consistently changes the tone of a team from reactive to focused.
