# Relay: An Async Collaboration Platform for Distributed Teams

Distributed work isn't new. What's new is how many engineering teams live in three, four, or five timezones at once. When handoffs happen while half the team sleeps, traditional tools turn into a blur of pings, doc links, and meetings that exist only to explain what happened overnight.

Relay is an async collaboration platform designed for that reality. In this launch post, we'll share why Threadline built Relay, how it's different from Notion, Linear, Slack, Loom, and Confluence, and what 200 beta teams taught us after eight months of real use. If you're leading distributed teams and want fewer catch‑ups and clearer decisions, you're in the right place.

## Distributed teams need handoffs, not more meetings

Picture a team with a lead in San Francisco, a staff engineer in Berlin, and a manager in Singapore. A 9 a.m. standup for one is 1 a.m. for another, and the “quick sync” that should take 10 minutes becomes a weekly ritual. The cost isn’t just sleep; it’s lost context.

Most tools assume everyone is online together, so updates get scattered across chat, docs, and ticket comments. You end up with a second meeting just to reconstruct the first. For distributed teams, the handoff itself is the work, and the tool should protect that handoff instead of adding more noise.

If you want proof that async is now the norm, the [GitLab Remote Work Report](https://about.gitlab.com/remote-work/) is a solid overview. The takeaway: teams are learning to operate across time, not just across desks.

## An async collaboration platform, built async-first

Threadline is a Series A company with 40 employees, founded in 2024. We built Relay after spending eight months in private beta with 200 teams who were already living in 3+ timezones. Their ask was simple: a single place where decisions, updates, and tasks don’t disappear when someone goes offline.

Relay is an async-first product, not a retrofitted chat tool. Unlike Notion, Linear, Slack, Loom, or Confluence, it treats time as a first‑class constraint—so your work is organized by when you were last online, not by who shouted the loudest in a channel.

Here’s what Relay brings together in one workspace:

- **Timezone-aware threads** that surface a narrative since your last login
- **Decision logs** that capture context, alternatives, and who was involved
- **2‑minute video snippets** with automatic transcripts and summaries
- **Async standups** that roll into a team pulse over weeks
- **Focus windows** that batch notifications to protect deep work

## Timezone collaboration that reads like a narrative

Timezone-aware threads are the heart of Relay. Instead of a scrolling wall of updates, you see a structured story: what changed, why it changed, and what needs your input. It’s timezone collaboration designed for clarity, not speed.

In our beta, this was the most-loved feature—mentioned in **80% of feedback**. One VP of Engineering at a 120-person company told us, “We stopped having catch‑up meetings. The catch‑up just… happens.”

What that looks like in practice:

- You open Relay and see a narrative summary since your last login
- Each update links back to the decision or task it affected
- Context travels with the update, so you don’t need a live explanation

## What an async-first week looks like in Relay

On Monday, the team posts weekly priorities as short updates tied to decision logs. The engineering manager in New York records a 90‑second video snippet outlining the top three goals, and Relay auto‑summarizes it for anyone who’d rather read.

On Tuesday, a backend engineer in Tokyo ships a change and adds the decision context. When the tech lead in London logs in, Relay surfaces a narrative: what changed, why it changed, and the two open questions. She answers in the thread during her focus window, and the response is waiting for Tokyo before they wake up.

Midweek, async standups compile into a pulse that makes blockers obvious without another meeting. By Friday, the team can scan the week’s decision logs and see which tradeoffs were made and which are still open. The cadence is simple, but the effect is big: handoffs are clear, and the timeline doesn’t depend on being awake at the same time.

This is the point of async-first: work moves forward every day, not just when calendars overlap.

## Decision logs, video snippets, and async standups that keep momentum

### Decision logs make reasoning portable

Every decision in Relay captures the context, the alternatives considered, and who was involved. That means a teammate waking up in a different timezone can read the “why,” not just the “what.” For example: when a team chose between Postgres and DynamoDB, the decision log showed performance constraints, cost tradeoffs, and the final call—no follow‑up meeting required.

### Two-minute video snippets with transcripts

Some updates are easier to show than to type. Relay limits video snippets to **two minutes** and auto‑transcribes and auto‑summarizes them. You get the human tone without the hour‑long recording nobody watches. A design lead can demo a UI change in 90 seconds, and the engineer in another timezone can skim the transcript before jumping in.

### Async standups plus deep work protection

Relay’s async standups are structured daily updates that compile into a team pulse over weeks. Patterns emerge without a daily meeting, and blockers surface early. Pair that with focus windows—configurable times where notifications are batched and summarized—and you get collaboration that respects maker time.
