# Relay: The async collaboration platform where the catch-up just happens

Most distributed teams don’t have a coordination problem — they have a narrative problem. You wake up to a pile of pings, scroll through half a dozen channels, and still don’t know what actually changed overnight. The “catch-up meeting” exists because the tools can’t tell a story. They just dump events.

Relay is Threadline’s new async-first collaboration platform built for distributed teams across multiple timezones. After eight months of private beta with 200 teams, Relay is launching publicly to make alignment feel effortless — like the catch-up happens by itself. It’s the async collaboration platform that treats context as a first-class feature, not an afterthought.

## The real cost of missing context
When people work across three or more timezones, the gaps aren’t just inconvenient — they’re expensive. A decision gets made while you’re asleep. By the time you log in, you have the outcome but not the reasoning. You ask for the context, a thread restarts, and the same meeting gets scheduled again next week. That cycle quietly drains velocity and morale.

The fix isn’t “more communication.” It’s better communication that respects time. Distributed teams need a way to carry intent, alternatives, and history across the day. They need to see what happened without reading a thousand messages. They need an async-first workspace that makes the story easy to follow — even if you join halfway through.

## Relay is built for timezone collaboration, not adapted to it
A lot of tools claim to be async-friendly because they allow comments or send notifications. But async-first means the product works when you aren’t there. It’s proactive about gathering the right context, surfacing it when you arrive, and protecting deep work while still keeping everyone aligned.

Relay was designed around that reality. Every feature — from threads to standups — is engineered so teammates can contribute on their own schedule and still feel in sync. Instead of asking everyone to be online together, it creates a shared narrative that moves with you.

## How the catch-up happens in Relay
### Timezone-aware threads that read like a story
Relay doesn’t show you a wall of notifications. It assembles your threads into a narrative based on when you’re online, so you see what happened while you were away in a coherent sequence. You get the decisions, the blockers, and the context — without the scroll fatigue. This is the feature that 80% of beta users called out, because it solves the actual pain of distributed teams: the gap between events and understanding.

### Decision logs that capture the “why,” not just the “what”
Most teams can find the outcome of a decision. The problem is remembering the reasoning two weeks later. Relay’s decision logs capture the context, the alternatives considered, and who weighed in. When a teammate in another timezone revisits the thread, they don’t have to restart the debate — they can trust the logic and move forward. It’s a small shift that pays back every time a question would have turned into a meeting.

### Video snippets that respect attention
Long recordings are great at creating more things you’ll never watch. Relay replaces the hour-long call with short, two-minute video updates that are auto-transcribed and summarized. The video attaches to the relevant thread, so it’s easy to find later. You get the human tone and nuance, plus the searchable text, without spending your afternoon in playback.

### Async standups that show patterns, not noise
Daily updates are useful — until they become a ritual you skim and forget. Relay’s async standups are structured and compiled into a team pulse, so you can spot patterns over weeks, not just scan today’s list. Managers see risks early. Teammates see momentum. And nobody has to book a call just to say, “here’s what I did yesterday.”

### Deep work protection that actually protects
Distributed teams often default to always-on availability to compensate for time gaps. Relay flips that. Configurable focus windows batch notifications and summarize them later, so you can do real work without falling behind. This is where async-first becomes humane: the tool protects maker time while keeping the narrative intact.
