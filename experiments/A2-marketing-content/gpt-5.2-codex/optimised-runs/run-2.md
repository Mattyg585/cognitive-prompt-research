# Relay: the async collaboration platform where catch-up just happens

Picture this: you open your laptop at 8 a.m., coffee in hand, and the “what did I miss?” Slack scroll doesn’t feel like a punishment. There’s a clear, readable narrative of what happened while you slept, the decisions are already documented, and the team’s momentum is obvious. That’s the promise of Relay—an async collaboration platform built for distributed teams who work across timezones and don’t have the luxury of being “always on.”

We built Relay at Threadline because we’ve felt the pain too. The endless meetings, the notification overload, the handoffs that drop context halfway. Tools like Notion, Linear, Slack, Loom, and Confluence are great at individual pieces of the puzzle, but none were designed async-first from the ground up. Relay is different. It treats timezone collaboration as the default, not the edge case.

Below is what’s new, why it matters, and how Relay makes “catch-up” feel like a natural part of the workday—not a recurring tax on your time.

## The real cost of catching up

Distributed teams don’t fail because they’re in different cities. They fail because their work gets fragmented across time. When context doesn’t travel, you get:

- Decisions without reasoning
- Updates without relevance
- Meetings that exist only to patch the gaps

The result isn’t just frustration. It’s lost momentum. Engineers lose deep work blocks to “alignment,” managers spend their mornings stitching together narrative from scattered updates, and teams slowly normalize meeting fatigue.

We believe async-first work can be better than real-time work, but only if the system is built to surface context and capture decisions. That’s where Relay starts.

## What Relay changes

Relay is a collaborative workspace that treats async work as a first-class citizen. Instead of asking people to translate their thinking into someone else’s timezone, Relay makes the work itself readable, shareable, and sequenced. The goal isn’t to avoid real-time collaboration; it’s to make real-time the exception, not the default.

At its core, Relay gives you:

- A narrative view of what happened while you were offline
- Decision logs that preserve the “why,” not just the “what”
- Short video updates that are actually watchable
- Async standups that reveal patterns over time
- Focus windows that protect maker time

Let’s break those down.

## Timezone-aware threads: a story, not a scroll

Most collaboration tools treat async as an afterthought. You get a stream of messages ordered by time, and the burden of meaning falls on the reader. Relay flips that. Timezone-aware threads automatically surface context based on when you’re online. It feels less like a chaotic feed and more like a story with a beginning, middle, and end.

For engineering managers, this means less time reconstructing what happened overnight. For tech leads, it means fewer “did we already decide this?” moments. For everyone else, it means you can read once and move forward, instead of chasing backscrolls across five tools.

This feature was mentioned in 80% of our beta feedback for a reason: it makes async work feel human again.

## Decision logs that travel with their context

Decisions are easy to make and surprisingly hard to remember. In distributed teams, the pain is worse: you’re asleep when a decision happens, you see the outcome hours later, and you have no idea what alternatives were considered or why a trade-off was made.

Relay’s decision logs capture the full reasoning. Every decision records the context, the options, and the people involved. That means the next person to pick up the thread doesn’t just see a result—they see the logic behind it. This keeps debates from restarting, helps new team members ramp faster, and reduces the “wait, why did we do this?” cycles that slow teams down.

If you’ve ever joined a team and spent weeks reverse-engineering past decisions, you’ll feel the difference immediately.

## Video snippets that respect your time

Video is useful, but hour-long recordings are a tax. Relay includes short, two‑minute video snippets that auto-transcribe and auto-summarize. They attach directly to the relevant thread, so the context is always connected.

This is small, but it matters. A quick screen share to explain a tricky bug or a product tweak can save a dozen back‑and‑forth messages. And because the snippet is short, people actually watch it. That’s a big deal when your team spans multiple timezones and you want clarity without yet another call.

## Async standups that reveal the pulse of your team

Standups are meant to create alignment, but for distributed teams they often become a scheduling headache. Relay’s async standups are structured daily updates that roll up into a longer‑term pulse. Instead of focusing only on today’s status, you can see patterns over weeks: where work is stuck, where momentum is building, and where the team might be over‑loaded.

For engineering managers, this means fewer surprises and better resourcing decisions. For ICs, it means less pressure to be “present” at a specific hour just to share updates. It’s a more respectful way to stay aligned across timezones.

## Focus windows that protect deep work

We hear the same complaint everywhere: “I can’t get a two‑hour block without interruptions.” Relay’s focus windows let teams configure times when notifications are batched and summarized. That way, makers can actually make—and managers can keep context without constant pings.

This isn’t just a nice‑to‑have. It’s a structural guardrail against burnout. Async-first work should create space, not fill it. Focus windows are Relay’s way of making that promise real.

Relay is designed specifically for the realities of engineering organizations. That means it integrates naturally with how teams already think: issues, decisions, and progress over time.
