# Relay is launching: the async collaboration platform built for distributed engineering teams

If your team spans three timezones, you already know the pattern: you wake up to a wall of pings, spend an hour reconstructing what happened while you slept, then schedule a “quick sync” that turns into… another meeting.

Relay is an **async collaboration platform** built to break that loop.

After eight months of private beta with 200 distributed engineering teams, today we’re opening Relay to the public.

This post is the why behind the launch: what we built, what we learned from the beta, and how Relay helps engineering managers and tech leads run calmer, faster teams—without turning Slack into a second job.

## Distributed teams didn’t fail. The tools did.

Distributed work won. The problem is that most collaboration tools were designed for “everyone’s online at the same time.”

So we adapted. We stacked more channels. We added more notifications. We recorded more meetings “for people who couldn’t make it.” And somehow, the *tools meant to connect us* became the biggest tax on our attention.

If you manage a team of 50–500 engineers spread across multiple timezones, you’ve probably felt all of these at once:

- Important decisions get made while half the team sleeps
- Context is scattered across threads, docs, DMs, and video links
- “Just to be safe,” people schedule more meetings
- Makers lose focus to a constant stream of interruptions

Relay exists for one simple belief: **async shouldn’t feel like catching up. It should feel like continuing.**

## What Relay is (and who it’s for)

Relay is a collaborative workspace built specifically for distributed engineering teams—think the clarity of Linear, the flexibility of Notion, and the humanity of short video updates, without the chaos of a notification firehose.

It’s for engineering managers and tech leads running teams across **3+ timezones** who want:

- Fewer meetings without losing alignment
- Decisions that are visible *and* understandable later
- A daily rhythm that supports deep work
- A single place where “what’s going on?” is easy to answer

And because no two teams work the same way, Relay starts with structure—but stays adaptable.

## A better default for timezone collaboration: narrative, not noise

Most products treat async like a settings panel: mute this, snooze that, turn on “digest mode.”

We treated async like a storytelling problem.

### Timezone-aware threads

In Relay, threads aren’t just a pile of messages. They’re **timezone-aware narratives**.

When you open a thread after being offline, Relay surfaces the context you missed based on when you were last active. You see what happened while you were asleep, organized as a story:

- What changed
- Why it changed
- What decisions were made
- What you need to respond to (and what you don’t)

Instead of scrolling through 84 messages to find the two that matter.

### Decision logs that keep the “why” attached

Distributed teams don’t just need decisions—they need the *reasoning*.

Relay’s decision logs capture:

- The decision
- The context
- Alternatives considered
- Who was involved

So the next person (often in a different timezone) doesn’t see a mysterious outcome—they see the thinking.

This is the difference between “I guess we’re doing this now” and “Got it, here’s why.”

### Two-minute video snippets with transcripts

Some things are faster to show than to write. But long recordings are where information goes to disappear.

Relay supports **2-minute max video updates**—with automatic transcripts and summaries—attached to the relevant thread.

You get the nuance of voice and visuals *without* the hour-long “please watch this later” burden.

### Async standups that turn into team pulse

Daily updates are useful. Daily updates scattered across a chat channel are… less useful.

Relay’s async standups are structured and compile into a **team pulse** over time. Instead of only seeing today’s status, you can spot patterns across weeks:

- Where work is getting blocked
- Who’s overloaded
- What’s trending up or down

It’s visibility without surveillance—and it’s surprisingly calming.

### Deep work protection (yes, really)

Your calendar isn’t the only threat to deep work. Notifications are meetings with worse branding.

Relay includes configurable **focus windows** where notifications are batched and summarized. The default posture is: protect maker time, then surface what matters.

## Why this matters now (and what “async-first” actually means)

That’s the heart of building for distributed teams. Not just enabling remote work—but making it sustainable.
