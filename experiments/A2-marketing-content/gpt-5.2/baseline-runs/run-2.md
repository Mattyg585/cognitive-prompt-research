# Relay: the async collaboration platform for distributed teams

If your team spans three (or ten) timezones, you already know the pattern: the day starts by catching up. You scroll through a wall of notifications, try to reconstruct what happened while you were asleep, and then book a “quick sync” to confirm what you *think* you learned.

That cycle is exhausting—and it steals the time your engineers need for deep work.

Today, we’re launching **Relay**, an **async collaboration platform** built for distributed engineering teams. After 8 months in private beta with 200 teams, Relay is now publicly available. Our goal is simple: help teams collaborate *without* turning every question into a meeting or every update into noise.


## An async collaboration platform that keeps context intact

Most tools treat collaboration like a stream: new message in, old message out. That works when everyone is online together. It breaks down when half the team is asleep.

For distributed teams, the real problem isn’t communication volume—it’s **context loss**.

Relay is designed around a different assumption: **async-first** work is normal. Instead of optimizing for speed-of-reply, we optimize for clarity-of-understanding. That means:

- Threads that read like a narrative instead of a notification waterfall
- Decisions that keep their “why,” not just the final “what”
- Updates that are quick to record, quick to consume, and easy to find later

When you can *trust the context*, you don’t need as many meetings to fill the gaps.


## Timezone-aware threads: wake up to the story, not the scroll

The most-loved feature in our beta wasn’t a flashy dashboard or a new emoji reaction. It was **timezone-aware threads**—mentioned in **80% of user feedback**.

Here’s what it does: Relay automatically surfaces the right context for whoever is reading, based on when they’re online. If you’re opening a thread in the morning, you see what happened overnight as a structured narrative: key updates, key decisions, and the “open loops” you can pick up.

Instead of:

- 47 pings
- 12 replies
- 3 side conversations
- and one “wait, what did we decide?”

…you get a clean, readable handoff.

One beta user—a VP of Engineering at a 120-person company—put it better than we ever could:

> “We stopped having catch-up meetings. The catch-up just… happens.”

That’s the bar we’re aiming for: the catch-up should be built into the workflow, not bolted on as another calendar event.


## Decision logs that travel with the work

Every distributed team has the same fear: *somebody decided something while I was offline and now I’m out of sync.*

Relay tackles this head-on with **decision logs** that capture:

- The context behind the decision
- The alternatives considered
- Who was involved
- The final outcome (and what it changes)

This isn’t bureaucracy. It’s a memory system.

In practice, decision logs reduce the two most expensive forms of async confusion:

1. **Re-litigating old discussions** because the reasoning got lost
2. **Hidden constraints** that only exist in someone’s head—or in a buried Slack thread

When decisions keep their context, new team members onboard faster, tech leads sleep better, and “why did we do it this way?” becomes a question you can answer in under a minute.


## Video snippets with transcripts: updates you’ll actually watch

Loom is great—until it becomes an archive of 18-minute videos nobody has time to open.

Relay supports **2-minute max video snippets** for updates that benefit from tone, nuance, or a quick screen share. Every snippet:

- Auto-transcribes
- Auto-summarises
- Attaches to the relevant thread (so it’s not lost in a separate tool)

The constraint is the feature. Two minutes forces clarity. And the transcript means you can skim if you’re in a rush—or search later when you’re trying to remember *that one thing* that was said during launch week.

For engineering teams, that’s the difference between “We recorded an update” and “We created reusable knowledge.”


## Async standups + deep work protection: fewer interruptions, more momentum

Daily standups don’t need to be daily meetings.

With **async standups** in Relay, teammates share structured updates that compile into a team pulse: what’s moving, what’s blocked, what’s trending over time. Over weeks, you can see patterns—recurring bottlenecks, overloaded services, or a project that keeps “almost shipping.”

And because constant interruption is the enemy of maker time, Relay includes **deep work protection**:

- Configurable focus windows
- Batched notifications
- Summaries that keep you informed without pulling you out of flow

Beta teams reported an **average 35% reduction in meetings**. That’s not a promise we can make for every team (your calendar has a life of its own), but it’s a strong signal: when async collaboration is structured, a lot of meetings stop being necessary.

If that sounds like your world, you’re exactly who we built Relay for.


## How Relay fits your stack (without forcing a rip-and-replace)

Most teams don’t wake up one day and decide to “switch collaboration tools.” You have a mix that’s evolved over time: docs in one place, tickets in another, chat everywhere, and video updates scattered across links.

Relay is built to **connect the dots**, not to demand a clean slate.

In practice, teams use Relay as the async home for:

- **Project narratives** — what changed, why it changed, and what’s next
- **Decisions** — the “why,” alternatives, and ownership that get lost in chat
- **Status without status meetings** — standups that compile into a pulse over time
- **Lightweight video when it helps** — two-minute snippets with transcripts so updates stay searchable

And because the work still lives in code, issues, and docs, Relay threads are designed to stay grounded in reality: link to the PR, reference the ticket, attach the snippet, capture the decision. When someone in another timezone arrives, they can follow the chain in minutes—without asking five people to repeat yesterday.

If you’re already using tools like Notion, Linear, Slack, Loom, or Confluence, Relay should feel like the missing layer: **async context that travels with the work**.
