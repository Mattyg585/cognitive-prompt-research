# Relay is live: the async collaboration platform built for timezone collaboration

If your team spans three or more timezones, you’ve probably built a whole second job around *catching up*.

You wake up to a wall of notifications. You scroll. You try to reconstruct what happened while you were asleep. Then you book a “quick sync” to make sure you didn’t miss anything.

And somehow, despite all that effort, the most important details are still scattered across:

- a Slack thread that branched into five mini-debates,
- a doc that got updated without the reasoning,
- a ticket that says “LGTM” but not *why*,
- and a recording nobody will watch because everyone is already behind.

Distributed teams aren’t failing at communication. The tools we’ve all inherited are.

Today, **Relay** is launching publicly.

Relay is an **async collaboration platform** for distributed engineering teams—built for the reality that someone important is always offline. It’s Notion meets Linear meets Loom, but designed **async-first** from the ground up.

After 8 months of private beta with 200 teams, we’re confident in the thing engineering leaders actually care about:

**Fewer meetings that exist purely to fill a context gap—without losing alignment.**

## The hidden tax of distributed teams: missing context

Timezone collaboration breaks down in a predictable way:

1. Something changes (a decision, a design, an incident, a priority).
2. The people who are online discuss it.
3. Everyone else returns to the aftermath.

Most tools are excellent at step 2. They optimize for live conversation.

But step 3 is where distributed teams pay the tax.

When you open a thread and see 38 messages, you don’t need all 38. You need the narrative:

- what happened,
- what was decided,
- what’s still open,
- what changed since you last looked,
- and what you should do next.

“Async” is having the conversation in text.

**Async-first** is designing the experience around the person who shows up later.

---

## What Relay does differently

Relay combines the things engineering teams already use—threads, docs, issues, updates, quick video—into a system that makes context travel across timezones.

Not by stapling “async” onto existing patterns. By rebuilding the core loop: **discussion → decision → execution → recap**.

### Timezone-aware threads (catch up by understanding, not scrolling)

When you’re online, you contribute normally.

When you’re offline, Relay turns what happened into a structured recap: key moments, decisions, open questions, and the “why” that usually disappears.

In beta, this became our most-loved feature by a wide margin—mentioned in **80% of feedback**.

That makes sense. Most teams don’t need *more* conversation. They need conversation to be readable later.

Here’s what that looks like in practice:

It’s 9am in London. A teammate in California left a proposal thread overnight: a new cache layer, a few trade-offs, and a question about rollout risk.

In most tools, you reopen the tab, scan dozens of messages, and still end up asking: “So… what did we decide?”

In Relay, you land on a recap that tells you:

- the current state of the discussion,
- the key points raised,
- what’s been decided vs. what’s still open,
- and the next action (e.g., “@you: confirm rollout plan before end of day”).

The thread is still there. You can still read the details. But you don’t have to sacrifice an hour of maker time just to rehydrate context.

### Decision logs (the “why” stays attached to the “what”)

Distributed teams don’t just need decisions made—they need decisions to be legible.

Relay’s decision logs capture:

- the decision,
- the context,
- alternatives considered,
- who was involved,
- and follow-ups.

So a teammate in another timezone doesn’t have to reverse-engineer intent from a ticket title or re-open a settled debate.

This is also how you keep async from feeling slow.

When decisions are logged with the reasoning attached, people don’t “wait for a meeting” to move forward. They can read, understand, and execute—with fewer backchannels, fewer clarifying pings, and fewer accidental do-overs.

### Video snippets (short, searchable, and actually useful)

Sometimes text isn’t the fastest path to clarity.

Relay supports **2-minute max** video updates that auto-transcribe and auto-summarize, then attach to the relevant thread.

You get the human signal—tone, emphasis, nuance—without asking everyone to watch an hour-long recording.

### Async standups (a daily check-in that becomes a weekly pulse)

Relay’s async standups are structured, quick to fill out, and compiled into a team pulse that shows trends over time—what’s moving, what’s blocked, and what keeps recurring.

Less “status theater.” More signal.

### Deep work protection (built in, not bolted on)

Notification overload is meeting fatigue’s evil twin.

Relay lets teams define focus windows where notifications are batched and summarized. You still get the important stuff—you just don’t get it in 47 separate pings.

## “We already have Notion / Linear / Slack / Loom.” Why add another tool?

Totally fair. Tool fatigue is real.

Relay isn’t trying to replace everything. It’s trying to solve a specific failure mode those tools weren’t built for:

**When collaboration is real-time by default, distributed teams inherit a constant context gap.**

In practice:

- Slack is great for immediacy, but painful for durable catch-up.
- Docs capture outcomes, but rarely preserve the decision trail.
- Issue trackers handle execution, but not the messy middle where work gets shaped.
- Long recordings have nuance, but they don’t scale.

Relay is built for the space between—where the team needs context to survive a handoff across timezones.

If your team is mostly co-located (or mostly online together), you may not need this.

If your team is distributed across timezones and you’re paying for it in meetings and missed context, you probably do.
