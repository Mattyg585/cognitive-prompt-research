# Relay is public: Async collaboration that actually works across timezones

If you manage a team spread across three (or more) timezones, you already know the punchline: the meetings you schedule to “stay aligned” are usually the meetings that keep you from shipping.

You add a weekly sync because context keeps slipping. Then a standup because the weekly sync isn’t enough. Then a “quick catch-up” because the standup turned into a status dump. Before you know it, half your team’s day is dedicated to explaining what they did to people who were asleep when it happened.

Distributed teams don’t have a communication problem. They have a **context reconstruction** problem.

Today, we’re launching **Relay**, Threadline’s new **async collaboration platform** built specifically for distributed engineering teams. After 8 months of private beta with 200 teams, Relay is now publicly available.

The goal is simple: **stop paying the catch-up tax**. Relay helps teams stay aligned across timezones without drowning in notifications, writing essays, or recording hour-long videos nobody watches.

## The thing we kept seeing in beta

In tools built for real-time chat, “being caught up” means “being present.”

That’s fine when your team shares a working day. It’s a mess when your timeline looks like:

- Sydney ships changes while New York sleeps
- Berlin wakes up to 120 notifications
- Someone asks a question that was answered 6 hours ago… in a thread they didn’t see
- A meeting appears to fix the confusion the tool created

Teams told us the same story in different words: *“I can find the information… if I have time to hunt for it.”*

Relay is built around a different assumption: **the system should do the reconstruction work.**

## What Relay is (and isn’t)

Relay is a collaborative workspace where engineering teams coordinate work, decisions, and updates **async-first**.

It’s not trying to replace every tool you use. We love docs. We love good issue tracking. We’re not here to dunk on Slack.

We *are* here to say: none of those tools were designed for **timezone collaboration** from the ground up.

Relay combines the best parts of a workspace (clarity), an issue tracker (structure), and lightweight video (human context), then adds the missing layer: *a model of time*.

If you’re picturing “Notion meets Linear meets Loom,” you’re in the right neighborhood—just with one constraint that changes everything: **nobody is online at the same time.**

## The five pieces that make async actually work

### 1) Timezone-aware threads that read like a narrative, not a notification dump

When you’re asleep, your team keeps moving. The question is what you wake up to.

In Relay, threads don’t just stack messages in chronological order and call it a day. They **surface context based on when you’re online**, so you see what happened while you were away as a coherent narrative:

- what changed
- what decisions were made
- what needs your input now

The result: you spend less time scrolling and more time acting.

This became Relay’s most-loved feature in beta—mentioned in 80% of feedback. Which makes sense: if you fix how people catch up, you fix the meeting problem at the root.

### 2) Decision logs that capture the “why,” not just the outcome

Decisions are where distributed teams usually lose the thread.

Someone makes a call in a meeting. Someone else gets a one-line summary. A week later, a new teammate asks, “Why did we choose this approach?” and the answer is… vibes, memory, and a link to a 58-minute recording.

Relay’s decision logs are designed to be boring in the best way:

- **Context:** what problem were we solving?
- **Alternatives:** what else did we consider?
- **Participants:** who weighed in?
- **Outcome:** what did we decide?

That structure isn’t bureaucracy. It’s compassion for Future You (and for the teammate who wasn’t awake).

### 3) Two-minute video snippets with transcripts, summaries, and the right attachment point

Sometimes text isn’t the fastest way to communicate. A quick screen share can be perfect—*as long as it stays quick*.

Relay supports **2-minute max** video updates that:

- auto-transcribe
- auto-summarize
- attach to the relevant thread so the context stays together

This is the “keep it human” layer—without creating a library of long videos nobody has time to watch.

### 4) Async standups that turn daily updates into a real team pulse

A daily standup is useful when it’s a signal. It’s exhausting when it’s a ritual.

Relay’s async standups are structured enough to stay readable and consistent, and they compile into a **team pulse** that shows patterns over time:

- Where are we blocked repeatedly?
- Which projects keep slipping because of handoff gaps?
- Are we “busy” or actually progressing?

Instead of debating what happened yesterday, you can see it.

### 5) Deep work protection that batches notifications instead of weaponizing them

Maker time is fragile. A collaboration tool that interrupts you every five minutes isn’t collaboration—it’s sabotage.

Relay lets teams configure **focus windows** where notifications are batched and summarized. You can still stay informed, but you don’t lose your day to a thousand tiny context switches.

This is a small feature with a big philosophy behind it: the platform should protect the work, not just report on it.

It’s a fair question. Tool sprawl is real.

Relay isn’t “one more place to talk.” It’s a system for the specific failure mode distributed teams hit: **context gets lost between timezones**.

Most tools assume that if you didn’t see the message, you’ll scroll back and find it.

In practice, people:

Relay reduces that loop by making catch-up a first-class workflow:
