## The problem isn’t remote work. It’s sync-first tooling.

When a team spans multiple timezones, the cost of synchronous coordination goes up fast:

- **Meetings multiply** because they feel like the only reliable way to align.
- **Notifications become the workflow**, and the loudest thread wins.
- **Decisions get separated from their context**, so new joiners (or different timezones) inherit outcomes without reasoning.
- **Maker time shrinks** to the cracks between pings.

Some teams try to solve this with rules (“no meetings before 10,” “use threads,” “write better updates”), but rules can’t fix tools that weren’t built for timezone collaboration.

An async-first team needs software that assumes:

- People will read later.
- People will respond later.
- People will need context later.

Relay is built on those assumptions.

## What makes Relay different (and why it matters at 2 a.m.)

We didn’t set out to build a “better Slack” or “a more flexible Notion.” Those products are powerful—but they weren’t designed to make distributed work feel coherent *across time.*

Here’s what Relay does differently.

### 1) Timezone-aware threads: context that reads like a narrative

In most tools, you open a thread and see a backlog of messages—some relevant, some not, and all formatted like you’ve been staring at the screen for six hours.

**Relay threads are timezone-aware.** When you come online, conversations automatically surface the context *you missed*, structured as a narrative—so you can understand what happened while you were offline without scrolling through an entire notification wall.

Beta teams told us this was the moment Relay “clicked.” It’s also our most-loved feature:

- Mentioned in **80% of feedback**
- The #1 driver of “I can finally keep up”

### 2) Decision logs: outcomes with the “why” attached

A decision without context is just a future argument.

Relay makes decisions explicit and durable by capturing:

- the context (what problem were we solving?)
- the alternatives considered
- who was involved
- what we chose and why

So when someone in another timezone sees the decision, they don’t just see the outcome—they see the reasoning.

This is the difference between “we decided X” and “we decided X because Y, and we rejected Z due to A.”

That’s not bureaucracy. That’s kindness to your future teammates.

### 3) 2-minute video snippets (with transcripts) that don’t steal an hour

Video is great for nuance. It’s also famous for turning into 47-minute recordings that nobody watches.

Relay supports **2-minute max video updates** that:

- auto-transcribe
- auto-summarize
- attach to the relevant thread

You get the tone and clarity of video, with the skim-ability of text—and you don’t have to book a meeting to explain something that takes 90 seconds.

### 4) Async standups that compile into a team pulse

Daily updates are useful. Daily updates scattered across chat history are… less useful.

Relay’s **async standups** are structured and compile into a rolling team pulse, so you can:

- spot patterns over weeks (not just “today’s status”)
- see blockers early
- get signal without a daily call

This is especially helpful for engineering managers who need a high-level view without micromanaging.

### 5) Deep work protection: focus windows that actually protect focus

Distributed teams often “solve” timezones by staying partially online all the time.

That’s not collaboration. That’s exhaustion.

Relay includes configurable **focus windows** where notifications are batched and summarized. Instead of being interrupted 30 times, you get one clean update—then you decide when to engage.

It’s a small shift with a big outcome: more maker time, less cognitive thrash.
