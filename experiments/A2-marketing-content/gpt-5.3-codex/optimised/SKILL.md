# Content Creation Skill (Optimised)

Write marketing content that people actually want to read.

Work in three explicit phases:
1. **Understand** (investigate)
2. **Draft** (generate)
3. **Polish** (evaluate)

Do not collapse these phases.

---

## Phase 1 — Understand the Brief (Investigation)

You are investigating, not writing yet.

Before drafting anything, answer these questions in your own working process:

- What is the real problem the audience feels today?
- What belief should change by the end of the piece?
- What is genuinely distinctive about this product (not just feature inventory)?
- What proof is strongest (data, quote, outcomes)?
- What emotional movement should the reader experience from opening to CTA?

### Investigation lenses

Use lenses, not formula seeds:

- **Friction lens**: Where does current workflow break for this audience?
- **Cost lens**: What is this friction costing them (time, trust, focus, momentum)?
- **Difference lens**: What does this product do differently at workflow level?
- **Proof lens**: What evidence makes the claims credible?
- **Adoption lens**: What lowers risk for first trial?

Do not commit to final structure until these lenses are clear.

---

## Phase 2 — Draft for the Reader (Generation)

You are writing now.

Do **not** run SEO/checklist compliance in this phase. Focus on clarity, rhythm, and conviction.

### Core writing principles

- Write to one smart reader, not an abstract market segment.
- Open with tension or recognition, not a generic setup paragraph.
- Build momentum: each section should earn the next.
- Use concrete details over broad claims.
- Let evidence do persuasion work (metrics, quote, observed outcomes).
- Keep tone direct, confident, and empathetic.
- Use wit sparingly and only where natural.

### Blog-post guidance (for this experiment)

Your final draft should include:
- A clear headline
- A compelling opening
- A logical narrative arc with useful section breaks
- A close that naturally leads to CTA
- A meta description

Do not force a fixed number of sections. Let structure follow argument.

### Headline direction (principles, not formulas)

A strong headline should:
- create a meaningful curiosity gap,
- promise concrete value for this specific audience,
- stay honest to what the post actually delivers.

### CTA direction

Use one primary CTA aligned with reader state at the end.
For A2 runs, CTA must be: **Start free trial**.

---

## Phase 3 — Polish for Channel + SEO (Evaluation)

Now shift to evaluation mode.
Preserve voice while improving discoverability and scan quality.

### SEO checks

- Primary keyword: **async collaboration platform**
  - include naturally in headline, opening, at least one subheading, and meta description
- Secondary keywords:
  - distributed teams
  - timezone collaboration
  - async-first
- Meta description under 160 characters
- Suggest URL slug
- Ensure heading hierarchy is clear

### Readability and formatting checks

- Break dense paragraphs when needed
- Keep transitions clear
- Ensure scannable subheadings
- Preserve original voice and energy; do not flatten prose into checklist language

### Link suggestions

If relevant, suggest likely internal/external link placements (anchor text + placement note).

---

## Channel references (use when relevant)

### Social
Adapt to native platform rhythm; first line must stop the scroll.

### Email
Subject line must carry value on its own; one clear CTA only.

### Landing page
Lead benefit-first; remove friction near CTA.

### Press release
Use conventional news structure (dateline, lead with who/what/when/where/why).

### Case study
Use challenge → solution → measurable results arc.

---

## Guardrails

- Do not overfit to templates.
- Do not force equal coverage of every feature.
- Do not add numeric section targets unless the brief explicitly requires them.
- If voice and SEO conflict, resolve in favor of natural readability while preserving required keyword intent.
