# The Hidden Role No One Assigned You: Middleware for Human Context

If you manage a distributed engineering team, there is a job you're probably doing every day that is not in your title.

You're middleware.

You translate decisions between people who were never online at the same time. You stitch context from chat, docs, tickets, and video. You run "quick" meetings to make sure everyone is finally looking at the same map.

It feels normal because everyone around you is doing it too.

It is also waste.

Threadline built Relay to remove that waste, and it's launching publicly today as an async-first workspace for distributed engineering teams.

## Why catch-up meetings keep multiplying

Most collaboration stacks were built for synchronous workplaces and retrofitted for async behavior. So even disciplined teams hit the same pattern:

- notifications show *that* something happened, not *why* it matters,
- decisions are captured after the fact without full rationale,
- updates are available but expensive to consume,
- managers become translators by default.

Catch-up meetings are the safety valve for this design mismatch. They are not strategy. They are compensation.

## Relay is built around context transfer, not message volume

Relay treats timezone gaps as a first-class condition.

### Timezone-aware threads

When you log in, Relay presents what happened as a narrative handoff instead of a wall of pings. You can move from "what changed" to "what do I do next" quickly.

### Decision logs with reasoning intact

Outcomes include alternatives considered, context, and participants. That means people can execute decisions they did not witness live.

### Two-minute video snippets with transcript + summary

Video remains high-signal because it is short, searchable, and attached to the right thread. No more 47-minute context debt.

### Async standups as pulse, not paperwork

Daily updates aggregate into trend visibility. Leaders can spot recurring blockers before they harden into delivery risk.

### Focus windows that protect deep work

Notification batching keeps people informed while reducing interruption tax. Maker time stops being aspirational policy and becomes operational behavior.
