## What Makes Relay Different

### Timezone-aware threads

When you open a thread in Relay, you don't see a wall of messages in chronological order. You see a structured narrative — what happened, what was decided, what needs your attention — organised around when *you* came online.

The context that's relevant to your workday surfaces to the top. The tangents and already-resolved questions stay accessible but out of the way. You arrive in a conversation, not at the bottom of a scroll.

This was the most-mentioned feature in our beta feedback, by a wide margin. Eighty percent of users called it out specifically. That number surprised us a little — until we remembered that this is the thing they do every single morning, the thing that costs them the first hour of every day.

### Decision logs

Here's the quiet dysfunction at the center of most distributed teams: the outcome of a decision gets shared across timezones, but not the reasoning.

Your team in Berlin makes a call about the architecture at 10am their time. By the time your team in San Francisco is online, all they have is the conclusion. The context — the alternatives that were considered, the constraints that shaped the choice, who pushed back and why — lives in a Slack thread nobody bookmarked, or in the heads of the people who were there.

Decision logs in Relay capture that context at the point of decision, not after the fact. Anyone reading it later — in another timezone, or six months from now during onboarding — understands not just what was decided, but why. The reasoning doesn't evaporate.

### Video snippets that people actually watch

Loom was a good idea. Hour-long Loom recordings are a bad idea.

Relay caps video updates at two minutes. Every recording auto-transcribes, auto-summarises, and attaches directly to the relevant thread. You don't need to watch it if the summary covers what you need. You can skim the transcript. If something catches your eye, you can jump to that moment in the video.

The result: video updates that work like written updates — scannable, searchable, attached to context — with the human expressiveness that text can't always carry.

### Async standups that reveal patterns, not just status

The daily standup is the most persistent meeting in distributed engineering. It's also, often, the least useful. Fifteen people reciting status updates that could have been a message, at a time that's inconvenient for half the team.

Relay's async standups are structured prompts — what you shipped, what you're working on, what's in your way — that compile into a team pulse view. Individual updates are useful. But the view across weeks shows you what you can't see in any single day: where blockers keep recurring, who's been heads-down for a long stretch, what's stalling and why.

It turns your daily standup from a status ritual into a visibility layer.

### Deep work protection built in

Every notification that interrupts a two-hour focus block isn't just an interruption — it's a context switch that costs another 20 minutes to recover from. Distributed teams often have it worse here because colleagues across timezones don't know when you're in the middle of something.

Relay's focus windows batch and summarise notifications during configurable blocks of time. When you surface, you get a digest, not a deluge. The platform doesn't just accommodate deep work — it actively defends it.
