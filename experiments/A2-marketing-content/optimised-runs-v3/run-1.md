You know the routine. You open Slack to 93 unread messages. Fourteen of them matter. You can't tell which fourteen without reading all ninety-three. Three decisions were made while you slept, but you can only find the outcomes — not the reasoning. So you schedule a meeting. "Just to get everyone aligned."

That meeting is someone's 7 AM. Someone else's 10 PM. And its entire purpose is to reconstruct context that existed twelve hours ago but got buried under notification noise.

This is what passes for async collaboration in 2026: synchronous tools with the real-time expectation quietly removed. Slack without the instant reply. Notion docs that six people edit in six different timezones without ever seeing each other's changes in context. Loom recordings that are thirty minutes long because the creator couldn't tell what the viewer would already know.

It's not that distributed teams are bad at communicating. It's that every tool they use was designed for a room, then adapted for a map.

We built Relay because we wanted to stop adapting.

## What "async-first" actually means

Most tools bolt async features onto synchronous foundations. A threaded reply here. A "schedule send" there. Relay started from a different question: *What would you build if you assumed nobody was online at the same time?*

That assumption changes everything.

**Timezone-aware threads** are the clearest example. When you open a Relay thread, you don't see a chronological wall of messages. You see what happened while you were away, structured as a narrative — decisions highlighted, context surfaced, tangents collapsed. The platform knows when you were last active and constructs a coherent catch-up, not a scroll.

One VP of Engineering at a 120-person company put it simply after eight months in our beta:

> "We stopped having catch-up meetings. The catch-up just... happens."

That quote sounds small. It isn't. Catch-up meetings are the single largest time cost of distributed work. They exist because tools don't carry context across timezone boundaries. Remove the need for them and you don't just save calendar time — you give people back the parts of their day they'd been surrendering to coordination overhead.

## The features that exist because of that assumption

When you design for async from day one, you end up building things that synchronous tools never think to include.

**Decision logs** capture not just what was decided, but the alternatives considered, the reasoning, and who was involved. When a teammate in Singapore wakes up to find the API architecture changed, they don't just see the outcome — they understand why, and they can see that their earlier concerns were weighed. No meeting needed. No "can someone catch me up?" message sitting unanswered for eight hours.

**Video snippets** are capped at two minutes. That's a deliberate constraint. They auto-transcribe, auto-summarise, and attach to the thread they belong to. No more hunting through a shared drive for a forty-minute recording where the relevant part starts at minute twenty-six.

**Async standups** compile individual updates into a team pulse that reveals patterns across weeks, not just today's status. You stop asking "what's everyone working on?" and start seeing where momentum is building and where it's stalling — without interrupting anyone.

**Deep work protection** flips the default. Instead of notifications interrupting you unless you manually silence them, Relay batches and summarises notifications during your configured focus windows. The platform actively defends maker time rather than treating it as an individual discipline problem.

We think the gap comes down to something simple: people open Relay because it reduces their work rather than adding to it. There's no guilt-scroll through channels you've fallen behind on.
