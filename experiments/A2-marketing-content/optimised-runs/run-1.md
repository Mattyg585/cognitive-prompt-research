## The Problem Isn't Your Team. It's the Architecture.

Distributed teams are often told their communication problems are cultural. "Write better docs." "Be more intentional with updates." "Over-communicate." The advice isn't wrong, but it puts the burden on people to compensate for tools that weren't built for their situation.

Think about what happens when a decision gets made while half your team is asleep. In Slack, the people who were online have full context — the debate, the trade-offs, the moment someone changed their mind. Everyone else gets an outcome. Maybe someone posts a summary. Maybe they don't. Either way, the late-timezone folks are working with less context, making worse contributions, and feeling like second-class participants.

This isn't a people problem. It's an architecture problem. And that's what Relay solves.

## What Changes When You Build for Async First

We spent eight months in private beta with 200 teams across every timezone configuration you can imagine — São Paulo to Sydney, Lagos to London, Portland to Prague. Here's what we learned matters most.

### Context that travels with the conversation

Relay's timezone-aware threads automatically restructure themselves based on when you're reading. Instead of a raw chronological feed, you see what happened while you were away presented as a narrative: what was discussed, what shifted, what was decided, and what still needs your input. Eighty percent of beta teams called this their favourite feature, and the reason is simple — it eliminates the morning archaeology of digging through overnight messages.

### Decisions that explain themselves

Every decision in Relay captures three things: the context that prompted it, the alternatives that were considered, and the reasoning behind the choice. This isn't a process burden — it's a lightweight prompt that takes thirty seconds to fill out and saves hours of "wait, why did we decide this?" conversations later. People in other timezones don't just see what was decided. They understand why.

### Video that respects your time

We all know the Loom problem. Someone records a forty-five-minute walkthrough. Nobody watches it. Relay caps video snippets at two minutes, auto-transcribes them, generates a summary, and attaches everything to the relevant thread. You can watch, read, or skim — whatever fits your moment. The information gets absorbed because it's sized for async consumption, not sync presentation.

### Deep work that's actually protected

Most tools treat "Do Not Disturb" as a blunt instrument — everything gets blocked, and when you come back, the flood is worse. Relay's focus windows are smarter. Notifications are batched and summarised during your protected hours, so when you emerge from a three-hour coding session, you get a structured briefing instead of a wall of pings. The platform actively protects maker time because we believe the best async collaboration happens when people have uninterrupted space to do the work they're collaborating on.

## Why Now

Distributed teams aren't a trend. The percentage of engineering teams spanning three or more timezones has been climbing every year, and the tools haven't kept up. Slack was built for real-time chat in an office. Notion was built for documentation. Linear was built for project tracking. They're all good at what they do. But none of them were designed around the fundamental constraint of timezone-distributed collaboration: that the people who need to work together are rarely online at the same time.

Relay doesn't replace your entire stack. It sits at the centre of it — the layer where context, decisions, and updates live so they can travel intact across timezones, across tools, and across the gaps in your team's waking hours.
