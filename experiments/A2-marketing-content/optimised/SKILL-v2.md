# Content Creation Skill

Write marketing content that earns the reader's attention. Work in two phases: find what's worth saying, then say it. Compliance comes after the piece exists.

---

## Phase 1: Investigate

Before any writing begins, read the brief as someone who is genuinely curious about this product and this reader.

Your investigation has two threads:

**Thread 1: The product**
What is actually happening here? Not the feature list — the change in how someone's day works when this exists versus when it doesn't. What problem was someone tolerating before? What do they stop doing? What becomes possible? Follow the surprising details: a beta metric that defies the industry norm, a user quote that captures something human, a feature whose name sounds boring but whose effect is not.

**Thread 2: The reader**
Who is the specific person reading this, right now, on the day it runs? What does their morning look like? What have they already tried? What would make them feel understood rather than marketed to? What emotional state are they in — frustration, skepticism, cautious hope? Where do they need to arrive by the end of the piece?

Let these threads run until something clicks — until you can articulate, in one sentence, what makes this particular piece worth reading over every other piece on this topic. That sentence is your angle. It may not be in the brief explicitly. It emerges from the exploration.

Do not move to Phase 2 until you have that angle. If you have several candidate angles, choose the one that most directly addresses the reader's emotional state and the product's most surprising truth. Commit to one.

---

## Phase 2: Write

You are writing now. The SEO requirements, character counts, and channel checklists are not your concern in this phase — those come in Phase 3. Your concern is the reader.

Write from the angle you found in Phase 1. Let it govern every decision: what goes in, what stays out, which sections exist, how long each runs, where the data lands, how it ends.

### What makes the writing work

**The opening has one job.** Make them read the second sentence. This is not a hook formula — it is whatever creates the specific tension that makes this reader, with this problem, unable to stop. A scenario they recognize. A claim they want to verify. A question they've been asking. A piece of data that doesn't match what they assumed. Generic openings kill good pieces. The opening should be written last or rewritten last, once you know what the piece actually became.

**Write for one person, not a segment.** "You" is your most powerful word. The reader should feel like you understand their specific situation, not that you surveyed their demographic. One person with a specific problem, reading on a specific day.

**Earn every section.** If a section exists because structure demands it rather than because the reader needs it at that moment, cut it or rethink the transition. The reader should feel pulled forward through the piece, not pushed through a template.

**Let evidence land.** When you have a strong metric, a real quote, or a concrete outcome — let it hit. Don't bury it in a list. Don't dilute it with hedging. Put it where it has maximum impact and let it sit. One vivid number does more than three paragraphs of claims.

**Close with consequence, not summary.** The reader should arrive at the end already wanting to act — not because you told them to, but because the piece built the case. The CTA should feel like opening a door the reader is already standing at, not like an interruption at the end of an article.

**Structure follows the angle.** Different angles need different shapes. A problem-solution story has different beats than a credibility piece. A vision argument structures differently than a user story. The structure that serves the angle is correct; the generic template is not. Length varies with content — let depth match the complexity of what you're saying.

### Content type guidance

**Blog posts**: The headline should capture the piece's actual argument or reframe, not just its topic. A headline that creates a gap — between what the reader knows and what they want to know — earns the click. Write the headline once you know what the piece became. Include a meta description that complements (not duplicates) the headline.

**Social media**: Each platform has its own native rhythm. The hook is everything — if the first line doesn't earn the scroll, nothing else matters. LinkedIn rewards real lessons and professional vulnerability. Twitter/X rewards compression and strong opening words. Write for the platform's actual readership, not for a generic "social" format.

**Email newsletters**: The subject line is the headline. It either creates the open or doesn't. Inside: one idea, one CTA. If the reader has to choose between three things, they choose nothing. Make the key insight scannable — bold the sentence that matters in each section.

**Landing pages**: Lead with the benefit the reader cares about most, not the feature you're most proud of. Every section should answer the reader's unspoken "so what?" Handle objections before asking for commitment. Make the next step obvious and the risk feel low.

**Press releases**: Follow the conventions — newsrooms expect them. Lead paragraph covers who, what, when, where, why. Factual headline. Supporting details and quotes in the body. Boilerplate and media contact at the close.

**Case studies**: Challenge-solution-results arc. Open with a snapshot (customer, industry, result) so the reader can immediately assess relevance. Quantify outcomes specifically. Include a quote that captures the human experience, not just the metric.

## Phase 3: Polish for the Channel

The draft exists. Now shift posture: you are an editor, not a writer. Review the piece against these guidelines and adjust what needs adjusting — without flattening the voice you built in Phase 2.

### SEO review

The primary keyword should appear naturally in the headline, the opening paragraph, at least one subheading, the meta description, and the URL slug. If placing the keyword makes a sentence awkward, the sentence wins — forced placement hurts both readability and SEO. Secondary keywords belong in body copy and subheadings where they fit without effort.

Meta description: under 160 characters, complements the headline, makes the reader want to click.
Title tag: under 60 characters where possible for full display in search results.
Heading hierarchy: one H1, descriptive H2s and H3s.
Links: note where internal links to related content and external links to authoritative sources would add value.

### Channel formatting check

**Blog**: Are paragraphs scannable? Are subheadings descriptive enough to navigate by skimming? Is there enough visual variety to keep the eye moving?
**Social**: Does it respect the platform's native constraints? Does the hook work before any fold?
**Email**: Does the subject line work on its own? One clear CTA? Scannable on mobile?
**Landing pages**: Is the primary CTA above the fold? Are trust signals near the action points?

### Voice preservation check

Read the piece aloud (mentally). Does it sound like a person who believed what they were writing, or like competent copy that was produced by committee? If the Phase 2 voice got flattened during editing — go back. The voice was the point. Restore it, then find a lighter way to satisfy the requirement that erased it.
