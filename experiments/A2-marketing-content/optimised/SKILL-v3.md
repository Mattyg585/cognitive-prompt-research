# Content Creation Skill

Write marketing content that earns the reader's attention.

---

## Before you write anything

Read the brief as a writer, not as someone filling a template. You are looking for the thing that makes this specific piece worth reading — not the feature list, but the change. What was the person tolerating before this product existed? What do they stop having to do? What would make them feel understood rather than marketed to?

Follow what surprises you. A beta metric that defies the industry norm, a user quote that sounds human rather than rehearsed, a detail whose implications aren't in the brief explicitly — these are where the piece lives. The angle comes from the intersection of what's surprising about the product and what the reader is actually feeling. Let the investigation run until you can name it in one sentence.

The structure, the length, the voice — all of these follow from what you find. Different briefs produce different shapes.

---

## Writing for the reader

Write from what you found. The angle you arrived at governs every decision: what goes in, what stays out, which sections exist, how long each runs, where evidence lands, how the piece ends.

**The opening.** Its only job is to make the reader continue. Not a formula — whatever creates the specific tension that makes this particular reader, with this particular problem, unable to stop. A scenario they recognize. A claim they want to verify. A question they've been asking. A piece of data that upends an assumption. Use what the material gave you.

**Write to one person.** "You" is more powerful than "teams" or "organizations." The reader should feel understood, not segmented.

**Earn every section.** If a section exists because structure demands it rather than because the reader needs it at that moment, cut it.

**Let evidence land.** A strong metric, a real quote, a specific outcome — don't bury it in a list or dilute it with hedging. Put it where it has maximum impact and let it sit.

**Close with consequence.** The reader should arrive at the call to action already wanting to act, because the piece built the case. The CTA opens a door they're already standing at.

**Write the headline last.** Once you know what the piece actually became — its real argument, its reframe — write the headline that captures that. Not the topic. The argument.

**Let structure follow the angle.** There is no correct section count. A problem-solution story has different beats than a credibility piece. Length varies with what you're saying.

### Channel guidance

**Blog posts**: The headline earns the click. The opening earns the scroll. Each section earns the next. Include a meta description that complements the headline without duplicating it.

**Social media**: The hook is everything — if the first line doesn't earn the scroll, nothing else matters. Each platform has its native rhythm. LinkedIn rewards professional storytelling and real lessons. Twitter/X rewards compression and strong opening words. Write for the platform's actual readership.

**Email newsletters**: The subject line is the headline. It either creates the open or doesn't. Inside: one idea, one action. Make the key insight findable for someone skimming.

**Landing pages**: Lead with the benefit the reader cares about most. Every section answers the reader's unspoken "so what?" Handle objections before asking for commitment. Make the next step obvious and the risk feel low.

**Press releases**: Follow the conventions — newsrooms expect them. Lead paragraph covers who, what, when, where, why. Factual headline. Supporting details and quotes in the body. Boilerplate and media contact at the close.

**Case studies**: Challenge-solution-results arc. Open with a snapshot so the reader can immediately assess relevance. Quantify outcomes. Include a quote that captures the human experience, not just the metric.

## Once the draft exists

The piece is written. Now read it as an editor, not a writer. The voice you built is the piece's most important asset — your job here is to check compliance without flattening it. If satisfying a requirement erases something the voice was doing, find a lighter way.

**SEO**: The primary keyword should appear in the headline, the opening paragraph, at least one subheading, the meta description, and the URL slug — where it fits naturally. If placing it makes a sentence read awkwardly, the sentence wins. Secondary keywords belong where they arise without effort.

- Meta description: under 160 characters, complements the headline, makes the reader want to click
- Title tag: under 60 characters where possible
- Heading hierarchy: one H1, descriptive H2s and H3s
- Links: flag where internal and external links would add value

**Formatting by channel**:
- Blog: paragraphs scannable, subheadings descriptive enough to navigate by skimming, enough visual variety to keep the eye moving
- Social: hook lands before any fold or cut, platform length respected
- Email: subject line works on its own, one clear CTA, scannable on mobile
- Landing pages: primary CTA above the fold, trust signals near action points

**Voice check**: Read the piece once more as a reader. Does it sound like a person wrote it with conviction, or like competent copy produced by committee? If the editorial pass flattened the voice — go back and restore it, then find a lighter way to satisfy whatever requirement caused the damage.
