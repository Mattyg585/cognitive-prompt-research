# Content Creation Skill

Create marketing content that connects with readers. Work in three phases: understand the brief, write for the reader, then polish for the channel.

---

## Phase 1: Understand the Brief

Before writing anything, explore what you're working with. You are investigating, not writing yet. Don't commit to a structure, headline, or angle until you understand what this piece needs to do.

Ask yourself:
- **What's genuinely interesting here?** What about this product, announcement, or story would make someone stop scrolling? Not the feature list — the thing that changes how someone works or thinks.
- **Who is reading this, and what do they care about?** What's their current pain? What have they tried? What would make them feel understood?
- **What's the emotional arc?** Where does the reader start (skeptical, curious, frustrated, unaware) and where should they end (convinced, excited, informed, ready to act)?
- **What's the angle?** The specific take that makes this piece worth reading over every other piece on the same topic. A product launch can be told as a problem-solution story, a vision piece, a credibility play, a human story. Which one fits *this* product and *this* audience?

Let this exploration shape everything that follows — the structure, the voice, the length, the headline. Different content warrants different shapes.

---

## Phase 2: Write for the Reader

You are writing now, not evaluating. Focus on voice, flow, and the reader's experience. Ignore SEO placement, character counts, and formatting checklists — those come in Phase 3.

### What makes content work (across all channels)

- **Open with tension.** The reader needs a reason to keep going in the first sentence. Create a gap between what they know and what they want to know. This can be a surprising fact, a question that reframes their situation, a bold claim they'll want to verify, or a scenario they recognize.
- **Earn every paragraph.** Each paragraph should make the reader want the next one. If a section exists because the template says so rather than because the reader needs it, cut it.
- **Write for one reader, not an audience.** "You" is more powerful than "teams" or "organizations." The reader should feel like you understand their specific situation.
- **Let evidence do the persuading.** Data, quotes, specific outcomes, and concrete examples are more convincing than adjectives. "73% daily active usage" hits harder than "incredibly high engagement."
- **Match the voice to the brand and audience.** Read the brief's voice guidance. A developer tool and a consumer wellness brand don't sound the same — and shouldn't.
- **End with momentum, not summary.** The reader should feel pulled toward action, not like they just finished a textbook chapter. The close should feel like a natural consequence of everything before it, not a bolted-on recap.

### Content type guidance

#### Blog Posts
The structure should serve the story, not the other way around. A blog post might need two deep sections or six short ones — let the content dictate the shape. What matters: a headline that earns the click, an opening that earns the scroll, sections that each carry their weight, and a close that moves the reader to act. Include a meta description that complements the headline.

#### Social Media
Each platform has its own rhythm. LinkedIn rewards professional storytelling and real lessons. Twitter/X rewards compression and strong opening words. Instagram is visual-first with storytelling captions. Facebook drives engagement through questions and conversation. Write for the platform's native voice, not for a generic "social media" format. The hook is everything — if the first line doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters.

#### Email Newsletters
The subject line is the headline of the email — it determines whether anything else gets read. It should create curiosity or promise specific value without revealing everything. Inside, make the content scannable — bold the key insight in each section so someone skimming still gets the point. One clear action per email. If you're asking for three things, you're asking for nothing.

#### Landing Pages
Lead with the benefit the reader cares about most, not the feature you're most proud of. Every section should answer the reader's unspoken "so what?" Speak directly to the reader's situation. Handle objections before asking for commitment. Reduce friction — make the next step obvious and low-risk.

#### Press Releases
Press releases have genuine conventions — newsrooms expect a specific format. Lead with the news: who, what, when, where, why in the first paragraph. Follow with supporting details and quotes. Close with the company boilerplate and media contact. Keep the headline factual and newsworthy.

#### Case Studies
Follow the challenge-solution-results arc. Open with a snapshot (customer, industry, key result) so the reader can decide immediately if this is relevant to them. Quantify results wherever possible. Include a customer quote that captures the human experience, not just the metric. Close with a path to learn more.

### Headlines and hooks

What makes a headline work:
- It creates a **gap** — between what the reader knows and what they want to know
- It promises **specific value** for the reader's time — not vague ("improve your workflow") but concrete ("stop having catch-up meetings")
- It matches the reader's **mental state** — if they're frustrated, acknowledge it; if they're curious, deepen the curiosity; if they're skeptical, lead with evidence
- It's **honest** — it delivers on what it promises. Clickbait erodes trust.

What makes an opening hook:
- It earns the next sentence. That's the only job.
- It connects to something the reader already feels, wonders, or has experienced — then adds a twist, a reframe, or new information that makes them need to keep reading.

### Calls to action

A CTA works when it feels like the natural next step, not a sales interruption. The reader should arrive at the CTA already wanting to act — the content built the case, and the CTA just opens the door.

- Be specific about what happens next ("Start your free trial" tells me more than "Get started")
- Reduce risk where genuine ("No credit card required" removes a real objection)
- Place the CTA where the reader's motivation is highest — after you've established value, not before
- One primary CTA per piece. If the reader has to choose between three actions, they'll choose none.

## Phase 3: Polish for the Channel

Now shift to evaluation mode. The draft exists — review it against these channel and SEO guidelines. Adjust what needs adjusting without flattening the voice you established in Phase 2.

### SEO review

- **Primary keyword**: should appear naturally in the headline, the opening paragraph, at least one subheading, the meta description, and the URL slug. If placing the keyword makes a sentence awkward, the sentence wins — forced placement hurts readability and SEO alike.
- **Secondary keywords**: weave into body copy and subheadings where they fit naturally.
- **Meta description**: keep it under 160 characters. It should complement the headline, not repeat it. Make the reader want to click.
- **Title tag**: keep it under 60 characters where possible for full display in search results.
- **Heading hierarchy**: one H1 per page, descriptive H2s and H3s. These help readers scan and help search engines understand structure.
- **Links**: include relevant internal links to related content and external links to authoritative sources where they add value.
- **Depth**: search engines reward comprehensive coverage. If you've treated the topic superficially, expand. If you've been thorough, this is already handled.

### Channel formatting check

- **Blog**: are paragraphs short enough to scan? Are subheadings descriptive enough to navigate by? Is there enough visual variety (lists, quotes, data) to keep the eye moving?
- **Social media**: does it respect the platform's native constraints? LinkedIn posts should front-load value before the fold. Twitter/X posts should be punchy and complete. Instagram captions should complement the visual.
- **Email**: does the subject line work on its own? Is there one clear CTA? Is the layout scannable on mobile?
- **Landing pages**: is the primary CTA above the fold? Are trust signals near the action points? Is the form as short as possible?

### Readability and voice check

- Read the piece aloud (mentally). Does it sound like a person or a template? If it sounds mechanical, the Phase 2 voice got flattened — go back and restore it.
- Is the reading level appropriate for the audience? Technical audiences tolerate complexity; broad audiences need simpler language.
- Is it in active voice? Passive voice weakens marketing copy.
