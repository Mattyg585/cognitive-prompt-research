PRODUCT INSIGHT

Relay doesn't add another tool to the stack — it replaces the duct-tape system distributed teams already have. Right now, async teams cobble together Slack (chat), Notion (docs), Linear (tickets), Loom (video), and calendar invites (catch-up meetings) into something that barely works. Every handoff between those tools loses context. Relay's actual value proposition is eliminating context loss across timezones. Not "better collaboration" — fewer moments where someone wakes up confused, behind, or forced into a meeting just to understand what happened while they slept.

The decision log feature reveals Threadline's real theory of the problem: distributed teams don't fail because communication is slow. They fail because decisions become opaque. Someone in Singapore sees the outcome of a discussion that happened in Berlin but has no idea why the team chose option B over option A. So they either accept it passively (resentment, disengagement) or relitigate it (wasted cycles, friction). Decision logs attack the trust problem that erodes distributed teams over months.

Deep work protection is the feature that turns Relay from a collaboration tool into something closer to a work philosophy. Most tools want more engagement. Relay actively tells you to stop checking it. That's a genuinely unusual product stance.

AUDIENCE UNDERSTANDING

Engineering managers and tech leads at 50-500 person companies, distributed across 3+ timezones. These are people who became managers because they were good engineers — and now spend their days in meetings they find pointless, triaging notifications across four apps, and writing "context summaries" in Slack that nobody reads.

They've tried everything. Async Slack channels with threading discipline. Loom recordings. Notion wikis with meeting notes. "No meeting Wednesdays." Every solution works for about two weeks before the team drifts back to synchronous defaults, because the tools weren't designed for async — they just tolerate it.

The specific frustration: they know async should work. They've read the blog posts, they believe the philosophy. But the tooling fights them. The gap between "we're an async-first team" and the lived reality — 7am meetings to overlap with Europe, catching up on 200 Slack messages, watching 45-minute Loom recordings at 2x — is exhausting and demoralizing.

What would make them pay attention: evidence that someone built the thing they've been wishing existed. Not another tool that claims to support async. A tool that was designed from scratch assuming nobody is in the same room, ever.

SURPRISING ELEMENTS

73% daily active usage vs. ~40% industry average. This is the single most important number in the brief. Collaboration tools live and die by adoption — most get purchased by managers and ignored by teams. 73% DAU means the engineers themselves are choosing to use it. That's not a management mandate; that's product-market fit showing up in behavior.

35% meeting reduction is concrete but the framing matters. Engineering teams at this size probably run 10-15 hours of meetings per week per person. 35% reduction is 3.5-5 hours back. That's nearly a full day of deep work recovered. Per person. Per week.

80% of feedback mentioning timezone-aware threads as most-loved. Features that get 80% unprompted mention in feedback are rare. This isn't "nice to have" — it's the reason people stay.

The 2-minute max on video snippets is a quiet design decision that reveals deep product thinking. Loom's problem isn't video — it's that people record 20-minute rambles. A hard cap forces the sender to think before recording. The constraint is the feature.

NPS of 67 is excellent for a B2B SaaS tool (above 50 is considered strong). Worth noting but less narratively interesting than the behavioral metrics.

EMOTIONAL TERRITORY

Starts: Resigned exhaustion. "This is just how distributed work is." They've normalized the pain — the early morning meetings, the notification anxiety, the sense of always being slightly behind. There's also a layer of guilt: they chose distributed/remote work, they advocate for it, but privately they wonder if it actually works at scale.

Ends: Recognition and relief. "Someone finally built this." The emotion isn't excitement about features — it's the feeling of being deeply understood. Relay's design choices mirror exactly what these managers have been jury-rigging manually. Seeing it in a product validates their instinct that async can work, that the problem was tooling, not the model.

The action driver is a combination of relief ("I don't have to keep duct-taping this together") and urgency ("my team is suffering from the current setup and there's now an actual solution").

POTENTIAL ANGLES

Problem-is-the-tools narrative. "You've tried async. It didn't stick. But you were blaming the method when you should have been blaming the tools." This works because the audience already believes in async — they just think they're bad at it. Reframing the failure from personal/team to structural is both true and emotionally powerful.

The catch-up meeting obituary. Anchor on the beta quote: "We stopped having catch-up meetings. The catch-up just... happens." Build the piece around the death of the most hated meeting type. Specific, relatable, and the beta data backs it up. Works because every engineering manager has a catch-up meeting they resent.

The invisible work story. Distributed engineering managers do enormous invisible labor — writing summaries, creating context documents, scheduling across timezones, being the "timezone bridge." Relay automates the invisible work. This angle resonates personally and elevates the product from tool to recognition of their effort.

Contrarian take on engagement. "We built a collaboration tool that tells you to stop collaborating." The deep work protection feature is the hook. In a market where every tool wants more screen time, Relay actively pushes users away. Counter-positioning against the attention economy lands differently than feature comparisons.

Data-led credibility play. Lead with the 73% DAU, 35% meeting reduction, and NPS 67. Let the numbers argue. Works for the analytical segment of the audience (engineering leaders who distrust marketing prose). Risk: feels like every other SaaS launch post unless the framing is strong.

VOICE NOTES

The brief describes "smart friend, not board presentation." In practice this means: short sentences, occasional fragments, specificity over abstraction. "35% fewer meetings" not "significant reduction in synchronous touchpoints."

Confident-but-not-arrogant means stating what Relay does without qualifying or hedging, but never positioning against competitors by name. "Nobody built this async-first" is fine. "Unlike Slack, which..." is not.

The empathy note is the most important voice instruction. The reader's pain is real and ongoing. Any sentence that sounds like it's trivializing their current situation ("Tired of too many meetings?") will lose them. The voice should convey: we lived this too, we built this because we needed it.

Wit should show up in structure and phrasing, not in jokes. A well-placed short sentence after a longer one. A section title that reframes something. Not puns, not cleverness for its own sake.

Avoid: buzzwords (revolutionize, seamless, game-changer), corporate hedging (helps enable, aims to), exclamation marks, anything that sounds like it was written by a marketing team that doesn't use the product.
