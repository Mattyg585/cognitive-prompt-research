PRODUCT INSIGHT

Relay doesn't add another tool to the distributed team's stack — it replaces the invisible labour of staying in sync across timezones. The real product isn't threads or video snippets. It's the elimination of "catch-up work" — the hours spent every morning reconstructing what happened while you slept. Right now, distributed engineers piece together context from Slack scrollback, missed meeting recordings, and stale docs. Relay replaces that reconstruction work with automatic narrative. The timezone-aware threading means context arrives already structured, not as raw chronological exhaust.

The deeper shift: Relay makes async the default mode rather than a degraded fallback. Most tools treat async as "what happens when you can't be synchronous." Relay treats synchronous communication as the exception — something you opt into when async isn't sufficient. That inversion is the real product thesis.

The decision log feature is quietly radical. Distributed teams don't just miss decisions — they miss the reasoning. Knowing "we chose Postgres" is useless without knowing "we considered DynamoDB for cost reasons but rejected it because of query complexity." Decision logs close the legitimacy gap that makes timezone-disadvantaged team members feel like passengers rather than participants.

AUDIENCE UNDERSTANDING

Engineering managers and tech leads at 50-500 person companies distributed across 3+ timezones. These are people who already know distributed work is hard. They aren't looking for a revelation — they're looking for relief.

Their current reality: They run too many meetings because async communication keeps failing. They've tried making Slack work ("use threads!" "don't post in #general!"), they've tried Notion for documentation, they've tried Loom for async video. Each tool solves one piece. None of them talk to each other. The meta-work of keeping everything connected falls on the engineering manager, who becomes a human router.

What they've tried: Slack conventions, Notion wikis, Loom libraries, "no-meeting Wednesdays," elaborate standup bots. All partial fixes that create their own overhead. The exhaustion isn't from any single tool — it's from the integration burden across all of them.

What would make them pay attention: Proof that async can actually replace meetings, not just supplement them. The 35% meeting reduction is the number that earns a second look. But what earns trust is specificity — the VP Eng quote about catch-up meetings simply disappearing reads as genuine because it describes a concrete behaviour change, not a vague improvement.

These people are allergic to hype. They've been sold "the future of work" fifteen times. They want to see the mechanism — how exactly does this reduce meetings? — not just the claim.

SURPRISING ELEMENTS

73% daily active usage vs. industry average of ~40%. This is the standout number. Collaboration tools have a notorious adoption cliff — teams sign up, use them for two weeks, and revert to Slack and email. Nearly double the industry average suggests Relay solved the adoption problem, not just the feature problem. This implies the product is genuinely easier to use within existing workflows than to ignore.

NPS of 67. For a B2B collaboration tool in beta, this is exceptional. Slack's NPS hovers around 30-40. This number says users aren't just tolerating the tool — they're actively recommending it.

35% meeting reduction. Meeting reduction claims are common but usually vague or self-reported without context. The fact that this comes from 200 beta teams gives it weight. The interesting question: which meetings disappeared? Likely status meetings and catch-up syncs — the meetings nobody wanted but everyone felt they needed.

80% of feedback mentions timezone-aware threads. When a single feature dominates feedback this heavily, it's the product. Everything else is supporting infrastructure. This tells you the core value proposition lives in one place: "you wake up and already know what happened."

2-minute video cap. Counterintuitive design constraint. Loom lets you record for as long as you want, which means most Loom videos are too long and never get watched. A hard two-minute limit forces clarity and makes watching feel cheap rather than costly. The constraint is the feature.

EMOTIONAL TERRITORY

Starting point: Tired resignation. These managers have accepted that distributed work means more meetings, more Slack noise, more context loss. They've optimized around the pain rather than expecting it to go away. There's a learned helplessness — "this is just what distributed teams deal with."

Midpoint: Recognition. The content should name their specific frustrations precisely enough that they feel understood. Not "collaboration is hard" — but "you spend the first 45 minutes of every day reading Slack threads backwards trying to figure out what your Taipei team decided about the API migration." Specificity creates trust.

Endpoint: Pragmatic hope. Not excitement — these people are too experienced for that. The feeling is: "this might actually work, and the risk of trying is low." The free tier makes trying frictionless. The beta results make hoping reasonable. The emotional destination is permission to believe this could be different, backed by evidence.

The action trigger is relief, not aspiration. They don't need to be inspired about the future of work. They need to believe tomorrow morning could feel less chaotic than today.

POTENTIAL ANGLES

**The "catch-up meeting is dead" narrative.** Lead with the meeting reduction data and the VP Eng quote. Frame the post around a specific ritual everyone hates — the Monday catch-up meeting, the timezone-overlap sync — and show how it evaporates. Works because it's concrete, measurable, and emotionally resonant. Every engineering manager reading this has a meeting they wish they could kill.

**The "async was always broken because it was bolted on" argument.** A more structural angle. The thesis: every tool you use for async work was originally designed for synchronous use and adapted. Slack is chat (synchronous) with threads (afterthought). Notion is a doc editor (real-time) with async comments (afterthought). Relay was built async-first, which changes everything from the data model up. Works for the architecturally-minded audience who think in systems. Risks feeling abstract if not grounded quickly.

**The "invisible labour" angle.** Name the work nobody talks about: the human routing, the context reconstruction, the "let me catch you up" conversations. Quantify it if possible — "your engineering managers spend X hours per week being human middleware." Position Relay as making that labour visible and then eliminating it. Works because it validates a frustration that doesn't have a name yet.

**The data-credibility play.** Lead with the numbers: 200 teams, 73% DAU, 67 NPS, 35% fewer meetings. Let the data do the persuading. Brief interpretation, then show the product. Works for the skeptical audience that dismisses marketing prose but respects metrics. Risk: can feel cold or boastful if not handled with the right tone.

**The "your Taipei team shouldn't be second-class citizens" angle.** Frame timezone inequality as the real problem. Some team members always join the meeting at midnight. Some always get decisions made without them. Relay doesn't just reduce meetings — it equalises participation across timezones. Works because it connects to a values-level concern (fairness, inclusion) that engineering leaders genuinely care about but rarely see addressed by tools.

VOICE NOTES

The brief calls for "smart friend" register — technically literate, zero jargon, no corporate hedging. This means: short sentences. Concrete examples over abstract claims. "You know that meeting" rather than "many teams experience."

Confident but not comparative. The brief explicitly says don't dunk on Slack. The voice should imply the comparison through describing what Relay does, not through naming what competitors don't. "Conversations surface context based on your timezone" is better than "unlike Slack, which buries everything in scrollback."

Empathy should be specific, not general. "Distributed teams struggle" is generic. "You opened Slack at 7am and had 200 unread messages across 14 channels" is specific. The voice earns trust through precision.

Wit should be structural, not decorative. A clever section header or an unexpected analogy works. A joke in parentheses feels forced. The beta user quote ("The catch-up just... happens") has exactly the right tone — understated, slightly surprised, real.

Avoid: "revolutionize," "game-changer," "seamless," "cutting-edge," "the future of work." This audience pattern-matches on these words and disengages.

Lean into: Plain statements of fact. Specific numbers. Named behaviours ("the Monday morning Slack archaeology"). Short paragraphs. The occasional one-sentence paragraph for emphasis.
