PRODUCT INSIGHT

Relay doesn't add another tool to the stack — it replaces the duct-tape system distributed teams have built out of four or five separate products. The real impact isn't any single feature. It's that people in late timezones stop being second-class participants. Right now, async teams run on a hidden penalty: if you weren't in the room (or the Slack thread) when it happened, you piece together what occurred from fragments. Relay makes "catching up" disappear as a task. The VP Eng quote nails it — catch-up meetings didn't get shorter, they ceased to exist. The product's deepest value proposition is restoring equal participation regardless of when you work.

The decision log feature is quietly the most radical thing here. Most collaboration tools treat decisions as outputs. Relay treats them as artefacts with full reasoning attached. This solves a specific and deeply felt frustration: arriving at work to find something was decided, with no way to understand why, and no way to constructively push back without sounding like you're relitigating. That dynamic poisons distributed culture. Relay defuses it structurally.

Deep work protection is the other significant move. Most tools profit from engagement — more time in the tool, more notifications, more dopamine loops. Relay actively pushes people out of it during focus time. That's a genuine philosophical inversion of how collaboration software works, and it signals trustworthiness to an audience that's been burned by attention-harvesting products.


AUDIENCE UNDERSTANDING

Engineering managers and tech leads at 50-500 person distributed companies across 3+ timezones. These people are drowning in coordination overhead. Their daily experience: wake up to 200+ Slack messages, spend the first 90 minutes of the day reconstructing what happened overnight, attend 2-3 "sync" meetings that exist only because async communication is broken, then try to do actual strategic work in whatever time is left.

They've tried everything. They've set up Notion wikis that nobody updates. They've recorded Loom videos that sit unwatched. They've built elaborate Slack channel structures that still collapse into noise. They've tried "async Mondays" and "no-meeting Wednesdays" — treating the symptom with calendar policies instead of fixing the tooling.

What they're frustrated by isn't any single tool — it's the cognitive overhead of stitching five tools together and still losing context. They don't need to be convinced that async is better. They already believe it. They need to be convinced that a product can actually deliver it, because everything they've tried has promised async and delivered more notifications.

The skepticism is specific: "Another collaboration tool? I already have six." The path through that skepticism is showing that Relay replaces the combination, not adds to it.


SURPRISING ELEMENTS

73% daily active usage against an industry average of ~40%. That's not incremental improvement — it's nearly double. For a collaboration tool, daily active usage is the hardest metric to move. It means people are choosing to open this product, not being forced into it by notifications. Worth interrogating what drives that.

35% meeting reduction is the metric that will stop a scrolling reader. Engineering managers know exactly how much meetings cost. At a 100-person engineering org paying average salaries, a 35% meeting reduction is worth roughly $500K-$1M annually in recovered engineering time. That's a concrete, calculable number that reframes the $12/user/month price as trivially small.

NPS of 67 is genuinely high for B2B SaaS (industry benchmark hovers around 30-40). This suggests something beyond satisfaction — it suggests advocacy. People are telling other people about this.

80% of feedback mentioning timezone-aware threads as the favourite feature. When one feature dominates feedback that heavily, it tells you what the product actually is in users' minds, regardless of how many other features exist. Relay is "the tool that makes timezones disappear." Everything else is supporting infrastructure.

2-minute max on video snippets is a design constraint that signals deep understanding of the audience. The problem with Loom in async contexts isn't video — it's that a 20-minute recording is functionally a meeting you're forced to attend alone. Capping at 2 minutes and auto-summarising is a design opinion that says: we respect your time even when we're the ones providing the tool.


EMOTIONAL TERRITORY

Start: Exhausted, skeptical, over-tooled. These readers have launch-announcement fatigue. They've seen "the future of work" pitched dozens of times. They feel the daily pain of broken async collaboration but have stopped believing any tool can fix it. There's a resigned quality — they've adapted to the dysfunction and built workarounds.

Middle: Recognition. The content should make them feel seen — specifically, it should name the problems they've normalised. The "catch-up meeting" they've accepted as inevitable. The morning scroll through overnight messages. The decision that got made without them. Naming these as unnecessary rather than inevitable is the shift.

End: Cautious optimism grounded in evidence. Not hype — proof. The beta numbers should land as "wait, that actually worked?" The feeling that drives action is relief: the possibility that the exhausting workaround system they've built might not be necessary. The CTA should feel like low-risk exploration, not commitment — and the free tier makes that structurally true.


POTENTIAL ANGLES

**The "invisible tax" angle.** Frame timezone-distributed work as carrying a hidden coordination tax that everyone pays but nobody quantifies. Then quantify it — hours in catch-up meetings, context lost in translation, decisions made without full input. Position Relay as eliminating the tax, not adding another tool. Works because it names a cost the audience feels but hasn't articulated, and reframes the purchase as a savings.

**The "async-first vs async-bolted-on" angle.** Draw a sharp line between tools that added async features to synchronous products (Slack's threads, Notion's comments) and a product built from the ground up for async. The architectural argument: you can't retrofit async onto a real-time messaging paradigm any more than you can make a sports car into a truck by adding a flatbed. Works because it gives the audience a new category to think in and explains why their current tools haven't solved the problem.

**The "meetings are a symptom" angle.** Don't position against meetings directly — position meetings as the symptom of broken information flow. When context doesn't travel well asynchronously, people schedule synchronous time to transmit it verbally. Fix the information flow, meetings dissolve on their own. The 35% reduction isn't a feature — it's a side effect. Works because it's counterintuitive and elevates the conversation above "fewer meetings good."

**The "second-class timezone" angle.** Lead with the human experience of being in the late timezone. You wake up, decisions were made, context is scattered, you spend your morning reconstructing yesterday. Position Relay as the tool that makes every timezone a first-class participant. Works because it's emotionally resonant for anyone who's lived it, and it's a story competitors can't tell because they weren't designed for it.

**The "proof, not promise" angle.** Lead with beta results. 200 teams, 73% DAU, 35% fewer meetings, NPS 67. Let the numbers make the case before explaining the product. Works for the skeptical audience because it inverts the typical launch post structure — evidence first, explanation second — and signals confidence.


VOICE NOTES

Threadline's voice is peer-to-peer, not vendor-to-customer. It should sound like one engineering leader explaining a tool to another. Contractions, direct address, occasional dry observation. "You know the drill" energy rather than "we're excited to announce" energy.

Lean into: Naming specific daily frustrations with precision. The brand earns trust by demonstrating it understands the problem at a granular level. Specificity over enthusiasm. "You open Slack and spend 45 minutes scrolling" lands harder than "staying aligned is challenging."

Avoid: Breathless future-of-work language. "Revolutionary." "Game-changing." "Transform your workflow." This audience has heard it all and it triggers immediate skepticism. Also avoid dunking on competitors by name — the brief is explicit about this, and it's strategically smart. The audience uses those tools and doesn't want to feel stupid for having chosen them.

The "slightly witty when it fits" note means: a dry aside or an unexpected turn of phrase, not jokes. Think raised eyebrow, not punchline. The wit should come from observation, not performance.

Confidence should come from data and specificity, not from adjectives. "73% daily active usage" is confident. "An incredible engagement rate" is not.


RAW MATERIAL

- 73% daily active usage vs ~40% industry average. The gap is the story.

- 35% average meeting reduction. Translatable to dollars, hours, and recovered engineering capacity.

- NPS 67. Context-dependent — powerful if framed against B2B benchmarks (30-40 typical).

- 200 teams across 8 months of private beta. Scale of validation.

- 80% of feedback citing timezone-aware threads. The product has a clear hero feature in users' minds.

- "Timezone-aware threads" — conversations that surface context as narrative, not notification wall. The distinction between narrative and notifications is worth building language around.

- "Decision logs" capturing context, alternatives, and participants. The "understand the reasoning, not just the outcome" framing.

- 2-minute max video snippets with auto-transcription and auto-summary. The design constraint itself is a selling point.

- "Deep work protection" — focus windows, batched notifications. A collaboration tool that actively protects you from itself.

- "Notion meets Linear meets Loom" — useful positioning shorthand, though risky if overused (can sound derivative).

- Free tier up to 5 users. Low barrier to trial. The CTA can honestly be "try it, it's free."

- Series A, 40 employees, founded 2024. Small team, recent founding — can be framed as agility and focus, but avoid if it triggers enterprise-buyer risk aversion.

- "Async-first" as a design philosophy, not a feature. The distinction between built-for-async and async-as-afterthought is the competitive moat.

- Configurable focus windows. The word "configurable" matters — it signals respect for team-level autonomy rather than imposed workflow.
