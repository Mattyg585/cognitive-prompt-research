# Your Meetings Are a Workaround for Something That's Actually Broken

You opened Slack this morning and started scrolling. Not reading — reconstructing. Piecing together what happened overnight from fragments scattered across channels, threads, and DMs. Fifteen minutes turned into thirty. Somewhere around the forty-minute mark, you gave up and pinged someone in London to just tell you what was decided about the API migration.

Then you joined the 9 AM standup — the one that exists because nobody on your team trusts that the overnight context actually made it across the timezone gap. Twelve people. Twenty-five minutes. Mostly spent re-establishing things that already happened.

You've accepted this as the cost of distributed work. It isn't. It's the cost of broken information flow — and the meetings are just the duct tape your team invented to hold things together.

## The async tools you have weren't built for async

Here's the thing nobody says out loud: your team isn't bad at async. Your tools are. They were designed for a world where everyone is online at the same time, and async got bolted on as an afterthought. Threaded conversations that bury context three clicks deep. Recorded walkthroughs nobody watches because they're forty minutes long and the relevant part starts at minute twenty-six. Wiki pages that go stale the day after someone writes them.

The intent is there. The effort is there. Your team has tried. They've built elaborate systems of tags and emoji reactions and "please read before standup" conventions. All of it is compensation for a structural problem: the tools assume real-time presence, and when you don't have that, everything degrades. Not dramatically — just enough that you need a meeting to fix what the tools couldn't carry.

You can't retrofit async onto software designed for "everyone in the room." The architecture won't let you. It's not a configuration problem. It's a foundation problem.

## Relay is built on a different foundation

Relay starts from a premise that most collaboration tools treat as an edge case: the people on your team are never all awake at the same time, and the software should be designed around that reality rather than despite it.

Three things about how it works matter most.

**When you open Relay after being offline, you don't see a wall of messages.** You see a structured account of what happened — threaded, sequenced, and surfaced based on when you're actually reading it. Timezone-aware threads were the feature 80% of beta users named first when asked what they'd miss. The difference is notifications versus narrative. One gives you a pile of fragments and asks you to assemble the picture. The other hands you the picture and lets you drill into the fragments if you want to.

**Decisions carry their reasoning with them.** Every decision logged in Relay captures what was considered, what was rejected, and who was in the room. Arriving in the morning to discover your team made a call without you is one thing. Arriving to find the full reasoning, the alternatives they weighed, and why they went the direction they did — that's a completely different experience. One breeds quiet resentment. The other builds trust across twelve time zones.

**The platform actively protects your focus time.** Relay has configurable deep work windows where notifications don't trickle in — they're batched, summarised, and waiting for you when you surface. A collaboration tool that pushes you out of it during your best working hours. That's a design value, not a feature toggle. It signals something about what Relay thinks your attention is worth.
