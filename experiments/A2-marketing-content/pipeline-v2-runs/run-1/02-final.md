## The catch-up meeting that nobody called

You have eleven people on your engineering team. Three are in London, two in Nairobi, four across US timezones, two in Singapore. It is a good team — experienced, fast, aligned on what matters.

And yet every Monday morning, someone is out of the loop. Not because they're disengaged, but because the decision happened at 2pm their time on a Friday, in a thread that was already seventeen messages deep by the time they woke up Saturday. They see the outcome. They missed the reasoning. So they ask. Then someone explains. Then someone else asks the same question later because they also missed it. And gradually, without anyone deciding it should be this way, you have a standing meeting whose only purpose is explaining what already happened.

That meeting has a name. Teams call it different things. But everyone on a distributed team knows exactly what it is, because they're in one.

Relay is built to end it.

Threadline's public launch today, after eight months of private beta with 200 engineering teams, is the arrival of an **async collaboration platform** that was designed from the beginning for the way distributed teams actually work — not retrofitted to it.

The difference sounds subtle until you've lived both sides of it. Most collaboration tools are synchronous tools with async features stapled on. Slack is a chat room that lets you leave messages. Notion is a document tool where you can tag people. Loom is a video recorder that sends links. Each of them asks you to fit your distributed workflow into their synchronous logic.

Relay starts from the other direction. The default is async. The synchronous moments are the exception you opt into, not the environment you're constantly fighting.

## What your team is actually tolerating

Here is what a normal day looks like for a tech lead at a 200-person company distributed across four timezones.

You wake up to 47 Slack notifications. Some are noise. Some are decisions that got made while you were asleep. Some are questions directed at you that have already been answered by someone else, or haven't been answered and are now blocking someone. You spend thirty minutes trying to figure out which is which before you've written a line of code.

Then there's the standup — the one where everyone describes what they did yesterday and what they're doing today. It takes twenty-two minutes. At least six of those minutes are context-setting for people who weren't in the thread where the plan changed. At least four minutes are someone sharing their screen to show a thing that could have been a short video. Two people say "let's take this offline" about things that will never actually get taken offline.

This is the tax. It's not dramatic. It's not a crisis. It is a steady, invisible drain on the energy of every person on your team, every day.

Beta teams using Relay reported a 35% reduction in meetings. That number compounds. A 35% reduction in standing meetings is not a scheduling win — it is hours of deep work returned to people who were hired for their thinking, not their availability.

## The feature that changes how decisions travel

Relay's most-loved feature, mentioned in 80% of beta feedback, is timezone-aware threads. The way it works is simple and the effect is significant.

When you open a thread, you don't see a wall of messages in chronological order with a notification badge telling you there are 23 new ones. You see what happened — structured as a narrative, shaped around when you were last online, showing you the context you need to understand where things stand. The thread understands that you were asleep during part of it.

This sounds like a small UX decision. It is actually a different theory of how information should move through a distributed team.

The second feature that changes things is decision logs. In most tools, a decision is something that happens in a thread or a meeting and then disappears into history. Later, when someone wonders why the architecture is this way, or why the launch date shifted, or why we're using this vendor instead of that one — the reasoning is gone. You have the outcome, not the thinking.

Decision logs in Relay capture the context, the alternatives considered, and who was in the room. Not as a compliance exercise. As something a teammate in a different timezone can actually read and come away understanding, not just informed of what happened but why. The catch-up happens in the artifact, not in a meeting called to explain the artifact.

## Protecting the thing that makes your team worth having

Deep work is load-bearing. It is not a productivity concept — it is the actual work. The thinking, the building, the solving. Everything else is overhead in service of the thing that happens when a good engineer gets three uninterrupted hours with a hard problem.

Distributed teams are, paradoxically, well-positioned to protect deep work — because nobody expects you to respond instantly to a message sent at 3am their time. But the tools don't reflect this. Notifications arrive whenever they're sent. The expectation of availability creeps in because the infrastructure supports it.

Relay's focus windows let team members configure blocks of time where notifications are batched and summarised rather than delivered as they arrive. The platform is built on the premise that maker time is worth protecting and that protecting it is a team decision, not an individual act of discipline.

The VP of Engineering at one beta company — 120 people, three major timezone clusters — put it simply: "We stopped having catch-up meetings. The catch-up just... happens."

That's the sentence that captures what Relay actually does. It doesn't eliminate the need to know what's going on. It makes knowing what's going on something that happens through how the tool works, not through meetings you schedule because the tool can't do it.
