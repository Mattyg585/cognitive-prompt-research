# You Already Know What Happened Last Night. You Just Don't Know It Yet.

You open your laptop at 8 AM. Slack has 94 unread messages. Three channels have those little red badges that mean someone probably needs something from you. There's a Loom link from your backend lead in Kraków — 47 minutes long. And someone made a decision about the API migration while you were asleep, but the reasoning is buried somewhere in a thread that forked into two other threads, one of which is now in a DM you weren't added to.

So you do what you do every morning. You spend the next 90 minutes reconstructing the night. Scrolling, skimming, piecing together context from fragments. Then you schedule a 30-minute sync to confirm what you think you understood. By 10 AM, you haven't done any actual work — but you're finally caught up.

You've been doing this so long it doesn't feel like a problem anymore. It feels like the cost of running a distributed team.

It's not. It's a tax. And you've been paying it because every tool you use was designed for people in the same room, then awkwardly retrofitted for people who aren't.

## Decisions that explain themselves

If you've ever opened a project management tool to find that a major technical decision was made while you were asleep, you know the feeling. The outcome is there. The reasoning isn't. So you ping someone, who's now asleep on their side of the planet, and wait twelve hours for the context you need to do your job.

Relay's decision logs capture the full arc: the context that prompted the discussion, the alternatives considered, who weighed in, and why the team landed where it did. When you read a decision the next morning, you don't just see what was chosen. You understand why — and you can see whether your perspective was already represented or whether you need to weigh in.

This sounds simple. It is simple. The reason it doesn't exist elsewhere is that other tools treat decisions as outcomes to be tracked, not conversations to be preserved. Relay treats them as artifacts of collaborative thinking that need to survive the timezone gap.

## The meeting that should have been a two-minute video

Your backend lead has an update on the database migration. In the old world, this becomes either a 47-minute Loom recording that three people will watch at 2x speed and nobody will finish, or a meeting that blocks four calendars across three timezones.

In Relay, it's a two-minute video snippet. Maximum length: two minutes. That constraint is deliberate. The video auto-transcribes, auto-summarises, and attaches to the relevant thread — so it's searchable, skimmable, and connected to the conversation it belongs to.

Beta teams reported a 35% reduction in meetings. Not because Relay has an anti-meeting philosophy. Because when context travels well, most meetings become unnecessary. The ones that remain are the ones that should have been meetings all along — the ones where you actually need real-time human interaction.

## Deep work isn't a setting. It's a commitment.

Most collaboration tools let you set a "do not disturb" status. It's a polite suggestion that your colleagues mostly ignore because they can see you're online and their thing is urgent-to-them.

Relay's focus windows are different. During your configured deep work hours, notifications aren't silenced — they're batched, summarised, and held until your focus window ends. The platform doesn't just protect your time; it restructures the information so that when you resurface, catching up takes seconds rather than derailing the next hour.

This matters because the real cost of notification overload isn't the interruption itself. It's the twenty minutes of context-switching after each interruption, multiplied across a day. Relay eliminates the multiplication.
