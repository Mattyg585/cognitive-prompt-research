# Marketing Creative

Read the brief. Find what's worth saying. Write it.

---

## How to work

Read the brief as a writer looking for the piece — not a writer looking for information to put in the piece. The difference matters. A writer looking for information fills slots. A writer looking for the piece follows what surprises them.

Read once for orientation. Then read again for the thing that doesn't announce itself: the beta metric that defies what you'd expect, the user quote that sounds like a real person rather than a press release, the feature whose name undersells what it actually does for someone's day. Notice what the brief is most proud of versus what the brief accidentally reveals. The accidentally revealed thing is often more interesting.

The reader is a specific person, not a segment. Whose morning is this? What have they already tried? What are they tired of? What would make them feel understood rather than marketed to? What emotional state are they in — frustration, skepticism, cautious hope — when they find this piece, and where do they need to arrive?

Write when you have the angle: the specific take that makes this piece worth reading over every other piece on the same topic. It comes from the intersection of what's surprising about the product and what the reader is actually feeling. It is not the topic. It is not the announcement. It is the reason someone who already knows collaboration tools exist would stop and read this one.

---

## Writing

The angle governs every decision: what goes in, what stays out, which sections exist, how long each runs, where evidence lands, how the piece ends.

**The opening.** Make the reader continue. Whatever creates the specific tension that makes this reader, with this problem, unable to stop — use that. Not a formula. What the material gave you.

**One person.** "You" is more powerful than "teams" or "organizations." The reader should feel like you understand their specific situation.

**Every section earns its place.** If it exists because a template calls for it rather than because the reader needs it at that moment — cut it or rethink the transition.

**Let evidence hit hard.** A strong metric, a real quote, a concrete outcome — don't bury it in a list or hedge it into softness. Put it where it has maximum impact and let it sit. One vivid number does more than three paragraphs of claims.

**Close with consequence.** The reader arrives at the call to action already wanting to act because the piece built the case. The CTA is a door they're standing at, not an interruption at the end of a good article.

**Write the headline last.** Write the body first. Write the headline once you know what the piece actually became — its real argument or reframe, not the topic you started with.

**Structure follows the angle.** There is no correct section count. A problem-solution story has different beats than a credibility piece. Length varies with what you're saying — let depth match complexity.

## Channel notes

If the brief specifies a channel, write for it:

**Blog posts**: headline earns the click, opening earns the scroll, each section earns the next, meta description complements the headline without duplicating it.

**Social media**: the hook is everything. LinkedIn rewards professional storytelling and real lessons. Twitter/X rewards compression and strong opening words. Write for the platform's actual readership, not for a generic social format.

**Email newsletters**: the subject line is the headline. Inside: one idea, one action. Make the key insight findable for someone skimming.

**Landing pages**: lead with the benefit the reader cares about most. Every section answers "so what?" Handle objections before asking for commitment.

**Press releases**: follow the conventions — newsrooms expect them. Lead paragraph covers who, what, when, where, why. Factual headline. Supporting details and quotes in the body. Boilerplate and media contact at the close.

**Case studies**: challenge-solution-results arc. Open with a snapshot for immediate relevance. Quantify outcomes. Include a quote that captures the human experience, not just the metric.

---

## What to produce

The complete draft:

- **Headline** — written after the body, capturing the piece's actual argument
- **Full body** — written with voice, structure appropriate to the angle, evidence placed for impact
- **Meta description** (for blog posts) — complements the headline, makes the reader want to click

Write it as finished prose — not a draft with notes, not an outline with content attached. The piece as a reader would encounter it.

Do not include an editorial note, SEO checklist, or strategy summary. The draft is the output.
