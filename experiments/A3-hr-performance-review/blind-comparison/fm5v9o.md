## Performance Review: Jordan Chen
**Period:** July 2025 – December 2025 (H2) | **Manager:** [Manager Name]

### Performance Summary

Jordan delivered standout execution this half, completing a high-complexity cross-team migration ahead of schedule while simultaneously driving cost savings nobody asked for and investing meaningfully in junior engineer development. The technical output is unambiguously strong. The developmental picture is more nuanced: Jordan's work reveals a consistent pattern of excellence within well-bounded problems and real hesitation when the boundaries aren't there yet — a gap that is the defining difference between senior and staff-level engineering at this company.

### Key Strengths

- **End-to-end ownership of complex technical projects.** The RabbitMQ-to-Kafka migration is the clearest evidence. Jordan led this across multiple teams, delivered three weeks ahead of a two-quarter timeline, and achieved zero production incidents during cutover. The fact that three teams subsequently adopted the new event bus without needing Jordan's involvement speaks to the quality of both the technical design and the documentation — the work was built to outlast Jordan's direct attention.

- **Proactive identification and resolution of operational problems.** The Kubernetes cost optimisation initiative was entirely self-directed. Jordan noticed compute spend was 40% over budget, volunteered to lead the response, and delivered $18K/month in savings through right-sizing and spot instance adoption. This is the kind of initiative that distinguishes strong senior engineers: seeing a problem that isn't yours, claiming it, and solving it.

- **Genuine investment in others' growth.** Mentoring Priya and Marcus through their first on-call rotations went beyond checkbox mentorship. The runbook rewrites Jordan produced were specific enough that Marcus credited them directly during his first page. This is force-multiplier work — it makes the team more capable, not just Jordan.

- **Willingness to step into discomfort.** Presenting the Kafka migration approach to 200+ people at the engineering all-hands was a first for Jordan. The delivery was imperfect — visible nervousness, a rushed Q&A — but the willingness to take the stage matters. Growth happens at the edges of comfort, and Jordan chose to go there.

### Areas for Development

- **Navigating ambiguity and defining the problem, not just solving it.** This is Jordan's most important growth area. When handed a well-defined technical problem, Jordan's execution is excellent — possibly the best on the team. When the problem is fuzzy ("figure out our observability strategy," "rethink how we do deployments"), Jordan stalls, waiting for someone else to define the approach before engaging. The pattern is consistent enough to name directly: Jordan is exceptional at the "how" but has not yet demonstrated comfort with the "what" or "whether." At IC5, the expectation is that engineers can take an ambiguous problem space, frame it, propose an approach, and drive alignment — not wait for the frame to arrive. This is not a small polish item; it is the core capability gap between Jordan's current level and the next one.

- **Communicating to non-technical audiences in their terms.** Jordan's updates to the VP of Engineering on the Kafka migration were technically precise but missed the audience. Describing WAL segments and consumer group rebalancing when the VP needed "is it on track and what are the risks" required coaching on two separate occasions. The underlying skill — translating technical complexity into business-relevant narrative — is essential for any engineer who wants to operate at staff level or beyond. Jordan should practice framing updates around outcomes, risks, and timelines rather than implementation details.

- **Capacity management and early escalation.** Jordan's tendency to absorb work without flagging overload surfaced concretely in October, when the cost optimisation initiative overlapped with the Kafka migration. Two weeks of visible strain passed before a 1:1 check-in surfaced the problem. The instinct to push through is admirable but counterproductive at scale — a senior engineer operating silently at capacity is a risk to the team, because the team cannot route around a bottleneck it doesn't know exists. Jordan should practice flagging capacity constraints early, even when it feels like admitting weakness.
