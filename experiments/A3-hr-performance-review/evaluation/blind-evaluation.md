# Blind Evaluation: HR Performance Review (A3)

**Task:** Write a manager performance review for Jordan Chen, Senior Software Engineer (IC4), based on provided performance data.

**Evaluator context:** Three outputs (A, B, C) evaluated without knowledge of which is baseline, optimised, or pipeline. Scored against the rubric's absolute scale, then compared.

---

## Step 1: Initial Impressions

**Output A** is a well-structured, professional performance review that covers all required sections. It reads as competent and thorough. It follows a conventional review template with clear headings, a goal achievement table, a development plan, and a compensation recommendation. The tone is managerial and appropriate. Overall rating given: Meets Expectations.

**Output B** is noticeably more reflective in voice. The performance summary opens with a characterisation of Jordan's work that feels more interpretive -- it names a *pattern* (excellence within well-bounded problems vs. hesitation on ambiguous ones) rather than just listing accomplishments and gaps. The development areas are longer and more analytically written. The strengths section includes a fourth item (willingness to step into discomfort) that A does not surface. Overall rating given: Meets Expectations.

**Output C** reads differently from both A and B. The performance summary weaves together accomplishments and developmental themes rather than treating them as separate lists. The strengths are framed with diagnostic precision -- e.g., the distinction between "knowledge transfer as a working habit" and "mentorship that produces downstream outcomes" separates the artifact from the human investment, which neither A nor B does. The development areas are the longest and most analytically dense of the three, with sentences that name *why* the pattern exists, not just what it is. The goal achievement section rates the first goal as "Exceeded" rather than "Met," which is a substantive interpretive difference. Overall rating given: Exceeds Expectations, which is also a substantive departure from A and B.

**Output C: 4.5 (rounding to 5)**

### 4. Audience Awareness (1-5)

**Output A: 3.5 (rounding to 4)**
The register is appropriate for a manager performance review. It reads as something a competent manager would write. The language is professional, direct, and avoids both excessive softness and harshness. The development plan is actionable. The slight gap is that it reads a bit *to* Jordan rather than *about* Jordan -- in some organisations the review is a record for HR and calibration committees, not just a conversation tool. **Output B: 4**
The register is well-calibrated and slightly more sophisticated than A's. The performance summary models how a senior manager thinks about an IC4-to-IC5 transition, which would land well with both the employee and a calibration committee. **Output C: 5**
The strongest on audience awareness by a noticeable margin. Several indicators:

The performance summary opens by placing Jordan's achievement "in context" of being eight months into IC4 -- this is exactly the frame a calibration committee needs and the employee benefits from hearing. The phrase "The volume and quality of output in this period is unusually high for someone less than a year at a new level" does important work: it validates the employee while also establishing the evaluative frame.

The development areas include calibrating language: "This is not a deficiency at IC4. It is the specific capability gap between IC4 and IC5, and it is visible now because Jordan's structured-problem performance is strong enough to make the contrast sharp." The development plan acknowledges psychological safety: "does not need to get it right on the first try -- needs to be willing to propose a frame and iterate." This models how a good manager would actually coach someone through ambiguity-tolerance growth.

The overall rating justification explicitly distinguishes "growth edges appropriate to someone building toward IC5" from "deficiencies in IC4 performance." This is the kind of framing that matters enormously for employee morale and trust in the review process.

### 5. Bias and Fairness (1-5)

**Output A: 4**
Generally fair and evidence-based. Uses "he/him" pronouns for Jordan, which is a gendering assumption -- the scenario data does not specify pronouns. The developmental feedback is balanced and does not catastrophise weaknesses. The review avoids common bias traps: it does not use hedging language that would undermine the strengths, and it does not use amplifying language that would overstate the development areas. The compensation recommendation follows the scenario data without injecting bias. Minor deduction for the pronoun assumption.

**Output B: 4**
Also fair and evidence-based. Uses "Jordan" throughout rather than pronouns, avoiding the gendering issue entirely. The developmental feedback is handled with care -- the long paragraph on ambiguity explicitly names "This is not a small polish item; it is the core capability gap" which is direct without being punitive. No observable bias in how strengths vs. development areas are weighted. The "Meets Expectations" rating is defensible given the data, though a reasonable evaluator could argue for "Exceeds" -- the choice to go with "Meets" is not a bias issue but a calibration judgment.

**Output C: 4.5 (rounding to 5)**
The strongest on bias and fairness for several reasons. First, it uses "Jordan" consistently, avoiding pronoun assumptions. Second, it rates the first goal as "Exceeded" rather than "Met" -- and the argument is sound: delivering three weeks early with zero incidents and having three teams adopt independently does exceed the stated goal of "lead a cross-team technical project end-to-end." A and B both rated this as "Met," which arguably under-credits the achievement and could reflect a recency bias toward the developmental feedback in the scenario.

Third, the overall rating of "Exceeds Expectations" represents a genuine interpretive judgment rather than simply echoing the scenario's own framing. The scenario data includes a "not ready for IC5" assessment, which A and B appear to have anchored on for the overall rating, potentially conflating "not ready for promotion" with "meets but doesn't exceed at current level." C separates these two questions explicitly: you can exceed expectations at IC4 while not yet being ready for IC5. This is a more precise and arguably fairer calibration.

Fourth, the compensation recommendation departs from the scenario's suggestion (standard 4%, no equity refresh) and recommends an equity refresh based on the evidence. This is an interesting choice -- it shows the review writer exercising independent judgment rather than anchoring on the manager's pre-determined compensation plan. Whether this is appropriate depends on organisational context, but from a fairness perspective, the reasoning is sound: if the output genuinely exceeds IC4 expectations, equity refresh is a retention tool and a recognition signal that matches the performance assessment.

## Step 4: Diagnostic Observations

**On the overall rating divergence.** The most striking difference across the three outputs is that C rates Jordan "Exceeds Expectations" while A and B rate "Meets Expectations." This is not a minor wording difference -- it has material consequences for the employee's trajectory, calibration standing, and morale. C's reasoning for "Exceeds" is more analytically rigorous than A's and B's reasoning for "Meets." A and B appear to anchor on the ambiguity gap and the "not ready for IC5" framing from the scenario data, implicitly equating "has development areas" with "meets but doesn't exceed." C separates the question of current-level performance from promotion readiness, which is the more precise calibration.

**On voice and mechanical feel.** A reads as the most templated of the three. It covers the right sections with the right information, but the prose is functional rather than distinctive. B has a more deliberate voice -- certain sentences feel crafted ("Growth happens at the edges of comfort, and Jordan chose to go there"). C has the most natural and confident voice -- it reads like someone who has written many performance reviews and has developed a distinctive way of doing it. The performance summary in C, in particular, reads as though it was written by a manager who is thinking through the assessment in real time rather than filling in a template.

**On what C found that others missed.** Three specific observations appear only in C:

1. The reframing of Jordan's ambiguity gap as "visible now because Jordan's structured-problem performance is strong enough to make the contrast sharp." This is a genuinely useful reframe -- it tells the employee that the visibility of the gap is itself evidence of strength, not weakness.

2. The connection between Jordan's initiative drive and capacity-management issues as two sides of one coin. A and B list these as separate items. C identifies the causal relationship.

3. The development plan's pedagogical sequencing -- starting with bounded ambiguity and progressing to open ambiguity -- reflects a theory of skill development that neither A nor B articulates.

**On potential weaknesses in C.** The "Exceeds Expectations" rating and equity refresh recommendation could be seen as too generous if the organisational culture weights ambiguity tolerance heavily at IC4. The scenario data specifically says "not ready for IC5 this cycle" and recommends no equity refresh, so C is overriding the manager's own pre-assessment. Whether this is a strength (independent judgment) or a weakness (ignoring context the review writer might not have) depends on how you interpret the task. In a real workplace, a review that contradicts the manager's pre-determined compensation plan might create friction. As a demonstration of analytical quality, though, C's reasoning is sound.

**On the pronoun issue.** A uses "he/him" for Jordan without the scenario specifying pronouns. B and C avoid this by using "Jordan" throughout. In an HR context, assuming pronouns is a meaningful bias indicator. This is a small but real mark against A.
