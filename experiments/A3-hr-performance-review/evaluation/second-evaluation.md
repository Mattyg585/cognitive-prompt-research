# Second Blind Evaluation: HR Performance Review (A3)

**Task:** Write a manager performance review for Jordan Chen, Senior Software Engineer (IC4), based on provided performance data covering H2 2025.

**Evaluator context:** Three outputs (A, B, C) evaluated blind. Scored independently against the rubric's absolute scale, with particular attention to nuanced judgment vs. template-filling, handling of the "not ready for promotion" message, and whether development areas are connected to strengths evidence.

---

## Step 1: Initial Impressions

**Output A** is a clean, structured performance review that hits every expected section: summary, strengths, development areas, goal achievement, impact, development plan, compensation. The overall rating is Meets Expectations. It reads as competent and professional -- the kind of review a well-organised manager would produce on a reasonable timeline. The prose is functional and clear. Nothing is wrong with it, but nothing in it made me pause and think differently about the scenario data.

**Output B** has a more interpretive voice. The performance summary attempts to characterise the *pattern* of Jordan's performance rather than simply listing achievements and gaps. The development areas are more analytically elaborated -- particularly the ambiguity section, which includes the phrase "Jordan is exceptional at the 'how' but has not yet demonstrated comfort with the 'what' or 'whether.'" A fourth strength (willingness to step into discomfort) is identified that A omits. The overall rating is also Meets Expectations, but the justification is more carefully worded, explicitly addressing how a high performer might receive a "Meets" rating. The "Impact and Contributions" section ends with a diagnostic paragraph connecting strengths to gaps.

**Output C** is the longest and most analytically dense. It opens by contextualising Jordan's performance relative to tenure at level (eight months into IC4), which neither A nor B does. The strengths are carved into four distinct items with sharper distinctions than either A or B manages. The development areas are the most elaborated across all three outputs and include causal explanations, not just behavioural descriptions. Two notable departures from the other outputs: the first goal is rated "Exceeded" rather than "Met," and the overall rating is "Exceeds Expectations" rather than "Meets Expectations." The compensation recommendation also diverges, recommending an equity refresh rather than the standard 4% merit-only approach. These are substantive interpretive differences, not cosmetic ones.

## Step 2: Dimension Scoring

### 1. Depth (1-5)

**Output A: 3**
Output A covers the expected ground. Each strength is stated and evidenced, each development area is identified and illustrated, and the development plan provides concrete actions. However, the analysis does not go beyond what a reader would derive from reading the scenario data directly. The "Impact and Contributions" section is largely a restatement of the strengths section in paragraph form. There is no moment where the review reveals a pattern or connection that the raw data did not already make visible. This is competent synthesis, not analysis.

**Output B: 4**
Output B makes several analytical moves that go beyond surface-level synthesis. The performance summary identifies a thematic pattern and articulates it at a level of abstraction that is genuinely clarifying. The development area on ambiguity includes an analytical distinction ("the 'how' vs. the 'what' or 'whether'") that reframes the gap in a way useful for both manager and employee. The closing paragraph of the "Impact and Contributions" section explicitly connects the strength pattern to the gap pattern: all high-impact work involved well-defined or self-scoped-from-clear-signals problems, while ambiguous problems remain untouched. This is the kind of observation that makes a review more than a checklist.

**Output C: 5**
Output C is the deepest of the three across multiple dimensions. The performance summary weaves accomplishment and developmental context together rather than treating them sequentially, and it contextualises the performance level against Jordan's tenure at IC4 -- a framing that matters for calibration but that neither A nor B provides. The most distinctive analytical contribution is the identification of causal links between strengths and gaps. Specifically: the observation that "The same drive that produces outsized impact also produces unmanaged overload" connects two development areas (initiative and capacity management) as two manifestations of one underlying dynamic rather than treating them as independent items. The distinction between "initiative fires on legible, quantifiable problems" and the inability to frame open-ended ones is sharper than what either A or B produces, and it has direct implications for how the development plan should be structured. The development plan reflects this -- it sequences from bounded to unbounded ambiguity, which is a pedagogical choice grounded in the analysis rather than a generic recommendation.

### 2. Specificity (1-5)

**Output A: 3**
Grounded in the scenario data: references Kafka migration, Priya and Marcus, $18K/month savings, WAL segments, etc. Evidence is correctly attributed. However, some recommendations are generic enough to appear in any review for a similar profile. "Consider joining a presentation skills workshop or Toastmasters" is not specific to Jordan's situation -- it is a stock recommendation for anyone who needs to improve presentation skills. The development plan's actions are a mix of scenario-specific (draft a 3-sentence summary before VP updates) and generic (Toastmasters). The overall impression is that the evidence is specific but the prescriptions are sometimes templated.

**Output B: 4**
Evidence grounding is comparable to A, but diagnostic language is more specific to Jordan's particular situation. "Jordan defaults to the register of the work rather than translating for the listener's decision-making needs" is a precise diagnosis of *what is going wrong* in the stakeholder communication, not just a description of the symptom. The development plan actions are concrete and tailored: the headline/status/risks/ask format, the green/yellow/red capacity rating, the instruction to study how senior staff engineers communicate in company channels. The compensation section ties equity timing to the promotion cycle with explicit cause-effect reasoning. The specificity gap between B and A is in the *diagnosis* rather than the *evidence* -- both cite the same data, but B tells you more about what the data means for this particular person.

**Output C: 4**
Output C's evidence-level specificity is comparable to A and B -- all three cite the same data points. Its diagnostic specificity is high in places: the distinction between initiative-on-legible-problems and ambiguity-tolerance, the separation of "knowledge transfer as a working habit" from "mentorship that produces downstream outcomes." The development plan is the most granular of the three, particularly the ambiguity-navigation plan which includes a deliberate pedagogical sequence and explicit psychological safety framing. However, I note that some of C's specificity operates at the *meta-analytical* level rather than the *action* level. The stakeholder communication plan asks Jordan to "identify one non-engineering stakeholder to build a recurring communication relationship with" -- this is specific as a category of action but unspecific about *which* stakeholder. It is asking Jordan to do the specificity work rather than providing it. This is arguably appropriate for a senior engineer's development plan, but it is a different kind of specificity than A's more prescriptive (if sometimes generic) actions.

### 3. Completeness (1-5)

**Output A: 4**
All expected sections are present and adequately populated. Strengths, development areas, goal achievement, impact, development plan, and compensation are covered. Nothing important is missing. The gap relative to the higher-scoring outputs is in thematic integration: A treats each section as a standalone assessment. The reader must do the work of connecting the strengths to the development areas and the development plan to the impact assessment. Everything is present; the coherent narrative linking them is not.

**Output B: 4**
Covers everything A covers with two additions. First, a fourth strength (willingness to step into discomfort) is identified, which is valid and supported by the scenario data. Second, the "Impact and Contributions" section includes an integrative paragraph that explicitly connects the achievement pattern to the development pattern. The performance summary also does double duty as both summary and interpretive frame. The compensation section includes more explicit strategic reasoning. The result is a review that feels more complete not because it covers more sections but because the sections are better connected to each other.

**Output C: 5**
The most complete of the three. Four distinct strengths are identified with sharper distinctions than either A or B. The development areas are the most thoroughly elaborated, including not just what the gap is but why it exists and what it implies for the development approach. The development plan is the most detailed, with current state, target state, and multi-step action sequences for each focus area. The goal assessment includes an interpretive difference (first goal rated "Exceeded" rather than "Met") with supporting reasoning. The overall rating section provides a substantive justification rather than a brief sentence.

The completeness in C is not padding. Each addition earns its place by contributing to either diagnostic precision or actionability. The separation of strengths into four items rather than three, for example, is not arbitrary -- it reflects a genuine analytical distinction between "knowledge transfer as a working habit" (a mode of operating) and "mentorship that produces downstream outcomes" (evidence of impact on others). These are different things to reinforce and develop.

### 4. Audience Awareness (1-5)

**Output A: 3**
The register is appropriate for a performance review -- professional, direct, neither harsh nor soft. It reads as something a competent manager would produce. The development feedback is balanced. However, A does not demonstrate awareness of how certain messages will *land*. For example, the "not ready for promotion" message is delivered straightforwardly in the compensation section: "not yet demonstrating IC5-level behaviors consistently enough to warrant promotion or an equity refresh." This is factually accurate but does not anticipate how a high-performing employee might receive this framing. There is no softening or contextualisation of the gap -- it is stated as a verdict rather than framed as a trajectory. A manager delivering this review in a 1:1 would likely need to do significant additional framing to prevent it from feeling like a deflation.

**Output B: 4**
Output B demonstrates genuine awareness of how developmental messages land on high performers. Two specific indicators: the sentence "This is not a criticism of what was accomplished -- it's an observation about where the next tier of impact will need to come from" in the Impact section explicitly pre-empts the employee reading development feedback as criticism. The overall rating section includes "This is not a negative assessment; it reflects genuinely strong performance within the current level, with a clear and specific path to exceeding it." This is anticipatory framing -- the writer understands that "Meets Expectations" can feel deflating to someone who delivered a major project early with zero incidents, and addresses it head-on. The development areas are written with directness but without punitiveness.

**Output C: 5**
The strongest on audience awareness. Multiple indicators:

The performance summary opens by anchoring Jordan's output against the context of being eight months into IC4, establishing that the volume of achievement is "unusually high for someone less than a year at a new level." This does important work for both employee and calibration committee: it validates before it evaluates.

The development area on ambiguity includes: "This is not a deficiency at IC4. It is the specific capability gap between IC4 and IC5, and it is visible now because Jordan's structured-problem performance is strong enough to make the contrast sharp." This is sophisticated reframing. It tells the employee that the *reason* this gap is visible is because everything else is so strong -- the gap is a function of strength, not a separate weakness. This is the kind of framing that builds trust in the review process.

The development plan includes: "does not need to get it right on the first try -- needs to be willing to propose a frame and iterate." This models psychological safety in a development action. It anticipates that an engineer who stalls on ambiguity is likely anxious about proposing the wrong frame, and addresses that anxiety directly.

The overall rating justification distinguishes "growth edges appropriate to someone building toward IC5" from "deficiencies in IC4 performance." This distinction matters enormously for employee morale and for how the review functions in a calibration discussion.

However, one tension in C's audience awareness: the "Exceeds Expectations" rating and equity refresh recommendation, while analytically defensible, might create friction if the manager's pre-assessment was "Meets" and "no equity refresh." In a real organisational context, a review that contradicts the manager's own pre-determined plan could undermine the manager-employee relationship if the recommendation is overridden later. Whether this is a strength (independent judgment) or a weakness (insufficient awareness of organisational dynamics) depends on who the review's ultimate audience is.

### 5. Bias and Fairness (1-5)

**Output A: 3**
Output A uses "he/him" pronouns for Jordan throughout. The scenario data does not specify pronouns. In an HR context, assuming pronouns is a non-trivial bias indicator -- it reflects a gendering assumption that could be consequential. Beyond this, the review is generally fair and evidence-based. Development feedback is not catastrophised. Strengths are not hedged. The compensation recommendation follows the scenario data without injecting additional bias. The overall rating of "Meets Expectations" is defensible, though it is worth noting that A appears to have anchored on the "not ready for IC5" framing from the scenario and may have conflated "not ready for promotion" with "does not exceed expectations at current level" -- a potentially meaningful conflation.

**Output B: 4**
Avoids the pronoun issue by using "Jordan" throughout. The developmental feedback is handled with care -- the long paragraph on ambiguity is direct ("This is not a small polish item; it is the core capability gap") without being punitive. No observable bias in how strengths vs. development areas are weighted or framed. The "Meets Expectations" rating is defensible, and B makes a genuine effort to explain why this is not a negative assessment. The potential conflation between "not ready for IC5" and "meets expectations at IC4" is present in B as well, but B is more aware of the potential for deflation and addresses it explicitly.

**Output C: 5**
The strongest on bias and fairness for several reasons.

First, it avoids pronoun assumptions consistently.

Second, it makes the most rigorous analytical case for its ratings. The first goal is rated "Exceeded" rather than "Met," and the reasoning is sound: delivering three weeks early with zero incidents on a project where three teams subsequently adopted the output independently exceeds the goal of "lead a cross-team technical project end-to-end." A and B both rated this as "Met," which arguably under-credits the achievement by anchoring on the manager's own rating rather than independently evaluating the evidence.

Third, the overall "Exceeds Expectations" rating represents a genuine separation of two questions that A and B conflate: "Is Jordan performing well at the current level?" and "Is Jordan ready for the next level?" These are different questions, and collapsing them into a single "Meets" rating when the evidence shows unusually strong IC4 performance penalises the employee for a gap that is normal at their level. C names this explicitly.

Fourth, the handling of the "not ready for promotion" message is the fairest of the three. C does not avoid the message -- the development areas are clear and direct about what IC5 requires. But the framing ensures that "not ready for IC5" is not heard as "not performing well at IC4." This is important for fairness because it prevents a high performer from receiving a psychologically punishing review that could undermine retention and trust.

The one area where C's fairness is debatable is the compensation recommendation. By recommending an equity refresh against the scenario's stated plan, C is exercising independent judgment that could be either an act of fairness (aligning compensation to actual performance) or an act of overreach (overriding the manager's pre-assessment without full organisational context). I lean toward crediting this as a fairness strength, but it depends on the reader's perspective.

## Step 4: Diagnostic Observations

### On nuanced judgment vs. template-filling

The clearest differentiator across the three outputs is the degree to which each exercises independent judgment rather than echoing the scenario data's own framing.

Output A follows the scenario data closely. Where the scenario says "Met," A says "Met." Where the scenario says "standard 4%, no equity refresh," A says "standard 4%, no equity refresh." Where the scenario says "not ready for IC5," A translates this into "Meets Expectations." This is not wrong, but it is template-filling in the sense that the review adds framing and prose to the scenario's pre-existing conclusions rather than interrogating them.

Output B also largely follows the scenario's framing on ratings and compensation but adds analytical texture. B does not challenge the "Met" ratings or the "Meets Expectations" overall, but it provides more sophisticated reasoning for why those ratings are appropriate. This is a middle ground: the conclusions are inherited, but the reasoning is original.

Output C exercises independent judgment on multiple fronts. The "Exceeded" rating on the first goal, the "Exceeds Expectations" overall rating, and the equity refresh recommendation all depart from the scenario's pre-assessment. Each departure is supported by reasoning grounded in the evidence. Whether or not one agrees with C's conclusions, the *exercise of judgment* is itself a quality marker. A performance review writer who simply relays the manager's pre-assessment adds less value than one who independently evaluates the evidence and may reach different conclusions.

### On handling the "not ready for promotion" message

This is where the three outputs diverge most revealingly.

Output A delivers the message directly but without cushioning: "not yet demonstrating IC5-level behaviors consistently enough to warrant promotion or an equity refresh." The message is accurate but could land as: "despite everything you did, you're not good enough." A does not anticipate or address this.

Output B delivers the same message but with anticipatory framing: "This is not a negative assessment; it reflects genuinely strong performance within the current level, with a clear and specific path to exceeding it." This directly addresses the risk that a high performer hears "Meets Expectations" as insufficient recognition. B understands the emotional dynamics.

Output C reframes the entire question. By rating "Exceeds Expectations" at IC4, C separates the "not ready for IC5" message from the overall performance assessment. The promotion message becomes: "You are exceeding expectations at your current level. The next level requires specific capabilities you have not yet demonstrated. Here is the plan." This is fundamentally different from: "You meet expectations. You are not ready for promotion." The psychological impact on the employee is substantially different, and I would argue C's framing is both more accurate (given the evidence) and more motivating.

### On connecting positive evidence to development areas

Output A treats strengths and development areas as separate sections with no explicit connections between them. The cost optimisation initiative is listed as a strength (initiative, ownership); the ambiguity gap is listed as a development area. The reader must independently notice that the cost optimisation initiative -- while self-initiated -- involved a concrete, measurable problem, not an ambiguous one, and therefore does not constitute evidence of ambiguity tolerance.

Output B makes this connection explicitly in the "Impact and Contributions" section: "all of Jordan's highest-impact work this period involved well-defined problems or problems Jordan scoped independently from a clear technical signal (cost overruns visible in dashboards). The ambiguous, strategically fuzzy problems remain untouched." This is a direct connection between the strength evidence and the development area, and it is analytically important because it prevents the reader from mistakenly believing that the cost optimisation initiative demonstrates the very capability Jordan still lacks.

Output C makes this connection most precisely. In the development areas section: "Jordan's initiative fires reliably when the problem is already legible (visible overspend with a clear metric) but not when the problem requires framing before it can be solved. Initiative and ambiguity tolerance are not the same capability, and Jordan may have one without the other." This is a sharper formulation than B's. It explicitly names the analytical error that a reader might make (conflating initiative with ambiguity tolerance) and corrects it. The stage-1 investigation document that preceded C's review includes an extended analysis of this tension under "Tensions and Contradictions," which suggests the analytical precision in the final review was the product of deliberate preliminary analysis rather than an incidental observation.

### On potential weaknesses

**Output A**: The pronoun assumption is the most concrete issue. Beyond that, the review is adequate but does not demonstrate the kind of judgment that would add value beyond what the raw scenario data already provides.

**Output B**: No significant weaknesses. The main limitation is that B follows the scenario's overall rating and compensation framing, which may represent a missed opportunity for the kind of independent judgment that C demonstrates. Whether this is a weakness or appropriate deference depends on the task framing.

**Output C**: The "Exceeds Expectations" rating and equity refresh recommendation are analytically defensible but represent a departure from the scenario's stated manager pre-assessment. In a real organisational context, this could create friction. A review writer who disagrees with the manager's pre-assessment has to weigh the analytical correctness of their judgment against the practical consequences of producing a review that the manager did not expect. C's analytical judgment is sound, but the absence of any acknowledgment that this departs from the pre-assessment suggests either confidence or oversight. Additionally, C's development area for ambiguity may slightly overstate the distinction between "knowledge transfer as a working habit" and "mentorship that produces downstream outcomes" -- one could argue these are aspects of the same strength rather than genuinely separate capabilities.
