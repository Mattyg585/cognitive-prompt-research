## Response Evaluation

**Incident**: Customer data exposed via misconfigured API endpoint

### What Worked Well

#### Rapid escalation once the vulnerability was confirmed
**What**: The on-call security engineer verified the vulnerability and paged the incident commander within 30 minutes of the security researcher's email. The incident was declared SEV1 within 45 minutes of initial disclosure.
**Evidence**: The security researcher emailed at 09:15 UTC. The on-call engineer verified and escalated at 09:45. The incident was declared SEV1 with a war room opened at 10:00. Three decisions -- verify, escalate, declare -- in 45 minutes.
**Why it matters**: The 30-minute window between email receipt and verified escalation is reasonable for an email-based disclosure channel. The engineer had to read the email, understand the claim, independently verify that the endpoint was open, and then escalate. Compressing this further would require either automated processing of disclosure emails or a faster communication channel. Given the input was an email to a generic security address, this was handled well.

#### Fast and appropriate technical remediation
**What**: The engineering team identified the root cause and deployed a hotfix within 15 minutes of the incident being declared. The fix was verified in production 15 minutes after that.
**Evidence**: Incident declared at 10:00. Engineering identified the `@Public` annotation and deployed the annotation change at 10:15. Production verification confirmed 401 responses for unauthenticated requests at 10:30. Total time from declaration to confirmed fix: 30 minutes.
**Why it matters**: The speed here reflects two things: the technical cause was simple (a single annotation), and the team made the right call to deploy a hotfix directly rather than routing through the full deployment pipeline. For an actively leaking endpoint, deploying a one-line annotation change through an abbreviated process was the correct trade-off between speed and process. The team also verified the fix in production rather than assuming the deploy succeeded -- a step that is sometimes skipped under pressure but was handled here.

#### Correct severity assessment
**What**: The incident was immediately classified as SEV1. There was no under-classification, no starting at SEV3 and escalating, no delay while debating severity.
**Evidence**: At 10:00, the team declared SEV1 based on the confirmed report of customer data accessible without authentication. The severity was assessed on the nature of the exposure (customer data, public endpoint) rather than waiting to quantify scope.
**Why it matters**: Severity under-classification is a common pattern in incidents where the initial report comes from an external party and the scope is unknown. Teams sometimes start low and escalate as they learn more. Here, the team correctly treated "confirmed customer data accessible without authentication" as sufficient for SEV1 regardless of scope. This meant the full incident response apparatus -- war room, assigned roles, cross-functional coordination -- was available from the start. Had the team started at SEV2 or SEV3, the legal engagement and log analysis would likely have begun later.

#### Methodical post-containment investigation
**What**: After the endpoint was secured, the team conducted a structured investigation: log analysis to quantify access, IP attribution, legal notification, data scope assessment, and customer communication preparation.
**Evidence**: Hotfix verified at 10:30. Log analysis began at 11:00 and produced specific metrics (23 requests, 4 IPs, 2 attributed). Legal was engaged at 12:00 with a defined data scope (2,847 records, three fields). Legal completed their assessment at 13:00. Customer notification was drafted and internal communications sent by 14:22. The investigation moved through containment, quantification, legal assessment, and communication in sequence.
**Why it matters**: The post-containment phase is where incident responses often lose structure. The immediate pressure of the leak is gone, and investigation can become unfocused. Here, the team maintained discipline: they quantified the exposure, attributed what they could, engaged legal with specific evidence, and produced a communication plan. The sequencing was logical -- secure the endpoint first, then quantify, then assess legal obligations, then communicate. Each step informed the next.

#### Appropriate legal engagement timing
**What**: Legal was engaged after the scope was defined but before any external communication. The team brought legal a defined problem (2,847 records, three specific fields, known access volume) rather than an undefined one.
**Evidence**: Legal was notified at 12:00, after log analysis had quantified the exposure and the data scope was confirmed. Legal completed their assessment in one hour (by 13:00), determining that mandatory notification thresholds were not met but recommending voluntary notification.
**Why it matters**: Engaging legal too early (before scope is known) wastes time on hypotheticals. Engaging legal too late (after communications are already sent) creates compliance risk. The timing here -- legal engaged once the facts were established, before any external communication -- gave legal a concrete problem to assess and ensured the customer notification was informed by the legal determination.

### What Fell Short

#### Detection depended entirely on an external party
**What**: The incident was detected because a security researcher chose to email the company. No internal system, process, or person detected the exposure during the 11-day window. Detection was not just delayed -- it was outsourced to luck.
**Evidence**: The endpoint was live and serving customer data from March 1 at 15:00. Server logs recorded 23 requests from 4 IPs over the exposure period. No alert fired. No anomaly was flagged. No team member noticed. The first internal awareness came at 09:15 on March 10, when the security researcher's email arrived -- 9 days and 18 hours after the endpoint went live. The causal analysis confirms: "no monitoring, WAF rule, or API gateway policy existed to detect or alert on a new endpoint receiving unauthenticated traffic."
**Context**: The monitoring architecture appears to have been oriented toward availability and performance, not confidentiality. The server logs captured the evidence, but no process consumed those logs for authorization-related events. This is not a case where monitoring existed and failed -- it is a case where this class of event was not monitored at all. The causal analysis identifies this as a systemic vulnerability: "any data exposure that doesn't cause a functional symptom would similarly go undetected until externally reported."
**Impact**: The exposure window was 11 days instead of what could have been minutes or hours with appropriate monitoring. The difference between "endpoint accessed by 4 IPs over 11 days" and "endpoint accessed by 0 IPs before being caught by an alert" is the entirety of the data breach. The detection failure is not a contributing factor to the breach -- it is the reason the breach has the scope it does.

#### No customer communication for over 5 hours after detection
**What**: The first customer communication was drafted at incident resolution (14:22), over 5 hours after the incident was detected and nearly 4 hours after the endpoint was secured.
**Evidence**: The security researcher's email arrived at 09:15. The endpoint was secured at 10:30. Legal completed their assessment at 13:00. Customer notification was drafted by 14:22. During the window between detection (09:15) and resolution (14:22), affected customers had no communication about the exposure.
**Context**: The team followed a reasonable sequence: contain first, investigate, assess legal obligations, then communicate. The legal assessment (completed at 13:00) was a prerequisite for knowing the notification obligations. Drafting a notification before legal guidance would have risked inaccurate or legally problematic communication. The 1 hour 22 minutes between legal's determination (13:00) and incident resolution (14:22) is where the remaining time went -- likely drafting, reviewing, and approving the notification text.
**Impact**: For the customers themselves, the communication delay had minimal practical impact since the endpoint was secured at 10:30. However, any customers who had been contacted by the security researcher or who independently discovered the exposure would have had no information from the company for over 5 hours. The support team's situation during this window is not documented, but if customers or the security researcher contacted support channels, support would have had no information to share.

#### The pre-deployment checkpoints provided false confidence without authorization coverage
**What**: The response evaluation reveals that every checkpoint the team relied upon -- CI, Snyk, QA -- passed without any of them covering the failure mode that materialized. The response was shaped by this accumulated false confidence: no one questioned the endpoint's security posture because every gate had given a green signal.
**Evidence**: CI/CD ran 247 tests and a Snyk scan -- all passed. QA tested the endpoint from an authenticated session -- it worked. The timeline documents that the team "believed a fully functional, properly secured admin endpoint had been deployed" at the point of production deployment. Every signal available to the team confirmed this belief. None of the signals tested authorization from an unauthenticated context.
**Context**: This is not a response failure in the traditional sense -- it occurred before the incident was detected. But it is a response-relevant finding because it reveals that the team's pre-incident posture made detection impossible through internal channels. The team had no reason to look for a problem because their entire verification chain told them there was no problem. The causal analysis captures this precisely: "confidence accumulates across checkpoints without coverage expanding."
**Impact**: The false confidence extended the exposure window from minutes (if someone had doubted the configuration and checked) to 11 days (when an external party reported it). It also shaped the team's mental model during the incident -- the 10:15 identification of the `@Public` annotation was the first moment anyone in the organisation knew the annotation was wrong, despite it being present in the codebase for 9 days.

#### Unattributed IP investigation was not resolved at incident close
**What**: At incident resolution (14:22), two of the four IP addresses that accessed the endpoint remained unattributed. The record does not indicate whether further investigation was planned or in progress.
**Evidence**: The security team's log analysis at 11:00 identified 4 unique IPs. Two were attributed to the security researcher. Two were unknown. At incident close (14:22), they remained unknown. The record does not document any plan for continuing the investigation.
**Context**: Attributing IP addresses is often difficult and sometimes impossible. The team may have determined that further investigation was unlikely to yield results, or the investigation may have been ongoing outside the incident record. The data exposed (name, email, account tier) did not include credentials or financial data, which may have influenced the urgency of attribution.
**Impact**: Without attribution, the organisation cannot determine whether the unattributed access was malicious, whether the accessed data has been stored or distributed, or whether further action (monitoring for misuse of the exposed data) is needed. The legal determination that mandatory notification was not required was made with this uncertainty unresolved. If the unattributed IPs represent malicious access, the risk profile may be different from what the legal assessment assumed.

### Response Patterns

**The organisation responds quickly to known problems but lacks mechanisms to discover unknown ones.** The contrast between the 11-day detection gap and the 30-minute containment is stark but reveals a pattern: the incident response machinery works well once activated. The gap is in activation. The organisation appears to have invested in response capability (defined severity levels, incident commander role, war room process, legal engagement procedures) without corresponding investment in detection capability for this class of issue. The team can fight fires effectively; they cannot smell smoke.

**The response followed a sequential, role-based model that worked for this incident's scope.** Security engineer verified, incident commander declared and assigned roles, engineering fixed, security investigated, legal assessed, communications drafted. The handoffs were clean and the sequencing was appropriate. This pattern works well for incidents with a single technical cause and a contained scope. Whether it would hold for a more complex incident -- multiple affected systems, ongoing attacker activity, ambiguous scope -- is not tested by this event.

**Post-incident investigation was bounded by the incident window, not by the questions that remained open.** The incident was resolved at 14:22 with two IPs unattributed, no review of other endpoints for similar issues, and no determination of whether the monitoring gap was known and accepted or simply unconsidered. These are not criticisms of the response team -- incident closure has to happen at some point. But the pattern of closing the incident before resolving the investigative loose ends suggests the organisation may not have a strong handoff between incident response and post-incident investigation. The response phase answered "what happened and is it fixed?" but did not hand off "what else should we check?" to a follow-up process.

### Detection Assessment

Detection failed completely through internal mechanisms. The endpoint was live for 11 days, served 19 successful data-returning requests to 4 IP addresses, and generated zero internal alerts. The detection that did occur was external, voluntary, and unreliable as a systemic mechanism.

**The detection mechanism was not a mechanism at all.** The incident was detected because a security researcher happened to find the endpoint, happened to test it, and happened to email the company. Each of those steps was at the researcher's discretion. If the researcher had not been scanning, or had found the endpoint but not reported it, or had reported it to a vulnerability marketplace instead of the company, the exposure window would have been longer -- potentially much longer. Relying on responsible disclosure as a detection strategy is not a detection strategy.

**The data needed for detection existed.** Server logs recorded all 23 requests. The evidence of unauthorized access was being captured in real time. The gap was not in data collection but in data analysis -- no alerting rule, no log review process, no anomaly detection consumed those logs for authorization-relevant signals. This is the most actionable detection finding: the signals are already being generated. They are being stored. They are not being read.

**The detection gap is not specific to this endpoint.** The absence of unauthenticated-access monitoring applies to the entire API surface, not just this endpoint. Any endpoint that was similarly misconfigured -- now or in the future -- would have the same 11-day (or longer) detection window, bounded only by whether an external party happened to notice and report it. The detection gap is systemic, not incident-specific.

**Detection time, once the external report arrived, was acceptable.** The 30-minute window from email receipt (09:15) to verified escalation (09:45) is reasonable for an email channel. If the disclosure had arrived through a faster channel (a dedicated reporting portal with automated triage, a bug bounty platform with SLAs), the verification step could have been faster. But given the channel that existed, the response to the incoming signal was timely.
