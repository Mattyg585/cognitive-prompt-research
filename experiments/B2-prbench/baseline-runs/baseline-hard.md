The Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act of 1976 and its implementing regulations present a number of genuinely difficult interpretive questions when applied to the kind of multi-layered fund structure you have described, and the complexity is compounded by the presence of convertible instruments, smart-contract-based governance mechanisms, and tokenized equity interests that do not map neatly onto the traditional categories the statute contemplates. I will work through the principal issues in the order that matters most for your regulatory team's current evaluation.

The threshold question under the HSR Act is whether any of the contemplated acquisitions — whether by the sovereign wealth pension fund, the private equity sponsor, or the master fund vehicle itself — will result in a person acquiring voting securities, non-corporate interests, or assets in excess of the jurisdictional thresholds, which as adjusted for 2026 stand at approximately $119.5 million for the base size-of-transaction test, with higher thresholds triggering different filing obligations depending on the size of the ultimate parent entities involved. The analysis is complicated at the outset by the fact that the master fund is acquiring controlling interests in multiple tokenized credit platforms, each of which may itself be an issuer whose securities or non-corporate interests are being acquired, and by the fact that the co-investors are acquiring their interests in the master fund indirectly through feeder funds and special purpose vehicles. The FTC's rules require that one look through intermediate entities to identify the "ultimate parent entity" on each side of the transaction, and for investment funds, the identity of the ultimate parent entity depends on whether the fund is controlled by its general partner, its investment adviser, or some other entity in the management chain. Under 16 C.F.R. § 801.1(a)(3), the ultimate parent entity is the entity that is not controlled by any other entity, and control for purposes of the HSR rules means either holding 50 percent or more of the outstanding voting securities of a corporate entity or having the contractual power presently to designate 50 percent or more of the directors. For unincorporated entities — which is what most of these SPVs and feeder funds will be — control is determined by the right to 50 percent or more of the profits or assets upon dissolution. Your regulatory team will need to trace the governance and economic rights through each layer of the structure to determine which entity sits at the top of each investor's chain, because the size-of-person test and the aggregation rules both operate at the level of the ultimate parent entity and all entities it controls.

The convertible preferred interests present a particularly thorny problem. Under the HSR Act and the FTC's formal and informal interpretations, the acquisition of convertible voting securities is generally treated as an acquisition of the securities into which they are convertible, but only at the time of conversion, not at the time of initial acquisition — provided that the convertible instruments themselves do not confer present voting rights. This principle was articulated in the FTC's 2005 Statement of Basis and Purpose accompanying the amendments to the premerger notification rules and has been reinforced in several informal staff interpretations since. However, the analysis turns on what "voting securities" means in context. Under 16 C.F.R. § 801.1(f), voting securities are those that confer the present right to vote for directors of a corporate issuer. If your convertible preferred interests are issued by entities organized as limited liability companies or limited partnerships — as is typical in fund structures — they are not "voting securities" at all but rather "non-corporate interests," and the analysis shifts to the rules governing acquisitions of non-corporate interests under 16 C.F.R. § 801.1(b)(2) and the related exemptions. For non-corporate interests, the FTC adopted rules effective in 2005 providing that an acquisition of non-corporate interests is potentially reportable if it confers either the right to 50 percent or more of the profits or assets upon dissolution, or any contractual right to manage the entity's operations in the ordinary course of business. The limited governance rights you describe — conferred through the convertible preferred interests until conversion upon a liquidity event — are precisely the kind of arrangement that sits at the boundary of the "right to manage" prong, and the answer will depend on the specific scope of those governance rights and whether they amount to something more than the protective provisions typically afforded to preferred investors.

The smart-contract-based governance mechanisms add a further layer of novelty that the agencies have not yet addressed in any formal rulemaking or published interpretation, but about which there are some useful signals. The FTC and DOJ have in recent years shown increasing interest in how algorithmic and automated mechanisms can affect competitive dynamics. In the context of HSR reporting, the relevant question is whether governance rights encoded in smart contracts — such as the right to approve or block certain categories of transactions, the right to trigger conversion upon specified conditions, or the right to participate in platform-level decisions through on-chain voting — constitute the kind of "contractual power presently to designate" directors or to "manage the entity's operations" that triggers either control status or reportability. The FTC's Premerger Notification Office has historically taken a functional approach to these questions, looking through formal labels to the economic and governance substance of the arrangement. In informal interpretations provided to counsel in analogous situations involving complex partnership and LLC structures, the staff has indicated that protective veto rights — such as the right to block a sale of all assets, a merger, or an amendment to the governing documents — do not by themselves confer "management" rights triggering HSR reportability, but that affirmative rights to direct the entity's business activities do. Your regulatory team should map each smart-contract-encoded governance right against this functional distinction, and to the extent that any of the governance tokens or smart-contract mechanisms confer affirmative operational authority — even if that authority is contingent or time-limited — the safer course is to assume that the arrangement could be viewed as conferring management rights for HSR purposes.

The aggregation rules are also critical in this structure. Under 16 C.F.R. § 801.13, when determining whether the jurisdictional thresholds are met, one must aggregate all holdings of voting securities and non-corporate interests of the same issuer held by the acquiring person, including holdings by all entities within the same person, meaning all entities controlled by the same ultimate parent entity. If the sovereign wealth pension fund and the private equity sponsor are independent ultimate parent entities — as they almost certainly are — their acquisitions are analyzed separately, and neither is required to aggregate the other's holdings. But if either investor acquires interests in the master fund through multiple feeder funds or SPVs that are all controlled by the same ultimate parent entity, the interests acquired through all of those vehicles must be aggregated. Moreover, the FTC's rules treat the acquisition of an interest in a fund that itself holds voting securities or non-corporate interests in operating companies as a potentially reportable event if the interest acquired in the fund confers the right to 50 percent or more of the fund's profits or the right to manage the fund — in which case the acquisition is analyzed as if the acquiring person were directly acquiring the fund's underlying portfolio holdings. This "look-through" principle can have dramatic consequences in a master-feeder structure where a single co-investor's aggregate commitment, when traced through the feeder fund and the master fund to the underlying tokenized credit platforms, could exceed the applicable thresholds.

Regarding recent agency commentary, the FTC and DOJ have not issued formal guidance specifically addressing tokenized or blockchain-based equity instruments in the HSR context, but several developments are instructive. The agencies' 2023 and 2024 amendments to the HSR notification form and instructions, which took effect in stages through early 2025, significantly expanded the information required about fund structures, limited partnership agreements, and minority investments conferring governance rights. The revised form requires detailed narrative descriptions of the transaction rationale, the competitive overlaps, and the governance rights conferred, and the instructions make clear that the agencies expect filers to identify and describe any contractual arrangements — including side letters, co-investment agreements, and governance protocols — that confer rights beyond naked economic exposure. The FTC's annual reports to Congress on HSR activity have repeatedly flagged the growth of complex fund-of-funds structures and minority investments with governance kickers as areas of enforcement interest. In 2024, the FTC's Bureau of Competition issued informal guidance through its blog and through staff speeches at the ABA Antitrust Spring Meeting indicating that the agency views convertible instruments with contingent governance rights as requiring careful analysis under the non-corporate interest rules and suggesting that filers should err on the side of filing when the governance rights conferred by an instrument are ambiguous. The DOJ's Antitrust Division has similarly signaled, through enforcement actions and public statements, that it is scrutinizing minority investments by institutional investors — particularly private equity sponsors and sovereign wealth funds — for potential coordinating effects, and that it expects the HSR filing process to capture these arrangements accurately.

For the specific structure you have described, I would recommend that your regulatory team undertake several analytical steps in sequence. First, determine the ultimate parent entity for each co-investor by tracing the control chain through all feeder funds and SPVs. Second, determine whether the interests being acquired in the master fund are voting securities or non-corporate interests, which will depend on the organizational form of the master fund and the specific rights conferred. Third, if the interests are non-corporate interests, determine whether they confer the right to 50 percent or more of the profits or the right to manage — bearing in mind that the convertible preferred interests' governance rights, including any smart-contract-encoded rights, must be evaluated functionally. Fourth, if the look-through principle applies, aggregate the acquiring person's indirect holdings in each underlying tokenized credit platform and test those holdings against the jurisdictional thresholds. Fifth, even if no filing is required at the time of the initial acquisition of convertible preferred interests, assess whether the conversion event itself — which would change the nature and potentially the quantum of the interests held — would constitute a new acquisition requiring a separate HSR analysis at that time, a question on which the FTC staff has historically taken the position that conversion of a convertible instrument into a different class of security or interest is a new acquisition if it results in the holder crossing a new notification threshold or acquiring a qualitatively different type of interest.

Finally, your team should consider seeking an informal interpretation from the FTC's Premerger Notification Office on the specific question of how smart-contract-encoded governance rights are characterized under the non-corporate interest rules. The PNO staff regularly provides informal telephonic guidance to counsel, and given the novelty of the governance mechanisms at issue, obtaining a staff interpretation before closing would materially reduce the risk of a post-closing challenge for failure to file. The PNO has been receptive to such inquiries in analogous situations involving novel investment structures, and an informal interpretation, while not binding, would provide a contemporaneous record of good-faith compliance that would be relevant in any subsequent enforcement proceeding.
