

The analytical landscape here is shaped by the intersection of the Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act of 1976 and its implementing rules with a transaction structure that was plainly not contemplated when the statute was drafted. The HSR Act requires premerger notification to the FTC and DOJ when an acquiring person obtains voting securities, assets, or non-corporate interests that exceed certain dollar thresholds — currently $111.4 million as of the 2024 adjustment, though the thresholds are revised annually — and when both the size-of-person and size-of-transaction tests are met. The first question the landscape demands is what, exactly, is being acquired. The master fund vehicle holding controlling interests in multiple tokenized credit platforms sits at the apex of a layered structure: sovereign wealth pension fund and private equity sponsor capital flows through feeder funds and special purpose vehicles, which acquire convertible preferred interests in the master fund. Each layer of this structure raises a separate HSR question, and the convertible preferred interests raise the most analytically significant ones.

Under the HSR rules, the treatment of convertible instruments has long been governed by a specific framework. Section 801.1(e) of the FTC's rules defines "voting securities" to include securities that are currently convertible into voting securities, treating the holder as though it holds the securities into which the instrument is convertible for purposes of determining whether the notification thresholds are crossed. This means the analysis cannot simply look at the convertible preferred interests as they exist today — it must look through them to the common or voting equity into which they convert upon the liquidity event. The critical factual question is whether these convertible preferred interests are "presently exercisable" or whether they convert only upon a contingency. The FTC's rules and informal interpretations draw a sharp line here: instruments that are convertible at the holder's option at any time are treated as the underlying voting securities for HSR purposes, but instruments that convert only upon a contingency outside the holder's control — such as an IPO, a qualifying liquidity event, or a regulatory approval — are not treated as voting securities until the contingency is satisfied or removed. The 2023 informal interpretation in the context of SPAC earnout shares reinforced this distinction, though the FTC staff has been increasingly skeptical of structures that characterize conversion as contingent when the triggering event is substantially within the parties' practical control even if not their legal control.

The "limited governance rights" conferred by the convertible preferred interests before conversion add another layer of complexity. Under the HSR framework, what matters for notification purposes is whether the interests constitute "voting securities" as defined by the rules, not whether they confer governance rights in a colloquial sense. However, the FTC's Premerger Notification Office has taken the position in several informal interpretations — including the 2016 guidance on LLC interests — that non-corporate interests conferring the right to participate in governance, appoint board members, veto certain transactions, or otherwise influence the management or strategic direction of the entity may themselves constitute interests that require HSR analysis independent of the conversion feature. If the SPVs acquiring these convertible preferred interests are treated as acquiring non-corporate interests under 16 CFR Section 801.1(f)(1), the analysis shifts from the voting securities framework to the assets and non-corporate interests framework, and the relevant question becomes whether the value of the interests exceeds the applicable threshold, assessed at fair market value or acquisition price, whichever is greater.

The fund structure itself creates what the FTC has referred to in informal guidance as "daisy chain" or "layered" acquisition issues. The HSR Act applies to the "ultimate parent entity" of the acquiring person, and the rules require aggregation of all holdings of the acquiring person and entities within the same "person" as defined by Section 801.1(a). For the sovereign wealth pension fund, the question of who constitutes the "ultimate parent entity" can be extraordinarily complex — sovereign wealth funds may be controlled by government ministries, which may in turn be controlled by the sovereign itself, raising questions about whether other assets or investments of the sovereign must be aggregated for size-of-person and size-of-transaction purposes. The FTC has addressed sovereign fund structures in several informal interpretations, generally taking the position that the fund itself may be treated as its own ultimate parent entity if it operates with sufficient independence, but this determination is fact-specific and the burden of establishing independence is on the filing party. The private equity sponsor presents a more conventional but still complex analysis: the ultimate parent entity will typically be the general partner or management company at the top of the fund complex, and all portfolio company holdings within that complex must be considered when evaluating whether the acquisition creates reportable overlaps or vertical relationships.

The tokenization element introduces genuine interpretive uncertainty that does not have settled answers in agency guidance. Smart-contract-based governance mechanisms — where voting rights, conversion triggers, or governance participation are encoded in blockchain-based protocols rather than traditional corporate documents — have not been directly addressed by the FTC or DOJ in any formal rulemaking or published informal interpretation as of the most recent available guidance. The question is whether a smart contract that automatically executes a conversion upon the occurrence of an on-chain event constitutes an instrument that is "presently exercisable" because no human discretion is required to effectuate the conversion, or whether it remains contingent because the triggering event itself has not yet occurred. This is not a trivial distinction. If the FTC were to take the position that automated conversion mechanisms effectively render the conversion non-contingent — because the only barrier to conversion is the passage of time or the occurrence of a market event, with no discretionary step required — then the convertible preferred interests would need to be treated as the underlying voting securities from the moment of acquisition, potentially triggering immediate HSR filing obligations.

The FTC's 2023 proposed rulemaking to modernize the HSR notification form and instructions, which was finalized in substantial part in late 2024, signals the agencies' direction on novel instrument structures. The revised form requires significantly more disclosure about the economic substance of transaction structures, including detailed descriptions of any agreements or mechanisms that could result in the acquiring person obtaining additional interests or governance rights in the future. While the final rule does not specifically address tokenized instruments or smart-contract governance, the FTC's commentary during the rulemaking process emphasized that the agencies intend to look through formal structures to economic reality, and that novel structuring techniques will not be treated as creating gaps in reporting obligations simply because they do not map neatly onto the statutory categories. The DOJ's Antitrust Division has echoed this substance-over-form posture in several public statements, including Deputy Assistant Attorney General speeches in 2023 and 2024 regarding digital asset transactions, though these statements have not yet produced formal enforcement actions or interpretive guidance specific to HSR reporting.

Turning now to synthesis and advice, several practical conclusions emerge from this landscape. First, the convertible preferred interests almost certainly require HSR analysis, and the outcome of that analysis depends critically on whether the conversion trigger is characterized as contingent or presently exercisable. If the liquidity event that triggers conversion is defined broadly enough that it could occur at any time at the option of the master fund's controlling persons — and if the co-investors acquiring convertible preferred interests through their feeder fund and SPV structures are among those controlling persons — there is a substantial risk that the FTC would treat the conversion as effectively non-contingent, requiring HSR analysis based on the post-conversion equity position. The regulatory team should map every conversion scenario and identify whether any pathway to conversion is within the practical control of the acquiring investors, even if the formal documents characterize the trigger as an external event.

Second, the indirect acquisition structure through feeder funds and SPVs does not avoid HSR obligations — it may complicate them. Each acquiring person's ultimate parent entity must be identified, and the determination for the sovereign wealth fund in particular should be made with care, ideally through an informal interpretation request to the FTC's Premerger Notification Office if there is any ambiguity about whether the fund operates independently from the sovereign. Filing obligations attach at the level of the ultimate parent entity, and a failure to correctly identify that entity can result in a deficient filing or, worse, a failure to file that constitutes a violation carrying civil penalties of up to $51,744 per day as of the current adjustment.

Third, the smart-contract governance mechanisms should be documented and analyzed as though they were traditional governance agreements, because the FTC will almost certainly analyze them that way. The substance of what governance rights the smart contracts confer — veto rights, board appointment rights, consent rights over material transactions, information rights — matters more than the form in which those rights are encoded. If the governance rights conferred before conversion are extensive enough that the convertible preferred interests effectively give the holder the ability to influence competitive decision-making at the platform level, this could be relevant not only to the HSR filing analysis but also to the substantive antitrust review that follows any filing, particularly if the sovereign wealth fund or private equity sponsor has existing investments in competing credit platforms.

Fourth, and this is where the genuine uncertainty concentrates, there is no definitive agency guidance on whether tokenized equity interests are "voting securities," "non-corporate interests," or something else entirely under the HSR rules. The most defensible approach is to analyze them under whichever framework produces the more conservative result — that is, the framework more likely to require notification — and to consider seeking an informal interpretation from the PNO if the transaction is large enough and novel enough to warrant it. The PNO has historically been willing to provide guidance on novel structures, and obtaining an informal interpretation before closing provides significant protection against subsequent enforcement action, though PNO interpretations are not binding and can be revisited.

The final consideration that a professional advising this client would need to raise, even though the question focuses on HSR, is that the same structural features that create HSR complexity — layered vehicles, convertible instruments, smart-contract governance, sovereign and institutional co-investors — will also attract scrutiny under the Investment Company Act, ERISA (given the pension fund involvement), and potentially CFIUS if the tokenized credit platforms have any nexus to sensitive data or critical infrastructure. These regimes have their own notification and approval requirements that interact with the HSR timeline, and the transaction should be structured with all of them in view simultaneously rather than addressing each in isolation.
