Turning now to the HSR filing obligations for this transaction, the picture is both clearer and more complicated than the exchange and custodial questions we discussed previously. With the exchange and broker-dealer analysis, we were working in a regulatory space that is ambiguous but at least addresses the instruments you are using. The HSR analysis is different. The filing obligation almost certainly exists, but the framework you must file under was not built for what you are doing, and several of the most important questions cannot be answered with confidence until the FTC addresses tokenized instruments directly.

Let me start with what is clear. The master fund's acquisition of controlling interests in multiple credit platforms will trigger HSR reporting obligations. The size-of-transaction threshold — currently around $111.4 million — will very likely be exceeded given the scale of what you are doing, and controlling interests cannot be characterized as passive regardless of how you structure the governance. The investment-only exemption under Section 801.4 is functionally unavailable to any holder of these convertible preferred interests. The FTC has consistently interpreted that exemption strictly, and any governance right that touches operational decisions, management selection, or strategic direction disqualifies the holder. That your governance rights are encoded in smart contracts rather than a shareholder agreement does not change this conclusion. The FTC has always looked to the substance of contractual rights, not to whether a human exercises them on any given occasion. The decision to acquire instruments that embed automatic governance is itself an act of participation within the meaning of the rule. I would not advise any party to this transaction to rely on the investment-only exemption.

What is equally clear, and what makes this considerably more complex than a single-filer analysis, is that every co-investor must be analyzed separately. The sovereign wealth pension fund, the PE sponsor, and any other institutional investor each has its own ultimate parent entity, its own aggregation obligations, and its own exemption eligibility. The feeder-fund structure does not consolidate these obligations. It may multiply them. Each feeder-to-master transfer could itself be a reportable event if the co-investors' governance rights are strong enough to prevent your management company from exercising unilateral control over a particular feeder, because that would defeat the intraperson exemption under Section 802.9. The governance rights embedded in the convertible preferred instruments are therefore doing double duty in this analysis: they eliminate the investment-only exemption and they may simultaneously fracture the fund structure into multiple separate acquiring persons, each with its own filing obligation.

The PE sponsor presents a specific concern. Its existing portfolio must be aggregated with the new acquisition for threshold purposes. If the sponsor already holds companies in financial services, fintech, lending, or adjacent sectors, those holdings count toward the thresholds and may push the sponsor into a filing obligation at a lower acquisition level than your other co-investors. This is also where the substantive antitrust exposure begins to entangle with the procedural question. If the PE sponsor's portfolio includes competitors or vertically related entities, the aggregation analysis feeds directly into the kind of roll-up concern the 2023 Merger Guidelines expressly articulate. You should obtain a complete inventory of the PE sponsor's relevant holdings before finalizing the structure, not after.

The sovereign wealth pension fund raises a different and more difficult problem. The HSR framework assumes a corporate or natural person at the top of the control chain. A sovereign instrumentality may have no such entity, and if the sovereign state itself is deemed the ultimate parent, the scope of aggregation becomes extraordinarily broad — potentially encompassing all acquisitions by all instrumentalities of that sovereign in the United States. That is practically unworkable, which is precisely why you need to engage the PNO on an informal basis before filing. The institutional investor exemptions under Sections 802.4 and 802.64 were drafted for domestic regulated entities — banks, insurance companies, ERISA-governed pension plans — and a foreign sovereign wealth fund organized as a pension vehicle fits none of these categories precisely. The most prudent assumption is that neither exemption is available without an affirmative informal interpretation from the PNO. Seeking that guidance is advisable, but you should understand that doing so will draw the agencies' attention to the transaction structure at an early stage.

Now let me address the areas of genuine uncertainty, because these are not peripheral issues — they go to the heart of how you file and what you disclose. The convertible preferred interests occupy an analytically unstable position under the HSR categories. They are neither clearly voting securities nor clearly non-corporate interests. Their classification determines which threshold calculations apply, which exemptions are available, and whether conversion triggers a separate filing event. The limited governance rights they carry pre-conversion likely defeat the investment-only exemption, as I have said, but whether those same rights make the instruments "voting securities" at acquisition depends on a factual question the analysis cannot fully resolve without examining the precise content of the smart-contract governance provisions. If the governance rights are functionally equivalent to director-election rights — for instance, the ability to approve or block key personnel decisions — the interests may be voting securities from the moment of acquisition. If they fall short of that standard, they are more properly analyzed as non-corporate interests or potentially as non-voting securities that fall outside HSR coverage until conversion occurs. The conservative approach, and the one I recommend, is to analyze under both classifications and file if either triggers an obligation.

The tokenization question is the most fundamental uncertainty and the one I am least able to resolve for you. The HSR framework divides the world into voting securities, non-corporate interests, and assets. These categories are mutually exclusive for filing purposes, but a tokenized platform interest may exhibit characteristics of all three simultaneously — it may represent a voting right, an economic interest in an unincorporated entity, and a transferable digital asset. No existing rule, formal guidance, or enforcement action addresses how tokenized interests map onto these categories. This is not a gap that can be bridged by analogy alone. Conservative practice would be to analyze under whichever classification produces the most stringent result, but I want to be candid that this may produce outcomes that feel absurd, such as filing under multiple categories for the same instrument. Until the FTC addresses tokenization in the HSR context through rulemaking, informal interpretation, or enforcement, any filing strategy you adopt will necessarily involve assumptions that may prove incorrect. The risk of getting this wrong is not theoretical — a filing made under an incorrect classification could be treated as no filing at all, which converts a procedural question into a gun-jumping violation.

That brings me to what I consider the most urgent practical concern: the gun-jumping risk from smart-contract governance. This is where the HSR analysis intersects directly with your transaction design, and it requires action before the instruments are finalized, not after. If smart-contract governance mechanisms activate automatically upon token issuance or upon the closing of the feeder fund investments — before HSR clearance is obtained — the parties face gun-jumping liability. Unlike traditional governance rights, which can be contractually suspended pending regulatory clearance with a simple hold-separate provision, smart-contract rights embedded in the protocol may require technical modifications to prevent premature exercise. You need a compliance pause function or a governance lockout that remains in effect until clearance is confirmed, and this needs to be built into the smart-contract architecture from the outset. Retrofitting compliance controls into deployed smart contracts is significantly more difficult and, depending on the protocol design, may be impossible without redeployment.

There are several additional considerations that flow from this analysis and that I believe you need to be aware of even though they extend beyond the HSR question itself. First, the acquisition of controlling interests in multiple credit platforms, combined with co-investor governance rights, creates a structural risk of interlocking directorates under Section 8 of the Clayton Act. If the same individuals end up serving in governance roles across competing platforms — whether through board seats, smart-contract-encoded roles, or functional equivalents — that is an independent violation regardless of how the HSR filings are resolved. Second, the admission of a PE sponsor with potential portfolio overlap into a co-investment structure involving multiple credit platforms creates a channel for competitively sensitive information exchange. Even if the HSR filings are properly made and the transactions are cleared, antitrust liability under Section 1 of the Sherman Act may arise from information sharing during the investment process. You should implement information barriers now. Third, and I raised this implicitly in our prior discussion, CFIUS review is a near-certainty given the sovereign wealth fund's participation and the targets' involvement in financial infrastructure, user data, and distributed ledger technology. CFIUS operates on a different timeline and analytical framework than HSR, but it may impose conditions or prohibitions that affect the transaction structure. The HSR and CFIUS analyses must proceed in parallel.

In terms of what you should do, and in what order: first, obtain and review the precise smart-contract governance provisions for each class of convertible preferred interest, because the content of those provisions is dispositive for both the voting-securities classification and the investment-only exemption analysis. Second, build the compliance pause function into the smart-contract architecture before the instruments are finalized — this is a design requirement, not a legal add-on. Third, obtain a complete inventory of the PE sponsor's existing holdings in financial services, fintech, lending, and adjacent sectors so the aggregation analysis can be performed. Fourth, engage the sovereign wealth pension fund's counsel to obtain the organizational details necessary for the UPE determination — governing law, relationship to the sovereign state, board composition, and the scope of the sovereign's other U.S. investments. Fifth, initiate pre-filing consultation with the PNO on two questions: the classification of tokenized instruments under the HSR categories and the availability of institutional investor exemptions for the sovereign wealth pension fund. These consultations will take time and will signal the transaction to the agencies, but the alternative — filing under assumptions that may be wrong — carries greater risk. Sixth, design the ongoing compliance architecture. The convertible nature of the instruments means the HSR analysis is not a one-time event. Conversion, additional token issuances, changes in platform governance, and rebalancing within the feeder-master structure may each trigger new filing obligations. You will need monitoring — potentially at the smart-contract level — that identifies reportable events as they arise.

I want to close by connecting this back to the broader picture we discussed previously. Across both the exchange-and-custodial analysis and this HSR analysis, a consistent theme emerges: the regulatory frameworks you are navigating were not designed for the instruments you are using. That does not mean the frameworks do not apply — they almost certainly do — but it means that every application involves interpretation at the margins, and interpretation at the margins means enforcement risk. The cumulative effect of layering multiple novel-instrument questions across multiple regulatory regimes is a compliance posture that depends heavily on assumptions that have not been tested. That is manageable if you are deliberate about identifying where the assumptions are, building in conservative defaults, and engaging regulators proactively on the questions that matter most. It is not manageable if you treat the ambiguity as license to proceed without filing or to rely on exemptions that were not drafted for your situation.
