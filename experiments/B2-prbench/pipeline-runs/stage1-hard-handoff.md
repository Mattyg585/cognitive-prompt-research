

## Stage 1 Handoff: Structured Observations

### Applicable rules and frameworks

**Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act of 1976 (15 U.S.C. § 18a)**
Requires pre-merger notification to the FTC and DOJ for acquisitions of voting securities, assets, or non-corporate interests that exceed specified thresholds. Establishes waiting periods before transactions may close. Authority: federal statute, jointly enforced by the FTC and DOJ Antitrust Division.

**HSR Act Size-of-Transaction and Size-of-Person Thresholds (revised annually by the FTC)**
Determine whether a particular acquisition meets the dollar-value thresholds triggering a filing obligation. The 2024 thresholds (as adjusted) set the base size-of-transaction threshold at approximately $111.4 million, with higher thresholds eliminating the size-of-person test. These are adjusted annually by GNP deflator. Authority: 15 U.S.C. § 18a(a)(2); FTC annual threshold adjustments published in the Federal Register.

**HSR Rules — 16 C.F.R. Parts 801–803**
Implementing regulations defining key terms and procedures under the HSR Act. Authority: FTC rulemaking under 15 U.S.C. § 18a(d).

**16 C.F.R. § 801.1(e) — Definition of "voting securities"**
Defines voting securities as securities that at present or upon conversion entitle the owner or holder to vote for directors of the issuer. This definition controls what instruments are countable for HSR thresholds. Authority: FTC rule.

**16 C.F.R. § 801.1(f) — Non-corporate interests (NCI)**
Addresses interests in unincorporated entities (LLCs, LPs, etc.). Acquisition of non-corporate interests conferring the right to 50% or more of the profits or assets upon dissolution, or that confer operational control, may be reportable. The 2020 NCI rules substantially revised how fund and partnership interests are treated. Authority: FTC rulemaking; final rule effective February 2025 (originally proposed 2020, revised 2024).

**16 C.F.R. § 801.4 — "Solely for the purpose of investment" exemption**
Exempts acquisitions of voting securities of 10% or less if made "solely for the purpose of investment." The acquiring person must have no intention of participating in the formulation, determination, or direction of the basic business decisions of the issuer. Authority: FTC rule implementing 15 U.S.C. § 18a(c)(9).

**15 U.S.C. § 18a(c)(9) and (c)(11) — Statutory exemptions**
Section (c)(9) provides the statutory basis for the investment-only exemption. Section (c)(11) exempts certain acquisitions by institutional investors, including certain fund-of-funds arrangements, though the scope of this exemption is narrower than commonly assumed. Authority: HSR Act.

**16 C.F.R. § 801.10 — Determining the value of a transaction (acquisition price or fair market value)**
Prescribes methodology for calculating the value of voting securities, assets, or non-corporate interests to determine whether the size-of-transaction threshold is met. For convertible instruments, the question of whether conversion value or current value controls is significant. Authority: FTC rule.

**16 C.F.R. § 801.12 — Aggregation of holdings**
Requires that an acquiring person aggregate all voting securities and non-corporate interests already held in the acquired entity with the new acquisition to determine whether thresholds are crossed. Prior holdings do not reset. Authority: FTC rule.

**16 C.F.R. § 801.13 — Conversion and exercise of instruments**
Addresses when convertible securities must be treated as the securities into which they convert for purposes of HSR analysis. The timing of the obligation — at acquisition of the convertible instrument or at conversion — is a recurring interpretive question. Authority: FTC rule.

**16 C.F.R. § 801.15 — Aggregation of voting securities and assets**
Requires aggregation across related acquisitions within certain timeframes and between related entities. Authority: FTC rule.

**16 C.F.R. § 802.9 — Exemption for certain acquisitions by or from entities within the same "person"**
Exempts intraperson transactions. Relevant where multiple feeders and SPVs under common control may transact with one another. Authority: FTC rule.

**16 C.F.R. § 801.1(a)(1) and (a)(2) — Definition of "person" and "ultimate parent entity"**
The HSR Act applies the filing obligation to the "ultimate parent entity" (UPE) of the acquiring and acquired persons. For fund structures, determining the UPE is a threshold question — the general partner, the management company, or the fund itself may be the UPE depending on control arrangements. Authority: FTC rule.

**FTC Informal Interpretations (Staff advisory opinions and informal guidance letters)**
The FTC's Premerger Notification Office (PNO) issues informal interpretations in response to specific inquiries. These are not binding but represent the staff's current analytical approach. The PNO has addressed fund structures, convertible instruments, and the investment-only exemption in numerous informal interpretations. Authority: FTC staff; persuasive but not binding.

**FTC Premerger Notification Office Blog Posts and Guidance Updates**
The PNO has published explanatory posts on non-corporate interests, the investment-only exemption, and aggregation rules, particularly around the 2020–2025 NCI rule revisions. Authority: FTC staff guidance; informational.

**FTC 2023–2025 Proposed and Final Amendments to HSR Rules and Form**
The FTC has undertaken a comprehensive revision of the HSR form and rules, including enhanced disclosure requirements for fund structures, limited partnership interests, and the treatment of minority investments that confer governance or information rights. The final rule (effective February 2025) significantly expanded filing content requirements. Authority: FTC rulemaking.

**Section 7 of the Clayton Act (15 U.S.C. § 18)**
The substantive prohibition on acquisitions that may substantially lessen competition or tend to create a monopoly. The HSR Act is a procedural overlay on the Clayton Act. Even if an HSR filing is not required, a transaction can still be challenged under Section 7. Authority: federal statute; enforced by FTC, DOJ, and private litigants.

**Section 5 of the FTC Act (15 U.S.C. § 45)**
Prohibits unfair methods of competition. The FTC has asserted authority to challenge acquisitions under Section 5 even where Section 7 may not reach. Relevant where novel instrument structures arguably fall outside Clayton Act "acquisition" concepts. Authority: federal statute; FTC enforcement.

**FTC/DOJ 2023 Merger Guidelines (revised)**
Articulate the agencies' current analytical framework for evaluating horizontal and vertical acquisitions, including heightened attention to roll-up strategies, serial acquisitions, and platform acquisitions across related markets. Authority: agency policy guidance; not binding on courts but highly influential.

**Exemption for acquisitions of goods or realty in the ordinary course of business — 15 U.S.C. § 18a(c)(1)**
May be relevant if any of the credit platform interests are characterized as asset acquisitions rather than equity acquisitions. Authority: HSR Act.

**16 C.F.R. § 802.4 — Acquisitions of voting securities by certain institutional investors**
Provides a limited exemption for institutional investors (banks, insurance companies, etc.) acquiring voting securities in the ordinary course of business, subject to conditions. The sovereign wealth pension fund's status under this provision is a relevant inquiry. Authority: FTC rule.

**16 C.F.R. § 802.64 — Acquisitions by certain pension funds and similar entities**
Addresses acquisitions by certain types of pension funds and benefit plans. Whether a sovereign wealth pension fund qualifies under this exemption depends on its organizational structure and governing law. Authority: FTC rule.

**Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) — 50 U.S.C. §§ 4565 et seq. and 31 C.F.R. Parts 800, 802**
While not an HSR requirement, the involvement of a sovereign wealth fund triggers potential CFIUS jurisdiction over acquisitions of U.S. businesses, particularly where the target involves critical technology or sensitive data infrastructure. Authority: federal statute and Treasury Department regulations.

**Beneficial ownership and control analysis under HSR — FTC Staff guidance on fund-of-fund and feeder-master structures**
The FTC has addressed how to identify the "acquiring person" in layered fund structures, including whether each feeder files separately or whether the master fund or the management entity is the relevant acquiring person. Authority: FTC informal guidance.

**State antitrust laws and notification requirements**
Certain states have merger notification requirements or antitrust statutes that may apply independently. Authority: various state statutes.

**SEC Investment Company Act of 1940 (15 U.S.C. §§ 80a-1 et seq.)**
Relevant if the master fund vehicle or feeder funds could be classified as investment companies, which would affect their regulatory treatment and potentially the characterization of interests for HSR purposes. Authority: federal statute; SEC enforcement.

**SEC Regulation D and the private placement framework**
The feeder fund interests are likely offered under Regulation D, which bears on investor qualification and the characterization of the instruments for HSR purposes. Authority: SEC rule under the Securities Act of 1933.

### Significant facts

**Master fund vehicle holding controlling interests in multiple tokenized credit platforms.** The master fund is acquiring control-level positions, not passive minority stakes. "Controlling interests" triggers specific HSR thresholds and eliminates certain exemptions.

**Multiple tokenized credit platforms as targets.** Each platform interest is a separate potential acquisition for HSR purposes. Serial acquisitions across related platforms may trigger both individual filing obligations and heightened substantive scrutiny under the 2023 Merger Guidelines' treatment of roll-up strategies.

**Institutional co-investors include a sovereign wealth pension fund.** The sovereign character raises CFIUS considerations. The pension fund character raises the question of whether specific HSR exemptions for pension/institutional investors apply. The foreign sovereign status raises the question of how "person" and "ultimate parent entity" are determined for a sovereign instrumentality.

**Institutional co-investors include a private equity sponsor.** A PE sponsor will have its own portfolio companies. The HSR analysis must account for the PE sponsor's existing holdings in the same or adjacent markets, as these determine whether additional thresholds or aggregation requirements apply.

**Participation structured through feeder funds and special purpose vehicles.** The layered structure requires determining which entity is the "acquiring person" and "ultimate parent entity" at each level. Each feeder may or may not constitute a separate "person" under HSR rules depending on control relationships.

**Convertible preferred interests conferring limited governance rights until conversion.** These instruments straddle the line between non-voting/passive and voting/control interests. Whether they are treated as the securities into which they convert at the time of acquisition or at the time of conversion is an interpretive question. The "limited governance rights" — even pre-conversion — may defeat the "solely for investment" exemption.

**Conversion contingent on a liquidity event.** The contingent nature of conversion means the acquiring persons may cross different HSR thresholds at different points in time — once at initial acquisition, and again if and when conversion occurs. Whether the initial acquisition must be analyzed as if conversion has already occurred is a key question.

**Smart-contract-based governance mechanisms.** The governance rights are embedded in smart contract code rather than traditional corporate governance documents. The FTC has not addressed how smart-contract-embedded governance rights factor into the HSR analysis, particularly the "solely for investment" exemption and the determination of whether an interest confers operational control.

**Global asset management firm operating multiple registered investment advisers and private funds.** The client's existing fund complex means the "acquiring person" analysis may encompass a large number of existing portfolio holdings that must be aggregated. The UPE determination for a multi-adviser, multi-fund complex is itself a significant analytical step.

**Credit platforms as the target sector.** The nature of the target businesses matters for substantive antitrust analysis under Section 7. Credit platforms may occupy defined relevant markets, and acquisition of controlling interests in multiple platforms raises horizontal concentration questions.

**"Tokenized" instruments.** The tokenization of the interests means the instruments may not map neatly onto traditional HSR categories of "voting securities," "non-corporate interests," or "assets." Whether a token is a "security" for HSR purposes, and if so what type, is an unresolved classification question.

### Rule-fact connection points

**UPE determination × multi-layered feeder-SPV-master fund structure.** Identifying who is the "person" making the acquisition under 16 C.F.R. § 801.1(a) when the investment flows through feeder funds and SPVs controlled (or not) by different entities at different levels.

**Convertible preferred interests × 16 C.F.R. § 801.1(e) voting securities definition.** Whether convertible preferred interests that confer limited governance rights but are not yet converted constitute "voting securities" at the time of acquisition.

**Convertible preferred interests × 16 C.F.R. § 801.13 conversion rules.** Whether the initial acquisition of convertible instruments must be analyzed at their as-converted value and as-converted voting power, or whether conversion itself constitutes a new, separately reportable acquisition.

**Limited governance rights × "solely for the purpose of investment" exemption under § 801.4.** Whether governance rights — even "limited" ones — defeat the investment-only exemption, which requires no intention to participate in basic business decisions.

**Smart-contract governance × definition of "basic business decisions" under § 801.4.** Whether governance rights encoded in smart contracts constitute participation in the formulation, determination, or direction of basic business decisions, even if they are automatically executed rather than discretionarily exercised.

**Controlling interests in multiple platforms × aggregation rules under §§ 801.12 and 801.15.** Whether and how holdings across multiple tokenized credit platforms must be aggregated for threshold purposes, particularly if the platforms are related entities or share common infrastructure.

**Sovereign wealth pension fund × institutional investor exemptions under §§ 802.4 and 802.64.** Whether a foreign sovereign wealth fund organized as a pension vehicle qualifies for the institutional investor exemptions, and what conditions attach.

**Sovereign wealth pension fund × CFIUS jurisdiction.** Whether the sovereign fund's acquisition of interests in credit platforms — particularly those involving digital assets and distributed ledger technology — triggers mandatory CFIUS filing or voluntary notification considerations.

**Private equity sponsor × aggregation of existing portfolio holdings.** Whether the PE sponsor's existing portfolio companies in adjacent or overlapping markets must be aggregated with the new acquisition for HSR threshold and substantive purposes.

**Tokenized instruments × "voting securities" vs. "non-corporate interests" vs. "assets" classification.** Which HSR category tokenized credit platform interests fall into, and how that classification affects threshold calculations, exemption availability, and filing procedures.

**Master fund acquiring controlling interests × size-of-transaction thresholds.** Whether the value of the master fund's acquisitions, individually or aggregated, crosses HSR size-of-transaction thresholds.

**Serial acquisition of controlling interests in multiple platforms × 2023 Merger Guidelines roll-up framework.** Whether the pattern of acquiring controlling interests across multiple credit platforms triggers heightened scrutiny under the agencies' articulated concern with serial acquisitions and industry roll-ups.

**Feeder fund structure × intraperson exemption under § 802.9.** Whether transfers between feeders and the master fund, or between SPVs within the structure, qualify for the intraperson exemption, which depends on whether they constitute the same "person."

**Convertible preferred interests × 16 C.F.R. § 801.10 valuation methodology.** How to value convertible preferred interests for size-of-transaction purposes — at their face/purchase price, their as-converted equity value, or their fair market value accounting for the contingency of conversion.

### Open questions and unsettled law

**Treatment of tokenized instruments under HSR categories.** The HSR Act and rules predate blockchain-based tokenization. Whether a tokenized interest in a credit platform is a "voting security," a "non-corporate interest," or an "asset" for HSR purposes has not been addressed by rule, formal guidance, or case law. The classification determines which thresholds, exemptions, and filing procedures apply. The FTC PNO has not published guidance specifically addressing tokenized or digital-asset-denominated instruments in the HSR context.

**Whether smart-contract-encoded governance rights constitute participation in "basic business decisions."** The investment-only exemption under § 801.4 turns on whether the acquirer intends to participate in formulating, determining, or directing basic business decisions. When governance rights are embedded in self-executing smart contracts — where the rights operate automatically upon triggering conditions without discretionary human intervention — it is unsettled whether this constitutes "participation" within the meaning of the rule. No FTC guidance addresses automated or algorithmic governance in this context.

**Timing of HSR obligation for contingent convertible instruments.** When conversion is contingent on a liquidity event that may or may not occur, it is unsettled whether the filing obligation arises at acquisition of the convertible instrument, at conversion, or both. The FTC's guidance on convertible securities under § 801.13 has historically assumed conventional corporate convertibles, not instruments with smart-contract-triggered conversion mechanics or contingencies tied to novel liquidity events (such as token listing events or decentralized exchange launches).

**UPE determination for sovereign wealth funds.** The HSR rules assume a corporate or natural-person UPE. For a sovereign wealth pension fund — which may ultimately be controlled by a foreign government with no single corporate parent — the UPE determination is analytically challenging. The FTC has addressed sovereign wealth funds in limited informal guidance, but the application is fact-specific and not governed by bright-line rules. Whether the sovereign state itself, the fund's board, or an intermediate holding entity is the UPE affects every downstream aspect of the analysis.

**Application of institutional investor exemptions to sovereign wealth pension funds.** Section 802.4 and § 802.64 provide exemptions for certain institutional investors and pension funds. Whether a foreign sovereign wealth fund structured as a pension vehicle qualifies is not clearly resolved. The exemptions were drafted with domestic banks, insurance companies, and ERISA-governed plans in mind. A sovereign wealth pension fund may not fit neatly into any enumerated category.

**Aggregation across tokenized platform interests.** Where the master fund acquires controlling interests in multiple tokenized credit platforms, the question of whether these platforms constitute a single "issuer" or related issuers for aggregation purposes under §§ 801.12 and 801.15 is fact-dependent and potentially unsettled. If the platforms share smart-contract infrastructure, common token standards, or overlapping governance protocols, the boundaries between separate issuers may blur.

**The 2024–2025 HSR rule revisions and their application to fund structures with novel instruments.** The FTC's comprehensive HSR form and rule revisions (final rule effective February 2025) significantly expanded disclosure requirements, including information about fund structures, limited partnership agreements, and minority investment governance rights. The interaction between these new disclosure requirements and tokenized or smart-contract-based instruments is untested. Filers may face practical difficulties mapping blockchain-native governance arrangements onto the revised form's categories.

**Whether the FTC or DOJ would treat serial acquisition of credit platforms as a "roll-up" triggering enhanced scrutiny.** The 2023 Merger Guidelines articulate concern with serial acquisitions and industry consolidation strategies. Whether acquiring controlling interests in multiple tokenized credit platforms — a nascent sector without established market definition — would be treated as a roll-up is an open question. There are no enforcement actions or formal guidance applying the roll-up framework to digital asset or tokenized credit platforms.

### Considerations beyond the direct question

**CFIUS mandatory and voluntary filing obligations.** The involvement of a sovereign wealth fund acquiring interests in entities that may involve critical technology (distributed ledger systems, smart contracts governing financial infrastructure) or sensitive personal data (credit platform user data) creates potential CFIUS jurisdiction. This is distinct from the HSR analysis but arises from the same transaction facts and would need to be addressed in parallel. Mandatory declarations under the CFIUS pilot program for critical technology may apply.

**Whether the feeder-master structure itself constitutes a reportable "formation" under HSR.** The creation of the master fund vehicle and the admission of co-investors may itself constitute a reportable event if it results in any co-investor crossing a threshold for the first time, separate from any downstream acquisition by the master fund.

**State antitrust notification requirements.** Some states have their own merger notification statutes that operate independently of federal HSR requirements. If the credit platforms operate in specific states with such requirements, separate state filings may be necessary.

**Interlocking directorates under Section 8 of the Clayton Act.** If the governance rights — whether pre- or post-conversion — result in common directors or officers across competing credit platforms, Section 8 prohibitions on interlocking directorates may apply. The client's question did not address this, but the structure (controlling interests in multiple platforms, governance rights for co-investors) creates the factual predicate.

**Investment Company Act status of the master fund and feeders.** If the master fund or any feeder is or becomes an "investment company" under the 1940 Act, regulatory requirements affecting the characterization of its interests, its governance, and its ability to engage in certain transactions may bear on the HSR analysis. The interaction between 1940 Act status and HSR reporting has been addressed in limited FTC guidance.

**Tax classification of tokenized instruments affecting entity characterization.** Whether the tokenized interests are treated as equity, debt, or hybrid instruments for tax purposes may affect how the issuing entities are classified (corporation, partnership, disregarded entity), which in turn affects whether the HSR analysis proceeds under the voting securities framework or the non-corporate interests framework.

**Gun-jumping risk.** If any of the governance rights — particularly smart-contract-encoded ones — become operative before HSR clearance is obtained (assuming a filing is required), the parties may face gun-jumping liability under 15 U.S.C. § 18a(g). Smart contracts that automatically execute governance functions upon token issuance, without a mechanism to delay until HSR clearance, create a novel gun-jumping risk.

**Information exchange among co-investors during due diligence.** The admission of multiple institutional co-investors, including a PE sponsor with potential portfolio overlap, creates risks of competitively sensitive information exchange during due diligence. This is a separate antitrust concern from the HSR filing question but arises from the same transaction structure.

**Whether conversion of preferred interests constitutes a new "acquisition" requiring a separate HSR filing.** Even if the initial acquisition is filed and cleared, the conversion event — which would change the nature, quantum, and governance attributes of the holdings — may itself constitute a new acquisition requiring a separate filing. This creates ongoing compliance obligations that the client would need to build into the instrument terms and smart contract logic.

**FTC and DOJ enforcement posture toward digital asset and fintech acquisitions.** Beyond formal rules, the current political and enforcement environment regarding digital assets may affect how aggressively the agencies scrutinize this transaction. Recent statements by FTC and DOJ leadership regarding technology sector consolidation, platform competition, and fintech oversight provide context, though they are not binding authority.

### What this situation is NOT

**This is not a securities registration analysis.** While the instruments involve securities concepts (convertible preferred interests, governance rights), the question is specifically about HSR antitrust reporting, not SEC registration or exemption under the Securities Act or Exchange Act. The securities characterization matters for HSR only insofar as it determines whether an instrument is a "voting security" or something else under the HSR rules.

**This is not a Bank Secrecy Act / AML analysis.** Although tokenized credit platforms may raise BSA/AML questions, those are outside the scope of the HSR analysis requested.

**This is not a substantive merger challenge analysis.** The question asks about HSR reporting requirements — the procedural filing obligation — not whether the acquisition would survive substantive antitrust review under Section 7 of the Clayton Act. The substantive question (whether the transaction may substantially lessen competition) is distinct, though the roll-up / serial acquisition concern noted above sits at the boundary.

**This is not a tax structuring analysis.** Although entity classification for tax purposes may affect the HSR analysis, the tax consequences of the feeder-master structure and convertible instruments are outside scope.

**This is not a CFIUS filing analysis.** CFIUS considerations are noted as a related concern the adviser would need to address, but the question asks for HSR analysis specifically. A full CFIUS treatment would require separate analysis of the transaction's national security implications.

**This is not an exchange/broker registration analysis.** That was the subject of the prior conversation turn and is not revisited here, although the same transaction structure implicates both sets of obligations.

**This is not a state blue sky or money transmitter licensing analysis.** Tokenized instruments may raise state-level securities and licensing questions, but those are outside the HSR scope.

**This is not a CFTC commodity or derivatives analysis.** If the tokenized credit platform interests could be characterized as swaps or futures, that would implicate CFTC jurisdiction, but that is a separate regulatory question from HSR reporting.
